# DSE Citizenship and Social Development (CSD) Complete Guide 2026

Citizenship and Social Development (CSD, sometimes abbreviated CS) is the core subject that replaced Liberal Studies in the senior secondary curriculum. It was phased in from Secondary 4 in September 2021, and the first public examination was sat in 2024. By 2026 it is a fully established, compulsory subject that **every** HKDSE candidate must take alongside Chinese Language, English Language and Mathematics.

For international and Mainland-background students considering the DSE route, CSD is one of the most misunderstood subjects: it is compulsory, it is graded on a simple pass/fail basis, and it is rarely a barrier to university — but only if you understand how it works. This guide explains the syllabus, the assessment design, the Mainland study tour, the 2026 exam, and how to comfortably secure an "Attained" result.

---

## 1. Why CSD Exists and How It Differs from Liberal Studies

Liberal Studies was famous (and controversial) for its open-ended Independent Enquiry Study (IES) and its 1–5\*\* grading. CSD was designed to be **leaner and more structured**:

- **No IES.** The single biggest workload reduction. There is no school-assessed independent research project counting toward the public exam.
- **Pass/fail reporting.** Results are reported only as **"Attained"** or **"Unattained"** — there is no level 1 to 5\*\* and no asterisks. You cannot earn "points" from CSD for JUPAS or most admissions formulas.
- **Reduced lesson time.** Roughly **135–150 hours** of curriculum time over Secondary 4–6, compared with the much heavier Liberal Studies allocation.
- **Defined content.** Where Liberal Studies prized open exploration, CSD has a **fixed, examinable body of knowledge** built around three themes. Students are expected to bring accurate factual knowledge into the exam, not just reasoning skills.

The practical consequence: CSD will not lift your university competitiveness the way a strong elective can, but a careless approach can cost you a conditional offer if you end up "Unattained". Treat it as a subject you must **clear cleanly and efficiently**, not maximise.

## 2. The Three Themes

The CSD syllabus is organised around three themes, progressing from the local, to the national, to the global:

### Theme 1 — Hong Kong under "One Country, Two Systems"
Covers the constitutional basis of the HKSAR (the Constitution of the People's Republic of China and the Basic Law), the relationship between the central authorities and the HKSAR, the rule of law and Hong Kong residents' rights and responsibilities, and Hong Kong's participation in national development including the Guangdong–Hong Kong–Macao Greater Bay Area. Themes such as the National Anthem Ordinance and Hong Kong's role in the country have featured prominently in past papers.

### Theme 2 — Our Country since Reform and Opening-up
Covers the nation's overall development since the reform and opening-up policy, the participation of citizens, national achievements and challenges, and concepts of national identity and citizenship. Expect questions on economic development, the Greater Bay Area, and the country's place in the world.

### Theme 3 — The Interconnectedness and Interdependence of the Contemporary World
Covers globalisation, the opportunities and risks of global interconnectedness, public health, sustainable development, and how Hong Kong and the country interact with global trends. This theme is the most "current affairs" oriented.

Across all three themes, the unifying skills are: reading and interpreting data, distinguishing fact from opinion, building evidence-based arguments, and considering issues from multiple perspectives.

## 3. The Public Examination (2026)

CSD has a **single written examination paper** taken by all candidates — there is no school-based assessment counting toward the public result.

- **One paper**, lasting around **two hours**.
- **Data-response questions** built around sources: written extracts, statistics, tables, charts, cartoons and photographs.
- Questions test comprehension of the data, application of subject knowledge, and the ability to construct a structured, evidence-based response.
- Reported as **Attained / Unattained** only.

**The 2026 emphasis on external knowledge.** A noted trend in recent sittings — and reinforced for 2026 — is that questions increasingly require candidates to bring in **knowledge beyond the data provided**. It is no longer enough to paraphrase the source booklet. You need accurate background facts about the Basic Law, the Greater Bay Area, national development milestones, and current global issues, and you must deploy them precisely. Pure "common sense" answers, which sometimes scraped through in the very first sittings, are now weaker.

**How forgiving is it?** Very. Day-school candidate "Attained" rates have been high: approximately **94.1% in 2024** and **93.2% in 2025**. CSD is designed so that any student who engages with the curriculum and writes organised answers will pass. The risk is concentrated among students who ignore the subject entirely or leave answers blank.

## 4. The Compulsory Mainland Study Tour

A distinctive, mandatory element of CSD is the **Mainland study tour**. Every student is expected to take part in a school-organised study tour to the Mainland, giving first-hand exposure to the country's development.

Crucially, this is **not** an IES replacement:

- It does **not** count toward the public examination.
- There is **no** prescribed report structure, word limit, or standardised assessment criteria set by the HKEAA.
- Schools design the project learning around the tour and assess students' performance **internally**, by whatever means they consider appropriate (a reflection, presentation, group project, etc.).

For students, the practical takeaway is that the study tour is a graduation/curriculum requirement managed by your school, not a high-stakes assessment. Take it seriously as a learning experience — themes from the tour (Greater Bay Area, national development) overlap directly with exam content — but it is not something you can "fail" in the way a graded coursework piece could sink you.

> **For Mainland and international candidates:** If you are sitting the DSE as a private (self-study) candidate rather than through a Hong Kong day school, you will not have a school-organised tour. Private candidates are assessed solely on the written examination. Confirm your exact obligations with the HKEAA and, if applicable, your tutorial centre.

## 5. Common Mistakes That Lead to "Unattained"

Because the pass rate is so high, an "Unattained" result almost always comes from avoidable errors:

1. **Treating it as a write-off.** Some students assume that because CSD gives no points, they can skip it. Leaving large parts of the paper blank is the single most common cause of failure.
2. **Only summarising the sources.** The 2026 paper rewards external knowledge. Restating the data booklet without adding subject content scores poorly.
3. **No structure.** Examiners reward organised, point–evidence–explanation answers. Unstructured paragraphs that mix opinion and fact lose marks.
4. **Ignoring "comment" and "to what extent" commands.** These require a position **and** a counter-perspective. One-sided answers underperform.
5. **Weak factual base on the Basic Law and national development.** These recur every year. Not knowing the constitutional framework is a predictable, fixable gap.

## 6. A Practical Study Plan

CSD does not need the time you would give an elective — but it does need *some* deliberate time. A focused approach:

**Secondary 5 (foundation):**
- Build a one-page fact sheet per theme: key concepts, the Basic Law essentials, Greater Bay Area facts, major national development milestones, and 4–5 current global issues with concrete examples.
- Follow the news lightly but consistently (10 minutes a day) — Hong Kong affairs, national development, and one or two recurring global issues (public health, sustainable development, technology).

**Secondary 6 (exam technique):**
- Drill **past papers and the official sample paper** under timed conditions. Pattern-recognition matters: the command words and data-handling tasks repeat.
- Practise the answer formula: **identify what the data shows → apply relevant subject knowledge → give a structured, two-sided judgement** for evaluative questions.
- Memorise a compact bank of named examples (a specific Greater Bay Area initiative, a concrete global health or sustainability case) you can deploy regardless of the exact question.

**Final month:**
- Revise the three theme fact sheets, not the textbook cover to cover.
- Do two or three full timed papers. The goal is fluency and time management, not new content.

Realistically, 2–3 focused hours a week in S6, plus exam-period drilling, is sufficient for the vast majority of students to attain comfortably.

## 7. How CSD Affects University Admission

Because CSD is reported only as Attained/Unattained, it does **not** contribute points to JUPAS calculations or to most overseas admissions formulas. However:

- **It is a graduation and minimum-requirement subject.** The standard "3-3-2-2" type minimum entrance requirement for Hong Kong undergraduate programmes requires CSD to be **Attained**. An "Unattained" can therefore void an otherwise strong profile.
- **Conditional offers** from Hong Kong and overseas universities frequently list "CSD: Attained" among the conditions. Treat it as a box that must be ticked, not an afterthought.
- **No upside ceiling.** There is no benefit to "over-performing" in CSD. Direct surplus effort into your point-bearing electives instead.

In short: CSD is **low effort, low reward, but non-negotiable**. The optimal strategy is efficiency — secure "Attained" with a modest, structured investment, and spend your remaining energy where points are actually earned.

---

## Key Takeaways

- CSD is a **compulsory core subject** for all 2026 DSE candidates, reported as **Attained / Unattained** only.
- The syllabus has **three themes**: Hong Kong under "One Country, Two Systems", Our Country since Reform and Opening-up, and the Interconnected Contemporary World.
- There is **one written paper** (~2 hours, data-response). The **2026 emphasis is on external knowledge** beyond the sources.
- The **Mainland study tour** is compulsory but **internally assessed** and not part of the public exam — there is no IES.
- Pass rates are high (~93–94%), so "Unattained" is almost always avoidable; the priority is to **clear it cleanly and efficiently**, not to maximise it.
