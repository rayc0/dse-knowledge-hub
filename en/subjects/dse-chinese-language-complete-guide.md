# HKDSE Chinese Language 2026 — Complete Guide

Chinese Language is one of the four core subjects every HKDSE candidate must sit, alongside English Language, Mathematics (Compulsory Part), and Citizenship and Social Development. For decades it carried the nickname **"the death paper" (死亡之卷)** because of its sprawling four-paper format and notoriously unpredictable reading comprehension. The good news for 2026 candidates: the exam has been significantly streamlined. This guide explains the current structure, what is actually tested, and how to build a Level 4–5\*\* strategy — written so that local, mainland, and international students (and their parents) can all follow it.

## Why Chinese Language matters so much

Chinese Language is not just another subject — it is a **gatekeeper**. JUPAS programmes almost universally require **Level 3 or above** in Chinese Language as part of the "3322" minimum (Level 3 in Chinese and English, Level 2 in Mathematics and Citizenship and Social Development). Many competitive programmes — medicine, law, global business, government-related disciplines — count Chinese in the "Best 5" and some weight it heavily. A weak Chinese grade can quietly sink an otherwise strong application.

For non-Chinese-speaking students there is an alternative route (the GCE/GCSE Chinese or Applied Learning Chinese pathways accepted by universities for non-Chinese-speaking applicants), but the overwhelming majority of candidates sit the mainstream HKDSE Chinese Language examination described here.

## The 2026 structure: two papers plus SBA

The most important thing to understand is that the format was **reformed from the 2024 examination onward**. The old Paper 3 (Listening and Integrated Skills) and Paper 4 (Speaking) were **abolished**. From 2024 — and continuing for 2026 — the subject has just two written public papers plus a school-based component:

| Component | Weighting | Duration | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Paper 1 — Reading** | ~40% | 1 hr 30 min | Comprehension of classical and vernacular Chinese, including the 12 set texts |
| **Paper 2 — Writing** | ~45% | 2 hr 15 min | One extended composition chosen from three prompts |
| **School-based Assessment (SBA)** | ~15% | Coursework | Reading/viewing tasks assessed by the school |

This is a major reduction in workload and exam-day stress. The removal of the speaking paper in particular eliminated the unpredictable group-discussion exercise that many students dreaded. Candidates should still confirm the exact weightings and any updates on the official HKEAA assessment framework page each year, as percentages and SBA arrangements have been adjusted over time.

## Paper 1 — Reading in detail

Paper 1 is split into two parts:

**Part A — Set texts (指定文言經典學習材料).** Since the set-text reform, candidates study a fixed list of **12 classical Chinese texts**. These are canonical works of literary Chinese (文言文) drawn from classical prose and poetry — pieces such as Confucius' *Analects* selections, Mencius, *The Peach Blossom Spring* (《桃花源記》), *On the Yueyang Tower* (《岳陽樓記》), Zhuangzi, Su Shi's *Former Ode on the Red Cliff* (《前赤壁賦》), and others. Part A questions test precise understanding of these texts: word meanings, sentence translation into modern Chinese, the author's argument, rhetorical devices, and the moral or philosophical point. Because the 12 texts are **known in advance**, Part A is the single most "scorable" part of the entire subject — there is no excuse for losing marks here. Memorisation of key sentences and confident paraphrasing into modern Chinese (語譯) is essential.

**Part B — Unseen passages.** This is the part that earned the "death paper" reputation. Candidates face one or more passages they have never seen — typically a **vernacular (白話文)** essay and an **unseen classical (文言文)** passage. Questions range from factual retrieval and inference to analysis of structure, tone, and the writer's intention, often with mark-heavy "explain in your own words" and "compare the two passages" items. The texts can be philosophical, literary, or argumentative, and the questions reward careful, evidence-based reading rather than rote answers.

**How to prepare for Paper 1:**

- **Lock down Part A first.** Drill the 12 set texts until you can translate any sentence and state every text's central argument. This is guaranteed, high-yield content.
- **Practise classical Chinese fundamentals**: function words (虛詞 such as 之、其、以、而), common substitutions, inversion, and ellipsis. Unseen classical passages reuse the same grammar patterns as the set texts.
- **Do timed past papers** from the post-2024 format. Read the questions *before* the long passage so you know what to hunt for.
- **Answer with textual evidence.** High-level answers quote or paraphrase the passage and then explain — never assert without support.

## Paper 2 — Writing in detail

Paper 2 gives **three prompts**; you choose **one** and write a single extended composition in 2 hours 15 minutes. The three options usually span different modes — typically a **narrative/descriptive** prompt, an **argumentative/discursive** prompt, and an **open or reflective** prompt (sometimes a quotation or scenario to interpret). There is generally no rigid word count, but strong scripts run roughly 700–900+ characters of focused, well-developed writing.

Examiners assess writing on three pillars:

1. **Content (內容)** — relevance to the prompt, depth of ideas, originality, and persuasive or emotional substance.
2. **Expression / language (表達)** — fluency, precision, vocabulary range, and grammatical control.
3. **Structure (結構)** — coherent organisation, logical paragraphing, and effective opening and closing.

**How to prepare for Paper 2:**

- **Build a "material bank."** Top scorers carry a stock of versatile examples — historical figures, current affairs, literature, personal anecdotes — that can be adapted to many prompts. Vague essays score Level 3; specific, vivid ones reach Level 5.
- **Master one argumentative framework.** Claim → reasoning → concrete example → counter-argument rebuttal → restatement. This structure travels across almost any discursive prompt.
- **Practise narrative craft.** For story prompts, show rather than tell: sensory detail, a turning point, and a controlled emotional arc beat a flat chronological recount.
- **Plan before you write.** Spend 8–12 minutes outlining. Drifting off-topic is the most common cause of capped marks.
- **Write legibly and finish.** Unfinished or rushed conclusions disproportionately hurt the structure score.

## School-based Assessment (SBA)

The SBA component (~15%) is coursework administered by your school across the senior secondary years, typically based on reading or viewing assigned books/films and producing oral or written responses assessed internally. Private and overseas candidates without a school may have alternative arrangements — always check current HKEAA rules, because SBA policy and its exact weighting have been subject to review. Treat SBA as "free marks": it is the most controllable component because you know the tasks and criteria in advance.

## Level descriptors and what grades mean

HKDSE Chinese Language is reported on the standard scale: **1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 5\*, 5\*\***. Practical benchmarks:

- **Level 2** — minimum for Citizenship/Maths-style requirements but **insufficient** for most degree programmes.
- **Level 3** — the critical threshold. Meets the "3322" Chinese requirement that unlocks the vast majority of JUPAS degrees.
- **Level 4** — competitive; safe for most mainstream programmes and a solid Best-5 contributor.
- **Level 5 / 5\* / 5\*\*** — distinction band. Genuinely difficult in Chinese because of the unseen reading and subjective writing marking; a 5\*\* here is a strong signal for top-tier law, education, journalism, and Chinese-related programmes.

Statistically, a far smaller proportion of candidates reach 5\*\* in Chinese than in many electives, which is exactly why securing a reliable Level 4 is a smarter target for most students than gambling on a 5\*\*.

## A realistic 12-month study plan

**Months 1–4 (foundation):** Finish and consolidate all 12 set texts. Build the classical-Chinese grammar toolkit. Start a weekly writing habit — one full or half composition, reviewed against the marking criteria.

**Months 5–8 (skill-building):** Move to timed unseen reading passages, both vernacular and classical. Develop your material bank for writing. Begin full past-paper Paper 1 sections under time pressure.

**Months 9–11 (exam simulation):** Full mock papers under exam conditions. Analyse every lost mark — categorise errors (misread question, weak evidence, off-topic, language slips) and target the largest category.

**Final month:** Re-memorise set-text key sentences and translations, polish two or three strong writing structures, and rehearse time management (a strict clock for both papers).

## Advice specifically for non-native and international students

If Chinese is not your first language, the subject is challenging but **not impossible** — and the post-2024 removal of the speaking and listening papers helps enormously, since written work can be prepared methodically.

- **Confirm your route early.** Non-Chinese-speaking students should check with target universities whether they accept GCE/IGCSE Chinese or Applied Learning Chinese (Chinese Language) in lieu of HKDSE Chinese. This can completely change your strategy — do this *before* committing two years to the mainstream paper.
- **If you take the mainstream paper, front-load the set texts.** They are finite, predictable, and worth a guaranteed chunk of Paper 1.
- **Read widely in modern Chinese** — quality newspaper commentary and essays build the vocabulary and register that Paper 2 rewards.
- **Get marked writing feedback regularly.** Self-study cannot reveal whether your expression reads as natural to an examiner; a tutor or teacher correction loop is the highest-leverage investment.

## Key takeaways

- Chinese Language is now a **two-paper** subject (Reading ~40%, Writing ~45%) plus SBA (~15%) — far lighter than the pre-2024 format.
- The **12 classical set texts** are the most scorable content in the whole exam — master them completely.
- Paper 2 rewards **specific, well-structured** writing over vague generalities; build a material bank and a reliable argumentative framework.
- **Level 3 is the make-or-break threshold** for JUPAS; a steady Level 4 is a wiser target than gambling on 5\*\*.
- Non-native speakers should **verify alternative-qualification acceptance** with universities before defaulting to the mainstream paper.

Always cross-check the latest weightings, set-text list, and SBA arrangements against the official HKEAA assessment framework, as the authority periodically revises the subject. With a disciplined plan, "the death paper" is very much survivable — and a strong Chinese grade remains one of the most valuable assets in a Hong Kong university application.
