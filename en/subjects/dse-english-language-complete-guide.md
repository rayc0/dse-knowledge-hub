# DSE English Language Complete Guide 2026

For almost every HKDSE candidate, English Language is the single most consequential subject on the timetable. It is a core subject sat by virtually all of the roughly 50,000 candidates each year, and — more importantly — it is the subject that most often decides whether a student clears the university door. The "3322" minimum entrance requirement used by the eight UGC-funded universities asks for **Level 3 in English Language**, and a large share of JUPAS rejections every year come down to a candidate missing English by a single level. This guide explains exactly how the 2026 paper is built, how the B1/B2 levelling system works, what School-Based Assessment contributes, and how to plan preparation around the parts of the exam that actually move your level.

## Why English Language matters more than any other DSE subject

The HKDSE university entrance benchmark is usually written as **3322 + 2**: Level 3 in Chinese Language, Level 3 in English Language, Level 2 in Mathematics, Level 2 in Citizenship and Social Development, plus Level 2 or above in two elective subjects. Notice that English carries a Level 3 requirement — higher than Maths or CSD.

Three points make English uniquely high-stakes:

1. **It is a hard gate, not a points contribution.** Even if your elective scores are outstanding, falling to Level 2 in English typically makes you ineligible for the standard JUPAS offer at the eight universities, regardless of your total.
2. **Many programmes demand more than Level 3.** Competitive programmes — Medicine, Law, Global Business, Journalism, English-related majors — frequently ask for Level 4 or 5 in English, and some weight it heavily in their JUPAS scoring formula.
3. **It travels overseas too.** UK universities applying through UCAS, and some Australian and Canadian institutions, will accept a strong DSE English level (commonly Level 4–5) in place of IELTS. A good English level can save an international applicant an entire separate test.

In short: improving English by one level is often the highest-return move a DSE candidate can make.

## The four-paper structure in 2026

The HKEAA English Language examination is made up of four papers plus a School-Based Assessment (SBA) component. The public examination is worth **85%** of the subject and the SBA contributes the remaining **15%**.

### Paper 1 — Reading (about 20% of the subject)

Paper 1 has two parts:

- **Part A** is compulsory and is the easier, foundational reading section.
- You then choose **Part B1** (easier) **or Part B2** (more difficult).

Tasks include comprehension of authentic texts, inference, summary, vocabulary in context, and interpreting visual/graphic information. The texts in B2 are longer, denser and more abstract; B1 texts are shorter and more concrete.

### Paper 2 — Writing (about 25% of the subject)

Paper 2 also has two parts:

- **Part A** is a compulsory short guided writing task (around 200 words).
- You then choose **Part B1 or Part B2** for a longer composition (around 400 words) on a topic linked to one of the elective modules studied in school (for example Short Stories, Poems and Songs, Sports Communication, Workplace Communication, Social Issues, Debating, or Popular Culture).

Writing is double-marked on domains such as Content, Language, and Organisation.

### Paper 3 — Listening and Integrated Skills (about 30% of the subject)

This is the largest single paper and the one many candidates underestimate.

- **Part A** is compulsory and consists of listening tasks based on recorded materials.
- **Parts B1 and B2** are integrated listening/reading-into-writing tasks built around a common data file: you listen, read accompanying documents, and then produce written output (emails, reports, proposals) using information from both sources.

Because Paper 3 rewards information handling and accuracy under time pressure rather than raw vocabulary, it is frequently where disciplined candidates gain the most marks relative to effort.

### Paper 4 — Speaking (about 10% of the subject)

Paper 4 is assessed in two components: a **group interaction (group discussion)** and an **individual response**. Candidates are graded on pronunciation and delivery, communication strategies, vocabulary and language patterns, and ideas/organisation.

### School-Based Assessment (SBA) — 15%

SBA is conducted internally by your school across S5 and S6 and is moderated by the HKEAA. It is normally based on an **extensive reading/viewing programme**: students respond orally (and sometimes in writing) to print fiction/non-fiction and films they have engaged with. Two marks are typically reported. For **private/self-study candidates** who have no school to administer SBA, the public-exam papers are reweighted to cover the full subject, so SBA is not a barrier to retaking as a self-study candidate.

## The B1/B2 levelling system — the single most important strategic decision

This is the part of DSE English that confuses candidates most, and where good advice changes outcomes.

In Papers 1, 2 and 3, after the compulsory Part A you must choose **Part B1 or Part B2**:

- **Choosing B2 keeps the full level range open.** Candidates who attempt Part A + B2 can be awarded the full range of levels, up to **Level 5\*\***.
- **Choosing B1 caps your maximum.** Candidates who attempt Part A + B1 can be awarded at most **Level 4** in that paper.

The practical implications:

- If you need **Level 3 for the 3322 university gate**, B1 is *enough* — and for a weaker candidate, attempting B1 well can be safer than attempting B2 badly, because B1 questions are more accessible and the route to a solid Level 3 is more reliable.
- If you are targeting **Level 5, 5\* or 5\*\*** for a competitive JUPAS programme or to replace IELTS for overseas applications, you **must** choose B2 in the relevant papers. There is no path to Level 5 through B1.
- The choice can be made **paper by paper**. A common, sensible profile is B2 on the paper that is your strength and B1 on a paper that is your weakness — but be careful: capping a paper at Level 4 pulls down your overall subject level if your other papers cannot compensate.

Decide your B1/B2 strategy honestly, based on mock results, **before** exam day — not in the exam hall under stress.

## How the subject level is calculated

Each paper is marked and contributes its weighted share, the SBA contributes 15%, and the HKEAA combines these into a single subject level reported on the 7-point scale: **U, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 5\*, 5\*\***. Levelling is criterion-referenced and statistically moderated across years, so raw marks are not directly comparable between sessions — what matters is performance against the standard, not a fixed pass mark.

For university planning, treat the levels as follows:

- **Level 2** — below the standard university English gate; viable only for some sub-degree, self-financing, or overseas foundation routes.
- **Level 3** — clears the 3322 gate for the eight UGC universities; the realistic minimum target for any degree-aspiring candidate.
- **Level 4** — opens most competitive programmes and is widely accepted by UK universities in lieu of IELTS.
- **Level 5 / 5\* / 5\*\*** — required or strongly advantaged for Medicine, Law, Global Business, journalism and English/translation majors; a meaningful JUPAS scoring boost in many formulas.

## A realistic preparation strategy

English Language does not reward last-minute cramming the way a content subject can. Skills compound slowly, so plan across the whole of S5–S6.

**1. Read and listen in English every day, deliberately.** Twenty to thirty minutes of authentic material — news features, opinion pieces, podcasts, documentaries — does more for Papers 1 and 3 than another grammar worksheet. Keep a vocabulary log of *useful* words you can actually deploy in writing, not obscure ones.

**2. Treat Paper 3 as a technique paper, not a luck paper.** Practise the integrated task workflow: listen for the data structure, annotate the reading documents, then map both into the required text type. Most lost marks here are information-handling errors, not English errors — and those are trainable.

**3. Drill writing under timed conditions and get it marked.** Self-assessment in writing is weak; you need a teacher or competent reader to mark against Content/Language/Organisation. Build three or four reliable, flexible structures for the Part B text types in your chosen elective module rather than memorising whole essays.

**4. Rehearse speaking out loud, in groups.** Paper 4 rewards interaction strategies — bringing others in, building on points, disagreeing politely, signposting. These are habits, not knowledge, and only develop through repeated spoken practice.

**5. Take the SBA seriously.** It is a "free" 15% if you engage genuinely with the reading/viewing programme. Choosing texts and films you actually find interesting makes the oral responses far stronger.

**6. Lock your B1/B2 decision from mock data.** Use S6 mock results to decide, per paper, whether you are realistically chasing Level 5 (B2) or securing a dependable Level 3–4 (B1 where appropriate). Indecision in the exam hall is the worst outcome.

## Common mistakes that cost a level

- **Defaulting to B2 because it "looks better"** while not being able to access its harder marks — a weak B2 attempt can score below a solid B1.
- **Neglecting Paper 3** because it is unglamorous, when it is the heaviest single paper.
- **Memorising model essays**, which markers detect quickly and which collapse when the prompt differs.
- **Ignoring SBA**, then losing a full 15% band that required only consistent effort.
- **Assuming Level 3 is "easy"** — it is the most common borderline outcome and the most common reason for a missed university offer.

## Bottom line

DSE English Language in 2026 keeps its established four-paper-plus-SBA design: Reading, Writing, Listening and Integrated Skills, and Speaking, with the B1/B2 levelling system controlling your ceiling. For a candidate aiming at university in Hong Kong, Level 3 is the non-negotiable minimum and Level 4–5 is what opens competitive programmes and replaces IELTS abroad. The students who do best are not those who cram hardest at the end, but those who read and listen in English every day, train Paper 3 as a technique, get their writing marked honestly, and make a clear-eyed B1/B2 decision well before they walk into the exam hall.
