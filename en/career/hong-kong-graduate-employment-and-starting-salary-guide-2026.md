# Hong Kong University Graduate Employment & Starting Salary Guide 2026

For most students and parents weighing up a Hong Kong degree, one question sits underneath all the others: **what do graduates actually earn, and how easily do they find work?** Rankings and campus tours are easy to find; honest salary data is harder. This guide pulls together the most recent Graduate Employment Survey (GES) figures from the University Grants Committee (UGC) and the individual universities, explains what the numbers really mean, and shows how starting pay varies by university, discipline, and the choices you make along the way.

## The headline numbers

Two figures get quoted constantly, and they measure very different things, so it is worth separating them clearly.

**The average starting salary for a fresh bachelor's-degree graduate was around HK$19,500 per month in 2025.** This figure had risen for five consecutive years and then plateaued — the first time in half a decade that entry-level pay did not grow. For a new graduate budgeting their first year in Hong Kong, this is the realistic anchor point: a typical first job pays somewhere in the high-teens to low-twenties of thousands of Hong Kong dollars per month, before tax (Hong Kong's salaries tax is low — see our cost-of-living guide for how that affects take-home pay).

**The average annual salary across all UGC graduates was about HK$329,000 — roughly HK$27,400 per month.** This is the figure that grew 4.8% year on year. The reason it is so much higher than the HK$19,500 starting figure is that it blends fresh graduates with people one to two years into their careers, and it includes master's and doctoral graduates, who earn substantially more than first-degree holders. When a headline says "Hong Kong graduate pay hits record high," it is almost always this blended number, not the entry-level one.

Keep both in mind. Use **~HK$19,500/month** when you are imagining your own first payslip, and **~HK$329,000/year** when you are comparing the long-run value of a Hong Kong degree against other destinations.

## Employment rates: the good news

Hong Kong's eight UGC-funded universities consistently report very high employment outcomes. In recent GES rounds, the combined unemployment rate for fresh graduates sat in the low single digits, and the **underemployment rate** — graduates working fewer than 35 hours per week, or stuck in part-time and temporary roles — fell to around **4.2%** across the eight institutions. Among the research universities, HKU, HKUST and PolyU reported underemployment in the 4–6% range.

In plain terms: if you graduate from a UGC-funded programme, the overwhelming majority of your cohort are in full-time employment or further study within months of finishing. The competitive pressure in Hong Kong is much less about *whether* you get a job and much more about *which* job — the gap between a HK$15,000 administrative role and a HK$30,000 graduate-scheme role in finance or tech is where the real stakes lie.

## Salary by university

The GES data shows a clear ordering by institution, driven largely by programme mix (universities with big medicine, finance and engineering faculties sit higher):

- **The University of Hong Kong (HKU):** the first institution to cross the HK$400,000 average annual mark, at roughly **HK$401,000**, equivalent to an average monthly gross salary of about **HK$33,500** for its bachelor's graduates — a year-on-year rise of around 4.7%.
- **The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK):** approximately **HK$381,000** per year on average.
- **The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST):** approximately **HK$330,000** per year, with year-on-year growth around 3.1%.
- **The Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU):** strong professional-programme outcomes, with growth of roughly 4.4%.
- **Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU)** and the remaining UGC institutions cluster below the research-university leaders, with HKBU growth around 3.9%.

A caution on reading these tables: the differences between universities are real but are heavily confounded by *what* each university teaches. HKU's average is lifted by its large medicine, dentistry and law cohorts. A computer-science graduate from HKUST and one from HKU will, in practice, see broadly similar offers. **Discipline matters more than the university crest.**

## Salary by discipline

This is where the spread becomes meaningful. Within the bachelor's starting-salary data, the higher-paying broad fields in 2025 included:

- **Engineering:** around **HK$21,500/month** starting — consistently one of the strongest first-degree fields.
- **Information technology / computing:** around **HK$20,500/month**.
- **Research and development roles:** around **HK$20,000/month**.
- **Finance and accounting:** around **HK$20,000/month** at entry, but with the steepest upward curve — investment banking, quantitative and actuarial roles diverge sharply upward within two to three years.

Sitting below these are many humanities, social-science, design and general-business roles, which tend to start closer to or below the HK$19,500 average. The fields that pay most at *graduation* are not always the ones that pay most at *career peak* — medicine and law start with structured, capped trainee pay but reach very high ceilings, while computing and finance can move fast early. Prospective students should look at both the starting figure and the trajectory.

## Why entry-level pay stalled in 2025

After five years of growth, the plateau in starting salaries is worth understanding rather than ignoring. Several forces converged:

1. **A larger graduate supply.** Expanded local cohorts plus a rising number of non-local graduates staying on under the IANG arrangement increased competition for entry-level seats.
2. **Cautious hiring in finance and professional services.** Several large employers slowed graduate intake and held starting bands flat rather than cutting them.
3. **Inflation-adjusted reality.** A flat HK$19,500 in nominal terms is a small real-terms decline. The blended HK$329,000 figure still rose because mid-career and postgraduate pay kept climbing, masking the entry-level squeeze.

The practical takeaway is not "Hong Kong degrees stopped being worth it" — employment rates remain excellent — but rather that **differentiation matters more than ever**. The gap between a generic graduate and a competitive one widened in 2025, and that is largely within the student's control.

## Postgraduate and master's outcomes

Master's and doctoral graduates pull the blended averages up significantly. Taught master's graduates in finance, data science, and other applied fields routinely start well above the first-degree average, and research postgraduates command a further premium in technical sectors. For mainland and international students in particular, a one-year taught master's in Hong Kong is often used explicitly as a salary and visa lever — it resets you into a higher starting band and extends your IANG eligibility. ## How to maximise your graduate earnings

The data points to a consistent set of moves that separate the top of a cohort from the middle:

- **Choose the discipline deliberately.** The single biggest lever on lifetime earnings is field of study, decided years before graduation. If income is a priority, weigh engineering, computing, finance, and the regulated professions seriously.
- **Do real internships, early.** Graduate-scheme conversion rates in finance, Big Four accounting, and tech are dominated by prior interns. Two summers of relevant internships is the strongest predictor of a top-band first offer.
- **Target structured graduate schemes.** Banking, consulting, government administrative officer roles, and major-corporate management trainee schemes start meaningfully above the HK$19,500 average and have defined progression. Their deadlines fall up to a year before graduation — missing them is the most common avoidable mistake.
- **Use the postgraduate lever consciously.** A targeted taught master's can move you into a higher starting band and is often a better investment than an extra year of generic work experience, especially for non-local graduates managing visa timelines.
- **Stay for the IANG window.** Non-local graduates can remain and work under the Immigration Arrangements for Non-local Graduates. The first two post-graduation years are when the salary curve is steepest; leaving early forfeits that compounding. ## How to read salary statistics critically

Whenever you see a Hong Kong graduate-salary headline, ask four questions before you trust it:

1. **Bachelor's only, or blended with postgraduates?** The gap is roughly HK$19,500 vs HK$27,400 per month — entirely an artefact of who is counted.
2. **Mean or median?** A handful of investment-banking and medical offers drag the *mean* upward. The *median* graduate earns less than the average. Most published figures are means.
3. **Which cohort year?** GES data lags reality by roughly a year. A 2026 headline often describes 2024 graduates.
4. **Self-reported response rate.** Surveys rely on graduates responding, and higher earners respond at higher rates in some datasets, nudging averages up.

None of this means the data is useless — it is the best available and the trend signal is reliable. It means you should treat the numbers as a well-lit map, not a personal guarantee.

## The bottom line

Hong Kong's universities deliver strong, reliable employment outcomes: near-full employment for graduates, an entry-level salary anchored around **HK$19,500 per month**, and a blended graduate average near **HK$329,000 per year** that continues to rise even as starting pay plateaus. The institution you attend matters less than the discipline you choose and the internships and graduate schemes you secure along the way. Use the figures in this guide to set realistic expectations — then focus your energy on the small number of choices that actually move you from the middle of the cohort to the top of it.
