# JUPAS Interview Preparation Complete Guide for Hong Kong University Admissions 2026

For most JUPAS applicants, the interview is the single moment where the admissions office stops looking at numbers and starts looking at you. A strong DSE score gets you onto the shortlist; the interview decides whether you walk away with the offer letter, get bumped to a Band B programme, or end up in the late-round reshuffle. With Hong Kong's eight UGC-funded universities running tens of thousands of interviews between February and August every year, the candidates who stand out are not necessarily the ones with the highest scores — they are the ones who showed up prepared.

This guide walks you through how the JUPAS interview system actually works in 2026: which universities interview whom, what each format looks like, what the hot programmes (medicine, law, business, engineering, education) really test, the questions you should rehearse, and a four-week preparation timeline you can start tonight.

## Why JUPAS interviews matter more than you think

Unlike UCAS in the UK, JUPAS is not a personal-statement-driven system. Your academic record dominates Stage 1 selection, but once you are shortlisted, the interview functions as a tie-breaker — and at popular programmes, it is much more than that. PolyU, for example, awards "bonus scores" for strong interview performance that can lift a candidate over the cut-off. HKU Business and Economics gives "additional bonus in the selection process" to applicants who perform well in optional interviews. CUHK Medicine reserves around 30% of the final admission weighting for the interview itself.

In short: at competitive programmes, the interview can move you up or down the order of merit by the equivalent of two or three DSE points. That is enormous when cut-offs are decided by half a level.

## When interviews happen: the JUPAS timeline

JUPAS interviews fall into two windows:

**Before DSE results (February to early July 2026)** — This is when most universities run their first round. HKU, CUHK, HKUST and PolyU all hold pre-results interviews, prioritising candidates who placed the programme as Band A. Selection is based on your S5/S6 mock results, school references and Other Experiences and Achievements (OEA) entries. Invitations typically arrive in February to April, with interviews running March to early July.

**After DSE results (mid-July to early August 2026)** — DSE results are released on **15 July 2026**. Late interviews happen in the gap between results day and Main Round offers (around 5 August). These are usually for borderline candidates, late-shortlisted programmes, or competitive courses (notably medicine, dentistry, law) that confirm impressions formed earlier. Some programmes like CUHK MBChB and HKU MBBS run interviews exclusively or primarily after results.

You may also receive interview invitations during the JUPAS Stage A (programme reordering) or Stage B (after Main Round) windows if a place opens up. Be ready to respond within 24 hours — universities will not chase you.

## The four interview formats you will encounter

### 1. Panel interview

Two to three faculty members ask you questions for 8–15 minutes. This is the default format for the majority of JUPAS programmes. CUHK Medicine, for example, uses a panel-only structure: two interviewers, one candidate, 10–15 minutes covering personal motivation, ethics, healthcare knowledge and teamwork.

Panels reward candidates who give structured, two-to-three-sentence answers, make eye contact with both interviewers, and show genuine curiosity. Rambling or one-word answers both kill you.

### 2. Group discussion

You are placed in a group of four to eight candidates and given a topic, business case or ethical dilemma. You discuss it for 15–25 minutes while interviewers observe silently. This is heavily used by HKU and CUHK Business programmes, HKUST Engineering, and many social science courses.

The trap here is that group discussions are not debates — they are collaborative exercises. Faculty are looking for candidates who listen, build on others' points, gently disagree without aggression, and bring quiet members into the conversation. Dominating airtime is a fast way to fail.

### 3. Multiple Mini Interview (MMI)

A circuit of 5–10 short stations (4–8 minutes each) where you tackle a fresh scenario at every stop. Used most prominently by HKU MBBS and increasingly by dentistry, pharmacy and nursing programmes. Stations may include ethical dilemmas, role-play with an actor, data interpretation, or personal questions.

MMIs test agility under pressure. The good news: a poor performance at one station does not sink you because each station is scored independently.

### 4. Subject-specific tests or auditions

Music, Visual Arts, Physical Education, Architecture, Design, Translation and a handful of language programmes require auditions, portfolio reviews or written tests in addition to (or instead of) a conversational interview. Education University programmes commonly include a brief teaching demo or microteaching task.

## What each university actually does

**HKU** — HKU runs both pre-results and post-results interviews. Band A applicants get priority for invitation, and most faculties (Arts, Science, Social Sciences, Engineering, Business and Economics, Education, Law, Medicine, Dentistry) interview at least their top tier of candidates. MBBS uses MMI; Dentistry uses panel + MMI hybrid; Law uses panel; Business is optional but highly recommended.

**CUHK** — CUHK publishes its annual Interview Arrangement document listing every JUPAS programme, the format (group / individual / written test), and whether it happens before or after results. MBChB uses panel only. Most CUHK Business programmes use group discussion. Global Business Studies, Quantitative Finance and Insurance Financial Studies typically run earlier and are more selective.

**HKUST** — HKUST favours group discussions for engineering and business (especially Global Business and World Business programmes) and panels for science and humanities. Many programmes interview only post-results, focusing on borderline cases.

**PolyU** — PolyU interviews ALL Band A applicants for selected competitive programmes (Design, Optometry, Physiotherapy, Occupational Therapy, Radiography, Nursing, Hotel and Tourism Management). Strong performance earns explicit bonus scores. Note this aggressive interview approach — PolyU treats interviews as core to admission, not optional.

**CityU** — Mostly post-results, panel format. Programmes in Veterinary Medicine, Creative Media, Surveying and Architecture run pre-results interviews or portfolio reviews.

**EdUHK** — Almost all education programmes require interviews, often including a short teaching demonstration or group discussion on educational issues. Subject-knowledge tests apply to BEd Mathematics, English and Chinese streams.

**HKBU and Lingnan** — More selective interview use; HKBU runs interviews for Communication, Chinese Medicine, Visual Arts, Translation and the Honours College. Lingnan emphasises liberal arts essays and short panel chats.

## Programme-specific expectations

### Medicine and dentistry

Expect questions clustered around four pillars: personal development ("Tell me about a time you failed"), medical ethics ("Should AI replace radiologists?"), the Hong Kong healthcare system ("Why is the public-private split a problem?") and course knowledge ("What do you know about the Enrichment Year at HKU?"). MMIs add role-play stations where you might break bad news to an actor playing a patient. CUHK GPS (Global Physician-Leadership Stream) interviews are notoriously deep on humanities and ethics.

### Law

LLB interviews at HKU, CUHK and CityU test reasoning, not legal knowledge. You will be asked to argue both sides of a case (e.g. "Should hate speech be criminalised?"), discuss a current legal controversy (national security law, judicial review, AI copyright), and demonstrate clarity of thought. Read SCMP's law section, the Hong Kong Free Press, and at least skim a textbook on legal reasoning.

### Business and finance

Group discussions dominate. Cases range from "How should HSBC respond to virtual banks?" to "Design a product launch for a Cantonese coffee chain." Read SCMP Business, HKEJ, the Wall Street Journal's Asia section, and follow Hang Seng major moves. Quantitative Finance interviews at CUHK and HKUST may include numerical reasoning questions.

### Engineering and computer science

Expect questions on why this specific stream, basic technical reasoning, and an applied problem ("How would you design an evacuation system for a 50-storey building?"). HKUST and HKU CS panels often probe project work listed on your OEA — make sure you can explain everything you wrote down.

### Education

Be ready for a microteaching demo (5 minutes on a primary or secondary topic of your choice) and a group discussion about a current education issue: AI in classrooms, the BCA assessment debate, mental health among students, or curriculum reform. Show you understand pedagogy, not just subject content.

### Liberal arts, social sciences and humanities

Generally panel format, exploring your reading habits, current affairs awareness and intellectual curiosity. The killer question: "What was the last book you read that changed your mind about something?"

## The 30 questions you must rehearse

Group A — Personal:
1. Why this programme?
2. Why this university over the other two?
3. Tell me about yourself in 90 seconds.
4. What is your biggest weakness?
5. Describe a failure and what you learned.
6. What do you do outside of school?
7. Who is your role model and why?
8. Where do you see yourself in 10 years?

Group B — Academic and motivation:
9. What did you not like about your DSE syllabus?
10. Which subject did you struggle with most?
11. What's a recent article or book in this field you read?
12. Why not study this overseas?
13. What if you don't get into this programme?

Group C — Current affairs and opinion:
14. What is the biggest challenge facing Hong Kong today?
15. Should AI be regulated?
16. Your view on the Greater Bay Area integration?
17. Climate change: who should pay?
18. Mental health among Hong Kong students — your take?
19. The role of universities in society.

Group D — Behavioural:
20. Tell me about a time you led a team.
21. A time you disagreed with someone — what happened?
22. How do you handle stress?
23. Describe a project you're proud of.

Group E — Programme-specific (substitute the field):
24. What is one issue facing this profession today?
25. Pick an ethical dilemma in this field and discuss.
26. Who is one influential figure in this field and why?
27. What do you know about our curriculum?

Group F — Closers:
28. What questions do you have for us?
29. Why should we choose you?
30. Anything else you'd like us to know?

## A four-week preparation plan

**Week 1 — Self-audit and content base.** Re-read your JUPAS application, OEA entries and any school recommendation. Anything you wrote, you must be able to defend. List every project, prize, society and volunteer role with dates and a 30-second story for each. Build a one-page "core narrative" answering: who you are, why this field, what you bring.

**Week 2 — Programme research.** Read the programme website end-to-end. Note compulsory courses, distinctive features (HKU MBBS Enrichment Year, CUHK Global Business Studies trips, HKUST design thinking core), faculty research strengths, and recent news mentioning the department. Memorise three specifics for each of your top three Band A choices.

**Week 3 — Mock interviews.** Run at least four full mocks — two panel, one group discussion, one MMI if applicable. Record yourself on video. Review for: filler words ("um," "like"), eye contact, posture, time per answer (aim for 60–90 seconds for opinion questions, 30–45 for behavioural). Ask a teacher, parent or older sibling to play interviewer.

**Week 4 — Current affairs and final polish.** Spend 30 minutes a day reading SCMP, RTHK English, HKFP and one international source (Reuters, BBC). Pick five issues you have a defended opinion on. Iron your interview outfit, test your video conferencing setup if remote, plan travel routes for in-person interviews, and rehearse three thoughtful questions to ask interviewers.

## Logistics on the day

Arrive 30 minutes early. Bring HKID, JUPAS application number, a printed CV / OEA copy, a notepad and pen. Dress smart-casual: collared shirt, no jeans, no sneakers; full business attire is not required except for finance and law. If the interview is online, sit against a plain wall, use a laptop (not phone), wired internet if possible, and test microphone quality 24 hours in advance. Smile when you enter; thank the panel by name when you leave.

## After the interview

Most universities will not give you feedback. Move on, focus on the next interview, and remember: late-round invitations during JUPAS Stage A or B can come at any time. Keep your phone on. Keep your suit pressed.

The JUPAS interview is not an obstacle — it is your one chance to prove that you are not a number. Treat it that way, and the offer letter follows.
