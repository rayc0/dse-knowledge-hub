# Hong Kong PhD Fellowship Scheme (HKPFS) Complete Guide 2026/27: Eligibility, Application, Stipend and Selection Strategy

For international students considering a PhD, the Hong Kong PhD Fellowship Scheme (HKPFS) is one of the most generous, fully-funded research awards in Asia. Administered by the Research Grants Council (RGC) of the University Grants Committee (UGC), the scheme grants 400 fellowships every year across the eight UGC-funded universities in Hong Kong, with an annual stipend of HK$340,800 (about US$43,690) plus HK$14,200 for conference and research travel. For high-achieving applicants from mainland China, India, the EU, ASEAN, North America and beyond, this is the gateway to a research career in Hong Kong with no nationality restriction and no requirement to find external sponsorship.

This guide explains exactly how the HKPFS 2026/27 cycle works, who is competitive, how to navigate the two-step application, what the eight universities offer on top of the base award, how the panels evaluate candidates, and what to do if you are not selected as a fellow but still want to pursue a PhD in Hong Kong.

## What the HKPFS actually is

The HKPFS was launched by the RGC in 2009 to attract the best and brightest research postgraduate students worldwide to study in Hong Kong. Unlike most national scholarships, the HKPFS is open to all nationalities, all academic disciplines, and all stages of life — there is no age cap, no work-experience requirement and no quota by country of origin. The scheme runs in parallel with normal PhD admissions: every year roughly 400 fellowships are awarded out of an applicant pool of more than 13,000.

The base package for awardees consists of:

- **Annual stipend:** HK$340,800 (~US$43,690), paid in monthly instalments
- **Annual conference and research travel allowance:** HK$14,200 (~US$1,820)
- **Duration:** up to three years (universities typically top up support if a PhD requires four years)

The stipend alone places HKPFS among the top-paying PhD programmes globally, comfortably above NSF GRFP, most ERC-funded PhD positions in Europe, and Singapore's NUS/NTU President's Graduate Fellowship base rates. Hong Kong's lower personal income tax (none on scholarship income) preserves the value, though the city's housing costs partially offset the headline figure.

## Eligible institutions

You may apply to PhD programmes at any of the eight UGC-funded universities. Each university has its own internal HKPFS quota and its own additional top-up scholarship for fellows:

1. **The University of Hong Kong (HKU)** — Presidential PhD Scholarship: HK$40,000 cash in Year 1, HK$20,000/year afterwards, full composition fee waiver, guaranteed Year-1 hall place
2. **The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK)** — Vice-Chancellor PhD Scholarship: HK$80,000 in Year 1, additional research and travel support, hostel allowance
3. **The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST)** — RedBird PhD Scholarship for HKPFS holders: tuition waiver and additional cash awards
4. **City University of Hong Kong (CityUHK)** — top-up scholarship and dedicated supervision support
5. **The Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU)** — Presidential PhD Fellowship Scheme top-up
6. **Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU)** — additional fellowship and tuition coverage
7. **Lingnan University (LU)** — supplementary stipend and research budget
8. **The Education University of Hong Kong (EdUHK)** — additional living allowance and conference funding

The exact value of each top-up varies year to year, so check the graduate school page of every shortlisted institution before you finalise your university ranking on the RGC system.

## Eligibility — who can realistically apply

The official eligibility statement is broad: any "new full-time PhD applicant" at a UGC-funded university, regardless of nationality or ethnic background. In practice, strong HKPFS applicants share a common profile.

**Academic record.** A first-class honours bachelor's degree (or top-decile equivalent) from a research-intensive university is the floor. Most successful candidates also hold a master's by research or a master's with a substantial thesis component, particularly in STEM fields. For mainland Chinese applicants, a 985/211 background with GPA 3.7+/4.0 is typical; for international applicants, a 1st-class UK degree, a 3.8+ US GPA, or a Beihang/Tsinghua-equivalent CGPA is common.

**Research output.** Even at the entry stage, the most competitive applicants in sciences and engineering have at least one peer-reviewed publication (often as second or third author), a conference paper, or substantive research-assistant experience. In humanities and social sciences, a published article, a strong master's thesis, or a clearly defined research proposal carries equivalent weight.

**English proficiency.** Each university applies its own threshold, but the practical bar is IELTS 6.5+ overall (no band below 5.5) or TOEFL iBT 80+. Some departments — particularly humanities, business and law — expect IELTS 7.0+. Native English speakers and graduates of English-medium programmes are usually exempt.

**Reference letters.** Two to three academic referees who can speak directly to your research ability are required. Reference quality is one of the strongest predictors of HKPFS success — lukewarm letters from senior figures are weaker than detailed letters from active researchers who have supervised your work.

## The two-step application — exactly how it works

The HKPFS uses a two-step process that trips up many first-time applicants. You must complete **both** steps before the December deadline or your application is invalid.

**Step 1 — RGC Initial Application (HKPFSES).**
Between 1 September 2025 and **1 December 2025 at 12:00 noon Hong Kong Time**, register on the HKPFS Electronic System (cerg1.ugc.edu.hk/hkpfs) and submit a single Initial Application. You will:

- Choose a maximum of **two PhD programmes** across one or two universities (in ranked order)
- Upload a CV, research proposal abstract, and basic academic credentials
- Provide referee details
- Receive an **HKPFS Reference Number** by email within minutes

The ranking matters: if you are awarded HKPFS, the offer is made by your **first-choice institution first**. If you decline that offer, the fellowship is **forfeited** — it does **not** transfer to your second-choice university. So rank strategically, not aspirationally.

**Step 2 — Full PhD Application to each chosen university.**
By each university's stated deadline (almost always also **1 December 2025**, but check — some close earlier or accept slightly later), submit the full PhD application via that university's online admission portal. Critically, you must:

- Tick the box indicating that you are an HKPFS applicant
- Enter your HKPFS Reference Number obtained in Step 1
- Upload the full document set: transcripts, degree certificates, English test scores, research proposal (5–10 pages), CV, publications/writing sample, statement of purpose, and reference letters

Failure to enter the HKPFS Reference Number on the university application is the most common reason a strong candidate is silently dropped from fellowship consideration.

## Find a supervisor before you apply, not after

The single most important strategic move in an HKPFS application is identifying and contacting a potential supervisor **before** the December deadline — ideally between September and early November. Hong Kong PhDs are supervisor-led: even with a perfect dossier, an application without a willing supervisor is unlikely to advance.

A professional outreach email should:

- Use a clear subject line: "Prospective HKPFS Applicant 2026/27 — [Your Name], [Research Topic]"
- Open with one sentence on who you are and where you studied
- State the specific aspect of the supervisor's work that motivates you (cite a paper)
- Briefly outline your proposed research direction (3–5 sentences)
- Attach your CV, transcripts and a one-page research summary
- Close by explicitly asking whether they would be willing to support an HKPFS application

A reply expressing interest — even a tentative one — gives you a credible person to name in your application and in many universities' internal nomination process is the deciding factor in whether your file is shortlisted.

## How the RGC panels evaluate you

Once universities nominate their top HKPFS candidates, the files go to one of two RGC selection panels:

- **Panel I:** Sciences, Medicine, Engineering, Technology
- **Panel II:** Humanities, Social Sciences, Business Studies

Each panel evaluates four official criteria:

1. **Academic excellence** — track record, degree class, GPA, recognitions
2. **Research ability and potential** — publications, master's thesis, research proposal quality, supervisor's assessment
3. **Communication and interpersonal skills** — demonstrated through references, statement of purpose, and (for shortlisted candidates) interviews
4. **Leadership abilities** — academic, professional or community leadership

There is no fixed weighting, but academic excellence and research potential dominate the early scoring. Communication and leadership become tiebreakers between technically equivalent candidates. The panels meet between January and April, and successful applicants are notified by RGC by email in **May 2026**.

## The research proposal — your highest-leverage document

Your transcripts and publications are largely fixed by the time you apply, but your research proposal is fully under your control. A strong HKPFS proposal is typically 5–10 pages and contains:

- A precise research question, situated in the existing literature
- A clear gap or contribution your work will make
- A workable methodology that the panel can evaluate
- A realistic three-year timeline aligned with the fellowship duration
- A short discussion of how the proposed supervisor's expertise enables the project
- A bibliography of 20–40 high-quality references

Avoid the two most common failure modes: a proposal that is too broad ("I want to study artificial intelligence") and a proposal that recycles your master's thesis without extending it. The panel is checking whether you can do *new* research at PhD level.

## What if you don't get the HKPFS?

Of more than 13,000 applicants each year, only 400 win the fellowship. The good news is that the HKPFS application is also the strongest possible regular PhD application — universities frequently extend a normal PhD admission offer (with their standard postgraduate studentship of around HK$18,000–HK$22,000/month) to applicants who narrowly missed the fellowship. Many universities also have parallel internal awards such as the HKU Presidential PhD Scholarship for non-HKPFS top candidates, or the CUHK Vice-Chancellor PhD Scholarship Scheme for non-HKPFS holders.

If you are committed to doing your PhD in Hong Kong, applying through HKPFS first and then accepting a regular PhD offer is almost always better than applying only for regular admission, because the HKPFS pipeline forces departments to evaluate you against their highest standard and surfaces you to multiple decision-makers.

## Visa, fees and life in Hong Kong as a fellow

Once you accept an HKPFS offer, the university sponsors your **student visa** (entry permit for study). You will pay the standard non-local PhD composition fee (around HK$42,100/year), but most universities waive this for HKPFS holders or fold the waiver into their top-up package. Confirm this in your offer letter before accepting.

After completing your PhD, you are eligible for the **IANG visa** (Immigration Arrangements for Non-local Graduates), which gives you an unconditional 24-month stay to find work in Hong Kong, extendable, and counts toward the **seven-year residency requirement for permanent residency**. Hong Kong PhDs are also competitive applicants for the **Top Talent Pass Scheme (TTPS) Category A** if they later move overseas and want to return.

## Timeline at a glance for the 2026/27 cycle

- **June–August 2025:** Identify supervisors, draft research proposal, prepare IELTS/TOEFL
- **September 2025:** RGC HKPFSES opens; supervisor outreach intensifies
- **October–November 2025:** Refine proposal with supervisor input, secure referees, request transcripts
- **1 December 2025, 12:00 noon HKT:** RGC Initial Application closes
- **1 December 2025 (or earlier per university):** Full university application closes
- **December 2025–February 2026:** University internal nomination
- **February–April 2026:** RGC panel review and interviews
- **May 2026:** Award notifications by RGC
- **August–September 2026:** Programme commences

Treat the December 1 deadline as **immovable**. Late applications — even by minutes — are rejected automatically by the system, and the RGC does not consider extensions.

## Final word

The HKPFS is competitive but transparent: the criteria are public, the evaluation is panel-based, and there is no hidden quota that disqualifies you because of where you were born or how old you are. The applicants who win it almost always combine three things — a serious academic record, a credible research proposal aligned with a willing Hong Kong supervisor, and an early start on the application. If you can build all three by November of your application year, your odds are far better than the headline 3% acceptance rate suggests, because you will already be in the small subset of applicants whose files reach the panel with a full set of strong signals.

For mainland Chinese students considering between mainland 985 PhDs, Singapore's NUS/NTU schemes, and overseas PhDs in the UK or US, HKPFS occupies a genuinely distinctive position: world-class universities, a Chinese-cultural environment, full English-medium research, the highest stipend in the region, and a clean post-PhD pathway via IANG and the seven-year permanent residency rule. For international applicants, it is one of the few Asia-based fellowships that pays at parity with North American and European awards while opening doors into research collaborations across the Greater Bay Area.
