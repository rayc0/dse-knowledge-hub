# The Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU) Complete Guide 2026

Among Hong Kong's eight UGC-funded universities, PolyU occupies a distinctive position. It is not the oldest — that honour belongs to HKU. It is not the highest-ranked overall — HKU and HKUST tend to sit above it on the QS table. But on a number of practical measures that matter to undergraduates — the quality of professional accreditation, the calibre of design education, the strength of hotel and tourism management, and the size of the campus right in the centre of Kowloon — PolyU is genuinely first among Hong Kong's universities, and in some subject areas it is first in Asia.

In QS World University Rankings 2026, PolyU sits in the global top 60, having climbed steadily over the past decade. More importantly for prospective students, eight of its subjects rank in the global top 50, and the School of Hotel and Tourism Management has been ranked the world's best for hospitality and leisure management for several consecutive years. PolyU's School of Design is the only Hong Kong institution to feature consistently in the global top 20 for Art and Design.

This guide walks through what PolyU is in 2026, the broad-based admission scheme that distinguishes it from HKU and CUHK, the JUPAS and non-JUPAS pathways, tuition and scholarships, the flagship faculties, accommodation, and the career trajectories that follow a PolyU degree. If you are deciding between PolyU and another UGC institution — or weighing PolyU's design or physiotherapy programmes against overseas offers — this is the context you need.

---

## 1. PolyU's Identity in 2026

PolyU traces its origin to 1937 as the Government Trade School, the territory's first publicly funded technical school. It became Hong Kong Polytechnic in 1972, and was granted full university status in 1994 as The Hong Kong Polytechnic University. That trajectory matters: PolyU is fundamentally an application-oriented research university, not a classical liberal arts institution.

Three features define PolyU's identity in 2026:

1. **Professional accreditation density.** PolyU offers more accredited professional programmes than any other Hong Kong university. Civil engineering, mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, building services, surveying, accounting, construction management, optometry, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, radiography, nursing, social work, speech therapy and Chinese medicine all lead directly to professional registration in Hong Kong. For students who want a clear line from degree to licence to career, PolyU is the most efficient route in the city.

2. **Two world-leading specialisations.** The School of Hotel and Tourism Management (SHTM) and the School of Design are not merely strong by Hong Kong standards — they are global leaders. SHTM has held the world number one position in QS subject rankings for hospitality and leisure management for several consecutive years. The School of Design occupies a comparable position for industrial and communication design in Asia.

3. **Broad-based admission.** Unlike HKU and CUHK, where most JUPAS applicants choose a single discipline at the point of admission, PolyU operates a "broad-based admission scheme" for many of its programmes. Students enter a faculty or scheme (for example, "Scheme in Engineering" or "Scheme in Design") and choose their major after a year of common-core study. This delays the discipline commitment and is particularly valuable for candidates who are torn between related fields.

The campus itself is unusual: 94,600 square metres concentrated in Hung Hom, directly opposite the Hung Hom MTR / cross-harbour rail terminus. Few large research universities anywhere are this central. The location explains why PolyU has retained a strong urban, working-city character that is markedly different from CUHK's Sha Tin valley campus or HKUST's Clear Water Bay coastal site.

## 2. Faculties, Schools and Flagship Programmes

PolyU is organised into six faculties and two independent schools, all offering undergraduate degrees:

- **Faculty of Business** — accounting, finance, management, marketing, global supply chain management, aviation management.
- **Faculty of Construction and Environment** — building engineering, building services engineering, civil engineering, environmental engineering, surveying, construction management, urban planning, real estate.
- **Faculty of Engineering** — mechanical, biomedical, electrical, electronic and information, computing (computer science, AI, data science, financial technology), industrial systems engineering.
- **Faculty of Health and Social Sciences** — applied social sciences, social work, social policy, nursing, optometry, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, radiography, medical laboratory science, speech therapy, food safety and technology, biomedical engineering.
- **Faculty of Humanities** — Chinese language and bilingual studies, English studies, English and applied linguistics, translation, Chinese culture, Japanese studies.
- **Faculty of Science** — applied biology, applied chemistry, applied mathematics, applied physics, food technology, investment science.
- **School of Design** — independent school. Programmes in advertising design, communication design, environmental design, industrial design, interactive media, product design.
- **School of Hotel and Tourism Management** — independent school. Programmes in hotel management, tourism and events management, integrated convention and event management.

Three programmes deserve detailed attention because they define PolyU's external reputation.

**The School of Design** is the only Hong Kong design school to consistently feature in the global top 20. It runs a four-year BA(Hons) Scheme in Design admitting students into a common first year before they specialise into one of six majors. Admission requires a portfolio interview in addition to JUPAS scores; the design entry score in DSE is consistently among the highest of any creative programme in Asia. Graduates work at firms such as Nike, IDEO, Frog Design, Apple, and Samsung's design studios in Seoul and London.

**The School of Hotel and Tourism Management (SHTM)** offers BSc(Hons) programmes in hotel management and tourism and events management. SHTM runs Hotel Icon, a fully operating four-star teaching hotel in Tsim Sha Tsui East, where students complete supervised placements as part of their degree — a feature unmatched anywhere else in Asia. The school's industry network includes the Mandarin Oriental, Marriott, Hyatt, Peninsula and Shangri-La groups, and a significant proportion of graduates begin their careers as management trainees rather than line staff.

**The Department of Rehabilitation Sciences** offers BSc(Hons) programmes in physiotherapy and occupational therapy. Both are accredited by the relevant Hong Kong professional boards and by the World Confederation for Physical Therapy / World Federation of Occupational Therapists, meaning graduates can practise in the UK, Australia, Canada and Singapore subject to local registration. JUPAS cut-offs for physiotherapy at PolyU regularly sit second only to medicine and dentistry — typically requiring six 5\* grades or equivalent.

## 3. Broad-Based Admission Schemes

For many of PolyU's largest programmes, applicants are admitted into a "broad-based scheme" rather than a single named major. The schemes in 2026 include:

- Scheme in Engineering
- Scheme in Computing
- Scheme in Construction and Environment
- Scheme in Business
- Scheme in Hotel, Tourism and Hospitality
- Scheme in Design
- Scheme in Health Sciences
- Scheme in Applied Social Sciences

Inside each scheme, students complete a common first-year curriculum and then declare a major. The scheme structure has two consequences worth understanding before applying.

First, the scheme as a whole has one entrance score, but individual majors inside the scheme may be competitive. A student admitted to Scheme in Engineering with a JUPAS score at the scheme cut-off will need to perform well in the first-year common modules to secure a place in their preferred major (for example, AI and Big Data, which is currently the most oversubscribed engineering major). For risk-averse applicants this can be uncomfortable; for genuinely undecided applicants it is liberating.

Second, some flagship programmes — physiotherapy, occupational therapy, radiography, optometry, accounting and finance, design — operate **outside** the broad-based schemes and admit directly into the named programme. These programmes are typically more competitive at the JUPAS stage and have a clearer admission ceiling.

PolyU also offers a small number of high-prestige niche programmes, including BSc(Hons) Investment Science (a quantitative finance programme), BBA(Hons) Aviation Management and Logistics, and the BSc(Hons) Bioinformatics and Genomics programme run jointly with the Faculty of Science.

## 4. JUPAS Admission

For Hong Kong DSE candidates, PolyU is admitted via the Joint University Programmes Admissions System (JUPAS). The 2026 admission rules to know:

- **No compulsory subject requirement at the institutional level.** Beyond the 332233 university-entry minimum (Chinese 3, English 3, Mathematics Compulsory Part 2, Citizenship and Social Development Attained, two electives at level 3), individual programmes may add specific elective requirements — for example, Physics for engineering, Biology for health sciences.
- **Preferred subjects** influence admission selection. Programmes list two to four preferred electives. Achieving a high grade in a preferred subject improves your effective admission score even if you do not declare it as one of your top weighted subjects.
- **Admission score formulae.** Most programmes use one of two formulae: "Best 5 subjects" or "Chinese + English + Best 3 subjects". Some programmes also apply weightings — typically 1.5x for Mathematics Extended Module M1/M2, or 1.5x for the most relevant preferred elective.
- **Programme priority.** PolyU only considers a programme on your JUPAS list if it appears in **Band A** (your top three choices). Programmes outside Band A will not be reviewed by PolyU for admission — a stricter rule than HKU, which considers Band B for many programmes.

The implication for JUPAS strategy is straightforward: if PolyU is your target, you must place its programmes in Band A. The university publishes the median and lower quartile admission scores for each programme on its admission website and the JUPAS handbook each summer; checking the trailing three years is the most reliable basis for a Band A list.

Programmes that require additional assessment beyond JUPAS scores include:

- **School of Design** — portfolio interview, typically in January–March.
- **School of Hotel and Tourism Management** — interview for borderline candidates.
- **Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, optometry, radiography** — interviews and aptitude tests.
- **Nursing** — group interview.
- **Social work** — interview assessing motivation and personal suitability.

## 5. Non-JUPAS and International Admission

Non-local applicants — including mainland Chinese (gaokao), International Baccalaureate, GCE A-Levels, US SAT/AP, and other international qualifications — apply directly to PolyU rather than through JUPAS. The 2026 routes:

- **Mainland gaokao admission** — via PolyU's dedicated gaokao admission scheme. Score requirements vary by province, but in 2025 PolyU admitted students at roughly the 985-university line for most programmes and at top-20 mainland university lines for the School of Design and physiotherapy.
- **International / other qualifications** — IB Diploma typically 36–42 points (programme-dependent); GCE A-Levels typically AAA–A\*A\*A\*; SAT 1450+ with strong AP scores. English requirement: IELTS 6.5 (no band below 5.5) or TOEFL iBT 80, unless the applicant's secondary education was conducted in English.
- **Associate degree / Higher Diploma top-up** — PolyU runs a substantial Senior Year Entry intake admitting students who have completed an associate degree or higher diploma. Most major faculties accept top-up applications, with quotas published annually.

The application portal for non-JUPAS first-year admission opens in September of the year before entry and closes in late January, with an early-round deadline in mid-November. Applicants for the School of Design and SHTM should plan for portfolio submission and interview windows before that close.

## 6. Tuition and Living Costs in 2026

PolyU is a UGC-funded university, so its undergraduate tuition is regulated by the Government:

- **Local students** — HK$42,100 per academic year for UGC-funded undergraduate places. This figure has not changed since 1997 and is identical across all eight UGC-funded universities. Self-financed places (taught masters, some second-degree programmes) are charged at programme-specific rates.
- **Non-local students** — HK$145,000 per academic year for most undergraduate programmes in 2026/27, raised in line with the Government's 2024 announcement that non-local UGC tuition would rise across the eight institutions. Some programmes (notably the School of Design and SHTM) charge slightly higher rates.

Living costs in Hong Kong remain high. A realistic 2026 budget for a non-local student living on or near PolyU's Hung Hom campus:

- On-campus hall (limited supply): HK$15,000–24,000 per academic year.
- Off-campus shared accommodation in Hung Hom or Ho Man Tin: HK$5,500–8,500 per month for a shared room.
- Food and daily expenses: HK$4,500–6,500 per month.
- Transport: HK$400–700 per month with Octopus student concession.
- Total annual cost (tuition + living) for a non-local student: roughly HK$240,000–290,000.

PolyU's hall capacity is limited relative to its student population. First-year non-local students are generally guaranteed a hall place; senior-year students enter a points-based allocation system. Many senior students live in the Hung Hom and Whampoa Garden area, which is walking distance to campus.

## 7. Scholarships

PolyU operates one of the larger scholarship programmes among Hong Kong universities, structured into three tiers.

**Entrance scholarships** are awarded automatically based on JUPAS or non-JUPAS admission scores. The Talent Admission Scheme offers full tuition plus a living stipend (around HK$60,000 per year) for the top tier of admitted candidates — typically those with seven 5\*\* DSE grades or international equivalent, and outstanding non-academic achievements. The Reaching Out Award and Hong Kong Polytechnic University Scholarships provide partial tuition coverage for the next tier.

**Faculty-level scholarships** are awarded by individual schools — particularly the School of Design and SHTM — based on portfolio quality, interview performance, or sustained academic achievement during study.

**Mainland scholarships** include the National Scholarship for Outstanding Self-Financed Students Abroad (administered by the China Scholarship Council) and PolyU's own mainland recruitment scholarships, which are generally awarded to gaokao top scorers from key provinces.

For internationally-funded students, the Hong Kong PhD Fellowship Scheme is research-only and does not apply to undergraduates, but PolyU's Talent Scholarship provides comparable support for outstanding international applicants.

## 8. Campus, Student Life and Hall Culture

PolyU's Hung Hom campus is compact and dense — a stark contrast to the sprawling CUHK and HKUST sites. The campus has been subject to significant redevelopment over the past decade, including the Jockey Club Innovation Tower (designed by Zaha Hadid) which houses the School of Design, and the student amenities centre on the western edge of campus.

Student societies are organised through the Federation of Polytechnic University Students (PolySU). The federation has had a complicated political history — it was deregistered in 2022 and operates today as a less politically engaged body than its CUHK and HKU counterparts. Student social life is concentrated around faculty societies (engineering, design, hotel and tourism), residence halls, and the active sports programme.

Hall culture at PolyU is moderately developed: traditional inter-hall sports tournaments, hall dinners, and orientation camps run each year. Halls are co-educational with single-sex floors; most rooms are shared (twin or quad).

The campus is connected by a footbridge to Hung Hom MTR station, and from there students reach Tsim Sha Tsui in two stops, Central in three. PolyU's location gives students unusually easy access to internships and part-time work in Tsim Sha Tsui, Mong Kok and Central — all of which are within 30 minutes of campus by MTR.

## 9. Career Outcomes

The PolyU Graduate Employment Survey 2024 reported a 95.4 per cent overall employment rate within six months of graduation, with median monthly starting salaries ranging from approximately HK$22,000 for humanities and applied social sciences to HK$32,000+ for engineering, computing, accounting and finance, and the School of Design. SHTM graduates entering management trainee programmes at international hotel groups typically reported starting salaries of HK$24,000–28,000.

The pattern of PolyU graduate destinations differs from HKU and CUHK in three ways:

1. **Higher proportion entering professional practice immediately.** Because so many programmes are accredited (engineering, surveying, optometry, physiotherapy, nursing, social work), a large share of graduates begin licensed professional careers within their first year. HKU and CUHK graduates more frequently enter business, finance and consulting roles where the discipline of the first degree is less determining.

2. **Strong representation in the design and creative sector.** The School of Design's network places graduates in international design consultancies and in-house design teams at technology companies — destinations that other Hong Kong universities cannot match.

3. **Hospitality leadership pipeline.** SHTM graduates dominate the management trainee pipelines of major hotel groups not just in Hong Kong but across the Greater Bay Area, Singapore, Tokyo and Dubai. The school's alumni network is a meaningful asset for early career mobility.

PolyU's Mainland Affairs Office and Career Services jointly support graduates returning to mainland China — particularly important given that around 25 per cent of PolyU's undergraduates are non-local, with the majority from the mainland. The IANG visa allows non-local graduates to remain in Hong Kong for up to 24 months after graduation to seek work, and a substantial proportion of PolyU graduates use this window to begin careers in Hong Kong before deciding whether to return to the mainland or move on internationally.

## 10. Should You Choose PolyU?

PolyU is the right choice if any of the following apply:

- You want a programme leading directly to professional registration (engineering, surveying, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, optometry, nursing, social work, speech therapy).
- You are aiming at the world's leading hotel and tourism management education.
- You want one of Asia's strongest design educations, with a portfolio-based admission process.
- You value a central, transit-connected campus with strong industry access.
- You are undecided between related disciplines and want the flexibility of a broad-based admission scheme with major declaration after a common first year.

PolyU is less likely to be the right choice if you are looking for medicine or dentistry (offered only by HKU and CUHK), if you want a classical liberal arts undergraduate experience (HKU and Lingnan are stronger fits), or if you specifically want a research-doctorate-track route — although PolyU's research output has grown significantly in the past decade and its postgraduate research programmes are now competitive with HKU and HKUST in several engineering fields.

In 2026, with the city's universities all competing aggressively for international students, PolyU's distinctive offer — applied, accredited, professionally networked, internationally recognised in design and hospitality, and sitting on one of the most accessible campuses in Asia — has rarely been clearer. For DSE candidates and international applicants with a clear sense of professional direction, it deserves serious consideration in any Band A list.
