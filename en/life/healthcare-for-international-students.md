# Healthcare and Medical Insurance for International Students in Hong Kong 2026 — Complete Guide

Hong Kong has one of the best healthcare systems in Asia — it consistently ranks in the top five globally on the Bloomberg and WHO health efficiency indices. For international students arriving on a student visa, the system is also relatively affordable, accessible, and English-friendly. Almost every major hospital has English-speaking doctors, most GP clinics accept walk-ins, and your university will provide free or low-cost primary healthcare services on campus.

But the system has its quirks: a dual public/private structure, a specific set of rules about who pays what, and insurance requirements that vary by institution and nationality. This guide walks through what every international student needs to know about Hong Kong healthcare before they arrive, during their studies, and if something goes wrong.

## The Hong Kong healthcare system in one paragraph

Hong Kong has a **dual public/private system**. The public system — run by the Hospital Authority (HA) — provides heavily subsidised care to Hong Kong residents at very low cost. The private system runs in parallel, with private clinics and private hospitals charging market rates. For international students holding a student visa, you are generally eligible to use public hospitals at slightly higher non-resident rates, and you also have the option of private clinics and private hospital care (usually covered by your student medical insurance).

**Public healthcare for non-residents (student visa holders)**:
- Accident and Emergency Department (A&E): HK$1,230 per visit (2026 non-eligible person rate)
- General outpatient clinic: HK$755 per visit
- Hospital admission: HK$5,100 per day (non-eligible rate)
- Specialist outpatient: HK$1,335 first visit, HK$990 follow-up

**Private healthcare**:
- GP consultation: HK$300–800 per visit
- Specialist consultation: HK$800–2,500 per visit
- Private hospital day stay: HK$2,000–8,000
- Private hospital overnight: HK$5,000–20,000+

**Comparison**: For a flu with a GP visit, private care is often cheaper and faster than public. For a serious illness requiring specialist consultation or hospitalisation, public care can be dramatically cheaper (but waits are longer and the experience is less hotel-like).

## Mandatory medical insurance

All major Hong Kong universities require international students to hold medical insurance for the duration of their studies. The exact requirements vary:

**HKU**: Mandatory insurance through the HKU Medical Insurance Scheme for Non-local Students, approximately HK$1,500–2,200/year. Coverage includes inpatient, outpatient, accident, and emergency.

**CUHK**: Mandatory enrolment in the CUHK international student insurance scheme, approximately HK$1,400–2,000/year. Similar coverage.

**HKUST**: Mandatory insurance through the HKUST scheme, approximately HK$1,500–2,100/year.

**PolyU, CityU, HKBU, EdUHK, LingU**: All require insurance; some accept equivalent private plans purchased by the student, others require enrolment in the university scheme.

**Typical coverage**:
- Inpatient hospitalisation: HK$500,000–1,000,000 per year
- Outpatient services: HK$15,000–30,000 per year
- Emergency and accident: HK$100,000+
- Dental: usually limited or excluded
- Maternity: usually excluded
- Pre-existing conditions: usually excluded or limited
- Mental health: limited coverage

**Important**: University insurance plans are usually basic. For comprehensive coverage, consider supplementary insurance (BUPA, AXA, AIA, or international plans like Cigna Global, IMG). Annual premium for comprehensive supplementary plans: HK$4,000–15,000.

## University health services

Every major Hong Kong university operates an on-campus health clinic with a GP:

**HKU — University Health Service (UHS)**:
- Location: Main campus, various clinics
- Services: GP consultation, nurse services, vaccinations, travel health
- Cost: Free or very low cost (HK$20–50 per visit) for enrolled students
- Phone: +852 2817 1800
- Hours: Monday–Friday, approximately 9am–5pm

**CUHK — University Health Service**:
- Location: Main campus (Pommerenke Student Centre)
- Free GP consultation, nurse services, vaccinations
- Phone: +852 3943 6462
- Hours: Monday–Friday

**HKUST — Health, Safety and Environment Office Student Health Service**:
- Location: Main campus, 6/F Chow Yei Ching Building
- Free GP consultation for enrolled students
- Phone: +852 2358 6375

**PolyU, CityU, HKBU, EdUHK, LingU**: All have equivalent services.

**Typical services**:
- General medical consultation (flu, minor injuries, routine issues)
- Women's health (pap smear, contraception)
- Travel medicine and vaccinations
- Mental health counselling and psychological support
- Basic lab tests (blood work, urinalysis)
- Referral to specialists when needed
- Prescription dispensing
- Vaccination programmes (HPV, hepatitis, influenza)

**Not typically available on campus**:
- Dental care (go to a private dentist)
- Specialist consultation (referral to hospital or private specialist)
- Surgery (referral to hospital)
- Radiology imaging beyond basic (referral to imaging centre)

## How to see a doctor in Hong Kong

### Option 1: University health service (first choice for routine issues)

- Walk in or book online via the university health portal
- Bring your student ID and health insurance card
- GP consultation is free or low cost
- For anything routine (cold, headache, minor skin issue, vaccination) this is the easiest option

### Option 2: Private GP clinic

- Walk-in at any private clinic in your area
- Search "family doctor" on DocDoc or FindDoc websites
- Cost: HK$300–800 per visit (paid upfront)
- Get receipt, submit to insurance for reimbursement
- English-speaking doctors are widely available

### Option 3: Public General Outpatient Clinic (GOPC)

- Pre-book via the 24-hour phone line (1836 115) — otherwise very long waits
- Cost for non-eligible persons: HK$755 per visit
- Wait times can be 2–4 hours even with booking
- Useful for complex medical issues, follow-up care, chronic disease management
- Not typically the first choice for routine visits due to cost and wait

### Option 4: Accident and Emergency (A&E)

- Use for actual emergencies (serious injury, severe pain, chest pain, breathing problems, suspected stroke)
- Cost: HK$1,230 per visit for non-eligible persons
- Triaged by severity — minor issues can wait 3–6 hours
- All major public hospitals have A&E

## Emergency contact numbers

- **999** — police, fire, ambulance (Hong Kong universal emergency)
- **112** — European emergency number (also works in HK)
- **1836 115** — Hospital Authority general inquiry and GOPC booking
- **2528 9900** — Samaritans Hong Kong (English-language mental health crisis line)
- **2896 0000** — Suicide Prevention Services

Save these in your phone on arrival.

## Hospital options

### Public hospitals (Hospital Authority)

Major teaching hospitals and their affiliated universities:
- **Queen Mary Hospital** (HKU affiliated) — in Pokfulam
- **Prince of Wales Hospital** (CUHK affiliated) — in Sha Tin
- **United Christian Hospital** — in Kwun Tong
- **Tuen Mun Hospital** — in the New Territories
- **Queen Elizabeth Hospital** — in Kowloon
- **Princess Margaret Hospital** — in Kwai Chung
- **Caritas Medical Centre** — in Sham Shui Po

All public hospitals accept non-residents at the non-eligible person rates. All have English-speaking staff. Quality of care is excellent but wait times for non-emergency specialist care are long.

### Private hospitals

Major private hospitals:
- **Hong Kong Sanatorium & Hospital** (Happy Valley) — most expensive, most luxurious
- **Hong Kong Adventist Hospital** — Tsuen Wan and Stubbs Road
- **Canossa Hospital (Caritas)** — Mid-Levels
- **St Paul's Hospital** — Causeway Bay
- **Evangel Hospital** — Tsim Sha Tsui
- **Hong Kong Baptist Hospital** — Kowloon Tong (near CUHK affiliated)
- **Matilda International Hospital** — The Peak (most expensive, most international)
- **Union Hospital** — Sha Tin (close to CUHK)

Private hospitals offer faster access, shorter wait times, more comfort (private rooms, English service), and specialist care. Prices are 5–10× higher than public but most insurance plans cover a significant portion.

## Common medical scenarios

### "I have a cold/flu/fever"

→ University health service first. Free or low cost, quick, English-speaking. Only escalate to private GP or A&E if symptoms are severe (high fever, difficulty breathing, severe weakness).

### "I twisted my ankle playing basketball"

→ University health service for minor sprains (ice, compression, elevation, pain relief). If severe (suspected fracture, severe swelling, cannot weight-bear), go directly to A&E at the nearest public hospital.

### "I have persistent stomach pain"

→ Private GP or university clinic for initial assessment. If concerning signs (bloody stool, severe pain, weight loss), escalate to specialist referral.

### "I need a prescription refill for my home country medication"

→ Bring a written prescription from your home country doctor with translation if not in English. See a private GP who can issue a local prescription. Some medications have different names in HK — check with the pharmacy (Watsons, Mannings have English-speaking pharmacists).

### "I need contraception"

→ University health service provides basic contraception (condoms, oral contraceptive pill). For IUD, implant, or specialist care, referred to women's health clinic at the university or a private gynaecologist.

### "I'm having a mental health crisis"

→ University counselling service (CAPS — Counselling and Psychological Services, or similar name). For urgent crises, Samaritans (2896 0000) or the nearest A&E.

### "I have a toothache"

→ Private dentist. University health services usually do not cover dental. Cost for a basic dental exam: HK$300–800. Filling: HK$800–2,500. Root canal: HK$5,000–15,000. Most student insurance does NOT cover routine dental — budget for this out of pocket.

### "I'm pregnant"

→ Private gynaecologist first, then decide on public or private prenatal care and delivery. Public hospital maternity care is heavily subsidised for residents but for non-eligible persons it costs HK$57,800 for a basic delivery. Private hospital delivery: HK$80,000–200,000. Most student insurance does NOT cover maternity.

## Mental health services

Mental health is a growing concern among Hong Kong international students, and every major university offers dedicated services:

**HKU**: Counselling and Psychological Services (CEDARS)
**CUHK**: Wellness and Counselling Centre
**HKUST**: Counselling and Wellness Centre
**PolyU**: Student Counselling Service
**CityU**: Student Development Services Counselling Section
**HKBU**: Counselling and Development Centre
**EdUHK**: Student Affairs Office Counselling
**LingU**: Student Counselling Team

**Services available**:
- Free individual counselling (limited sessions per year)
- Group therapy and support groups
- Crisis intervention
- Referral to psychiatrists for medication management
- Workshops on stress, sleep, relationships, academic pressure

**For cultural reasons, many Chinese and Asian students are reluctant to use counselling services.** Counselling is completely confidential — your professors, classmates, family, and immigration office will never see your records without your written consent. Academic grades, visa status, and housing are not affected by counselling use. If you are struggling, use the services.

**Serious psychiatric conditions** (severe depression, bipolar disorder, psychosis, active suicidal ideation, severe anxiety) need psychiatric medication and specialist care. University counselling services will refer you to a psychiatrist. Private psychiatric consultation costs HK$1,500–3,500 per visit; public psychiatric care through the Hospital Authority is cheaper but has long waits for non-urgent cases.

## Vaccinations and travel health

**Recommended vaccinations for Hong Kong**:
- MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) — should be up to date
- DTaP/Tdap (diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis) — booster if needed
- Hepatitis A + B — recommended
- Influenza — annual
- HPV — women under 26, men if applicable
- COVID-19 — follow current Hong Kong recommendations
- Varicella (chickenpox) — if not already immune

Most of these can be administered at your university health service for free or low cost. Travel vaccinations (typhoid, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis) for trips outside Hong Kong can be obtained at the same clinic.

## Before you arrive — preparation checklist

1. **Vaccination records** — bring documentation of all completed vaccinations, translated to English if not already
2. **Medical history summary** — a one-page document summarising any chronic conditions, allergies, surgeries, current medications
3. **Prescription medications** — bring 3 months' supply with a letter from your home doctor. Check that they are legal in Hong Kong (most common medications are legal but some controlled substances need import permission)
4. **Dental check-up** — have a dental cleaning and exam at home before arrival; dental care in Hong Kong is expensive and not usually covered by student insurance
5. **Eye exam** — get a current eye exam and bring glasses/contacts prescription
6. **Insurance cards and policy details** — bring physical and digital copies of any insurance documentation
7. **Emergency contact information** — have your family's contact info and your own emergency contacts recorded

## During your studies — ongoing reminders

- **Enrol in mandatory university insurance** — your university will automatically enrol most students
- **Register at the university health service** during orientation
- **Save emergency numbers** in your phone
- **Know your nearest A&E hospital** and how to get there (taxi is the fastest option for most hospitals)
- **Keep a list of prescription medications** in English and Chinese for emergencies
- **Have a small stash of basic medicines** (paracetamol, ibuprofen, cold medicine, bandages)

## Common pitfalls

1. **Assuming healthcare is "too expensive"** — many international students avoid seeing a doctor due to perceived cost, but the university clinic is often free or very cheap. Don't delay care.

2. **Forgetting to submit insurance claims** — you paid for insurance; use it. Keep receipts and submit claims.

3. **Ignoring mental health issues** — academic stress, homesickness, and cultural adjustment are real and common. Use the free counselling service.

4. **Self-prescribing with old medication** — if you run out of your home country medication, see a GP and get a proper prescription rather than buying random alternatives.

5. **Travelling with medication that is illegal in Hong Kong** — check the Hong Kong list of controlled substances before importing medications. Some common prescription drugs (ADHD stimulants, some pain medications) require special import permits.

6. **Not having travel medical insurance for trips outside HK** — your university plan may not cover you outside Hong Kong. If you travel to mainland China or elsewhere, buy temporary travel insurance.

## Practical conclusion

Hong Kong healthcare for international students is one of the easier parts of student life to navigate — the system is accessible, mostly English-friendly, affordable at the university level, and high-quality at the professional level. The **first-line resource is always your university health service** (free or nearly free, quick, English-speaking), with private GPs for slightly faster service and public A&E for genuine emergencies. Mandatory insurance provided by the university covers most serious incidents, and supplementary insurance is worth considering if you want comfort-level coverage.

The single most important piece of advice: **use the services**. Many international students go years without visiting a doctor or mental health counsellor because they feel it is "not necessary" or "too embarrassing." Both attitudes lead to worse outcomes. The system exists for you — take advantage of it.
