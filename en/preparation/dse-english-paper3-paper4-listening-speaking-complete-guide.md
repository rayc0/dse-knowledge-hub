# DSE English Paper 3 (Listening & Integrated Skills) & Paper 4 (Speaking) Complete Guide 2026

Most candidates over-invest in Paper 1 (Reading) and Paper 2 (Writing) and treat Paper 3 and Paper 4 as afterthoughts. That is a strategic mistake. **Paper 3 alone is worth 30% of your subject mark — more than any other single paper** — and Paper 4 Speaking, at 10%, is the paper where well-prepared candidates with average English routinely outscore stronger but unprepared classmates. Together these two papers decide 40% of your HKDSE English grade, and both reward technique far more than raw vocabulary.

This guide covers exactly how each paper works in 2026, the decisions that matter most (especially the Section 2 vs Section 3 choice), and a preparation plan you can run in the months before the exam.

## The assessment framework: where Papers 3 and 4 sit

The HKDSE English Language assessment is built from four written/spoken papers plus School-Based Assessment (SBA):

| Component | Weighting | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Paper 1 — Reading | 20% | ~1.5 hours |
| Paper 2 — Writing | 25% | 2 hours |
| **Paper 3 — Listening & Integrated Skills** | **30%** | ~2 hours |
| **Paper 4 — Speaking** | **10%** | ~20 minutes (plus preparation) |
| School-Based Assessment (SBA) | 15% | continuous |

The headline number is Paper 3's **30%**. No other paper carries this much weight, and yet it is the paper where the biggest score swings happen — because it combines listening accuracy with the ability to synthesise information into written tasks under time pressure. Treat Paper 3 as the centre of gravity of your English revision, not the edge of it.

## Paper 3: Listening & Integrated Skills

### Structure and the critical section choice

Paper 3 is divided into **three sections, each worth 15% of the subject** (so Paper 3 = 30% total, since you attempt Section 1 plus one of Section 2/3 — note the assessment is reported in difficulty-tiered terms; confirm the exact section mark allocation against the current HKEAA assessment framework for your year).

- **Section 1** — compulsory. Targets roughly Level 1–4. Mostly straightforward listening comprehension and short integrated tasks built around an audio recording and a Data File.
- **Section 2** — the **easier** elective. Section 1 + Section 2 together assess up to about Level 4–5.
- **Section 3** — the **harder** elective. Section 1 + Section 3 together open up the full range to **Level 5\*\*** and demand more sophisticated, longer integrated writing tasks.

**The single most important decision in this paper is whether you attempt Section 2 or Section 3.** The rule of thumb:

- If you are realistically aiming for **Level 5, 5\*, or 5\*\*** in English, you generally **must attempt Section 3** — the easier section caps how high your overall listening/integrated grade can go.
- If your target is **Level 4 or below**, or English is a genuine struggle, **Section 2** protects your marks: it is more accessible and the integrated task is shorter and more structured.

Decide your default section *before* the exam based on mock results, but stay flexible: glance at both in the reading-through time and switch if Section 3 looks unusually approachable (or Section 2 unusually nasty) on the day.

### How the Data File works

Both elective sections give you a **Data File** — a booklet of source materials (memos, emails, advertisements, charts, articles, transcripts) — alongside the audio recording. The integrated task asks you to combine information *heard* with information *read* into a written product such as a report, proposal, letter, article, or speech.

The skill being tested is **selection and synthesis**, not invention. Markers reward you for:

1. Pulling the *right* points from *both* the audio and the Data File (cross-referencing the two sources is where most marks live).
2. Reorganising those points to fit the task's purpose and audience, not copying them verbatim.
3. Using an appropriate genre, register, and layout for the required text type.
4. Language accuracy and a logical, signposted structure.

### Note-taking technique (this is the whole game)

You hear the recording while having the question paper and Data File in front of you. You cannot rewind. Your notes are the bridge between listening and writing, so train them deliberately:

- **Pre-read aggressively.** Use every second of reading time to scan the Data File and questions so you know *what to listen for* before the audio starts. Underline names, numbers, dates, and the task instruction.
- **Annotate the Data File directly.** Number the points you'll need. Many integrated marks come from spotting a *contradiction* between what a speaker says and what a document states — flag these the instant you hear them.
- **Use a shorthand system** you have practised: arrows, abbreviations, symbols for "but / because / increase / decrease." Capture meaning, not full sentences.
- **Listen for signposts.** "However," "the main problem is," "what we decided," "to summarise" almost always precede a markable point.
- **Numbers and proper nouns** are the most common comprehension marks and the easiest to mis-hear — write them down immediately and double-check spelling against the Data File where possible.

### The 60-minute integrated writing block

Budget your time. A common workable split for the integrated task: ~5 minutes planning a clear structure from your annotated notes, ~45 minutes writing, ~10 minutes checking. The biggest score leak is **running out of time** and leaving a thin conclusion or missing a required source point — plan the whole answer before you write the first paragraph.

Lock in the **genre conventions** for the most likely text types (report headings, proposal recommendations, formal letter openings/closings, article titles and hooks, speech direct address). These are free marks you can secure entirely through practice.

## Paper 4: Speaking

### Structure

Paper 4 is short but high-yield. It has two parts:

- **Part A — Group Interaction.** You are placed in a group (typically four candidates). You get around **10 minutes of preparation** with a prompt, then the group holds a discussion of roughly **8 minutes**. You are marked on your *individual* contribution within the group — not on the group's collective performance.
- **Part B — Individual Response.** Immediately after the discussion, each candidate answers a follow-up question individually (about **one minute**).

Examiners assess four domains: **Pronunciation & Delivery**, **Communication Strategies**, **Vocabulary & Language Patterns**, and **Ideas & Organisation**.

> Note: HKDSE Speaking arrangements have been subject to review in recent years (including periods when Paper 4 was adjusted or suspended). Always confirm the exact format for your exam year against the official HKEAA notice and your school's briefing before relying on the structure above.

### Group Interaction: how to actually score

The Group Interaction is a *test of interaction*, not a debate you must win. The candidates who score highest are the ones who keep the discussion alive and bring others in — not the ones who talk the most.

- **Initiate, but don't dominate.** Open with a clear position or framing if no one else does, then deliberately invite others: "What do you think about that, [name]?" Examiners explicitly reward eliciting and responding.
- **Build on others.** "I agree with your point about cost, and I'd add that…" earns Communication Strategy marks that a fresh, disconnected point does not.
- **Have a structure ready in prep time.** Identify 2–3 sub-points from the prompt, your stance on each, and one example for each. You only need enough to speak for ~90 seconds of net airtime spread across the discussion.
- **Manage the silent and the dominant.** Gently bring in a quiet member ("[Name], do you agree?") and redirect a monopoliser ("That's interesting — can we also consider…?"). Both moves are scored as communication strategy.
- **Rescue, don't withdraw.** If the group goes off-topic or stalls, the candidate who steers it back ("Maybe we should decide on a recommendation now") stands out.
- **Don't script.** Memorised speeches collapse the moment the discussion moves; examiners can spot them instantly and they kill the "interaction" marks.

### Individual Response

The one-minute Part B rewards a tight micro-structure: a direct answer, one reason, one example/elaboration, and a brief close. Do not ramble to fill time — a clear 45-second answer beats a meandering 70-second one. Practise giving structured one-minute answers on common DSE themes (education, technology, environment, youth, Hong Kong life).

### Pronunciation and delivery

You are *not* marked on having a native accent. You are marked on **intelligibility, appropriate stress and intonation, and fluency**. The fastest gains: slow down slightly, finish your word endings (especially -s, -ed, plurals), and use pausing for emphasis rather than fillers ("um," "like"). Recording yourself weekly and listening back is the highest-return practice you can do for this paper.

## A realistic preparation plan

You do not need a separate study block for "listening" and "speaking" — integrate them into a weekly rhythm over the months before the exam:

1. **Weekly: one full Paper 3 under timed conditions.** Use past papers from the HKEAA. Do the *whole* paper including the integrated writing — partial practice teaches the wrong habits about time. Mark it against the official marking scheme and log every point you missed and *why* (mis-heard? not in notes? wrong genre?).
2. **Daily (15–20 min): active listening.** English podcasts, news, or lectures with deliberate note-taking using your shorthand system — then summarise what you heard in 3 written sentences. This builds the exact muscle Paper 3 tests.
3. **Weekly: one timed Group Interaction** with classmates, ideally rotating who is "quiet" or "dominant" so you practise managing both. Record it; review for airtime balance and how often you brought others in.
4. **Decide your Section 2 vs Section 3 default by your second mock**, and from then on practise only that pairing — but do one or two of the *other* section so you can switch on the day if needed.
5. **Build genre templates** for report, proposal, letter, article, and speech. Internalise the layout and register so structure is automatic and your effort goes into content.
6. **Lock numbers and names.** In every Paper 3 you practise, separately grade your accuracy on figures, dates, and proper nouns — these are the highest-frequency, easiest-to-recover marks.

## Common mistakes that cost grades

- **Choosing Section 2 when targeting Level 5+** — you cap your own grade before you start.
- **Listening passively** and trying to write the integrated task from memory instead of from structured notes.
- **Ignoring the Data File** and answering only from the audio (or vice versa) — the marks are deliberately split across both sources.
- **Wrong genre/register** in the integrated task — a perfect "essay" when a "report" was required loses easy structural marks.
- **Dominating or going silent** in Group Interaction — both extremes are penalised; balanced, responsive participation is the target.
- **Memorising speeches** for Paper 4 — examiners detect this immediately and it destroys the interaction marks.

## Bottom line

Paper 3 and Paper 4 together are 40% of your HKDSE English grade and are the most *trainable* parts of the subject. The decisive moves are: commit to the right elective section for your target level, make note-taking and source-synthesis a daily habit rather than an exam-week scramble, and treat Speaking as a collaboration you facilitate rather than a contest you win. Candidates who internalise these habits months out consistently outperform stronger but technique-blind peers.
