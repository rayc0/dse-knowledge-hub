# Korea University Pathway for DSE Students: TOPIK, English-Taught Programmes, GKS Scholarship and D-2 Visa Guide 2026

Korea has rapidly climbed the wishlist of Hong Kong DSE graduates over the last five years. The K-culture wave is part of it, but the practical case is stronger: tuition at top private universities like Yonsei or Korea University runs roughly HK$60,000–90,000 per year, English-taught degree programmes have multiplied at the SKY universities (Seoul National, Korea, Yonsei), the Global Korea Scholarship covers full tuition plus a stipend, and the post-graduation D-10 job-seeking visa lets you stay on for up to two years to find work. From 2025 onward the HKEAA added Korean as a Category B Other Language subject in the DSE — a signal that the bilateral student pipeline is now formally recognised.

This guide walks through both application tracks (English-medium and Korean-medium), TOPIK requirements, the SKY universities and rising alternatives, the GKS scholarship process, costs, the D-2 visa, and what happens after graduation.

## The two application tracks

Korean universities admit international undergraduates through a dedicated *foreign student* track, typically called 외국인특별전형 (foreign student special admission). The simplifying detail for DSE students: this track does not compete against Korean nationals taking the Suneung (CSAT) — you are evaluated on your high school record and language proficiency only.

There are then two routes within that track:

**Korean-medium degrees** — the bulk of programmes, taught entirely in Korean. You will need a TOPIK score (usually Level 3 or 4 minimum, Level 5 or 6 for competitive majors) by application time, or you can sometimes be conditionally admitted and complete a year at the university's Korean Language Centre first.

**English-medium degrees (English-Taught Programmes, ETP)** — a smaller but high-quality set of programmes where instruction is in English. You submit IELTS or TOEFL instead of TOPIK. Most ETPs sit in business, international studies, engineering, and life sciences.

Pick the track first, because it dictates your two-year preparation plan. If you have any meaningful Korean already, the Korean-medium track opens vastly more programmes; if you don't, the ETP track is realistic but narrower.

## Top universities and what they want

### The SKY universities

**Seoul National University (SNU)** — the prestige choice and the hardest to enter. Foreign student admission is highly competitive, with most applicants holding strong AP / IB / A-Level results in addition to DSE. For Korean-medium programmes, expect to need TOPIK Level 5–6 in practice (though Level 3 is the formal minimum for some colleges). English-taught options are limited to a handful of programmes, including the College of Liberal Studies and parts of the College of Engineering and Business.

**Korea University (KU)** — strong international intake and a well-developed Division of International Studies (DIS) taught entirely in English. KU's foreign student track typically requires TOPIK Level 4 or higher for Korean-medium admission (Level 3 minimum, but Level 4 is more competitive). The Korea University Business School (KUBS) and DIS are the two flagship English-taught entry points.

**Yonsei University** — runs the Underwood International College (UIC), one of the largest English-taught liberal arts programmes in Asia. UIC has multiple admission rounds, accepts DSE results directly, and has a broad curriculum spanning humanities, social sciences, life sciences, and quantitative economics. Outside UIC, Yonsei's main campus also offers the foreign student track for Korean-medium degrees.

### Strong second-tier options

**Sungkyunkwan University (SKKU)** — top-ranked in engineering and business, partnered with Samsung. Has substantial English-taught content in business, computing, and natural sciences.

**Hanyang University** — engineering powerhouse, strong industry links, generous foreign student scholarships.

**Ewha Womans University** — long-standing reputation, large international division (Scranton College and Division of International Studies), generous needs-based aid.

**KAIST and POSTECH** — Korea's MIT and Caltech equivalents. KAIST teaches almost everything in English at undergraduate level and is essentially free for admitted international students through the KAIST International Scholarship. Highly selective in mathematics, physics, and engineering; expect strong DSE Maths Extended Module results to be persuasive.

**Chung-Ang, Konkuk, Kyung Hee, Hongik** — solid mid-tier private universities, more accessible TOPIK thresholds, popular with Hong Kong students aiming for media, design, hospitality, or arts programmes.

## TOPIK: what level and when

The TOPIK (Test of Proficiency in Korean) has six levels divided into two papers:

- **TOPIK I** — covers Levels 1–2 (beginner). Not enough for degree admission.
- **TOPIK II** — covers Levels 3–6. Level 3 is conversational; Level 6 is near-native.

For most foreign student admissions, the formal minimum is TOPIK Level 3, with Level 4 considered competitive and Level 5–6 expected for top programmes in humanities, law, or business at SKY-level universities. Some universities accept conditional admission with Level 2 if you commit to a year at their Korean Language Centre before starting your degree — useful if your Korean isn't there yet but you want to lock in a place.

Test logistics: TOPIK is held roughly six times a year, with sessions in Hong Kong (usually Hong Kong Korean International School or designated centres). Register through the official TOPIK Hong Kong portal at least two months ahead. Results take about a month. Build your timeline so you have a usable score *before* the application deadline, not after — universities don't wait.

If you're starting from zero in S5, a realistic path is one year of Korean classes (Sejong Institute, Korean Cultural Centre, or self-study) to reach Level 2, then intensive prep through S6 to push to Level 3 or 4 before final-year applications. Aiming higher than Level 4 from a cold start in two years is ambitious but feasible if you can do a summer language course in Korea between S5 and S6.

## English-taught route: IELTS, TOEFL and what's substitutable

For ETP admissions, most universities ask for one of:

- IELTS 6.0–6.5 (some flagship programmes ask 7.0)
- TOEFL iBT 79–90
- DSE English Language Level 4 or 5 — increasingly accepted in lieu of IELTS at Korea University, Yonsei UIC, and several others, but always check the current admission guide for your target programme.

DSE English at Level 5 typically maps to IELTS 6.5–7.0 for these admission purposes. If your DSE English is solid, you may not need to sit IELTS at all — but confirm this in writing before relying on it.

## Application timing

Korean universities have two intakes:

- **Spring intake** — March start. Applications open from August/September of the previous year and close around October–November.
- **Fall intake** — September start. Applications open from March–April and close around May–June.

Most DSE students target the **Fall intake of the year they sit DSE** (apply March–June, results released July, start September). Some students with stronger overseas paperwork target the following Spring (extra prep time, but six months of gap to fill).

Many universities have multiple application rounds within an intake (Round 1 / Round 2 / Round 3) — earlier rounds usually have more places and slightly easier thresholds. If you're confident, apply early.

## Documents you'll need

The standard foreign student application bundle for Korean universities:

1. Online application form
2. DSE results (predicted grades for early-round applications, plus official statement of results when available — HKEAA can issue verified result statements)
3. Full secondary school transcripts (S4–S6, often with school stamp and a sealed-envelope certified version)
4. Certificate of Graduation or expected graduation letter
5. Language proficiency — TOPIK (Korean track) or IELTS/TOEFL/DSE English (English track)
6. Personal statement / Study plan (1–2 pages, in Korean or English depending on track)
7. Letter of recommendation (usually one from a teacher or principal)
8. Passport copy (yours and both parents')
9. Family relationship documents — proving both you and your parents are non-Korean citizens
10. Financial documents — bank statement, sponsor's income proof, often showing roughly USD 18,000–20,000 in liquid funds for one year
11. Application fee — around USD 80–130 per university

Note the *both parents non-Korean* requirement: if either parent holds Korean citizenship, you generally cannot apply through the foreign student track and must compete in the regular admission with Suneung. This is a hard rule at most universities.

## Global Korea Scholarship (GKS)

The GKS is the Korean government's flagship scholarship, fully funding 280 undergraduate students per year (2026 cycle) from 71 countries. Hong Kong is eligible.

**What it covers:**
- Full tuition (up to a generous cap)
- One year of Korean language training at a designated language institution before starting the degree
- Monthly stipend of around KRW 900,000 (~HK$5,400)
- Round-trip airfare
- Settlement and research allowances
- Korean medical insurance

**Headline 2026 eligibility:**
- Under 25 years old at time of entry (born after a cut-off date in 2001 for the 2026 cycle — verify against the current call)
- Completed or about to complete secondary school by 31 December 2025
- Top 20% of class, or CGPA above 80% on your school's scale
- Both you and your parents non-Korean citizens
- Sound health

**How to apply:** GKS undergraduate applications open through the Study in Korea website (studyinkorea.go.kr) typically in September of the year before entry. There are two tracks: *Embassy track* (apply via the Korean embassy or consulate in your country — Hong Kong applicants apply via the Consulate General of the Republic of Korea in Hong Kong) and *University track* (apply directly through a designated university). The embassy track has more flexibility; the university track is faster.

The GKS is highly competitive — more so than direct admission to most universities — but the financial package transforms the cost equation completely.

### Other scholarships worth chasing

- **University-internal foreign student scholarships** — almost every university (Korea, Yonsei, SNU, KAIST, Hanyang, etc.) offers tuition reductions of 30–100% based on academic merit. These are awarded automatically with admission at most schools; check each university's foreign student admission guide.
- **KAIST International Scholarship** — full tuition + stipend for admitted international undergraduates at KAIST.
- **Posco TJ Park Foundation Scholarship** — for Asian students; competitive.
- **Korean Government Self-Finance Scholarship** — partial support, lower bar than GKS.

## Costs: tuition and living

### Tuition (per year, 2026 estimates)

- **National universities (SNU, Pusan National, KAIST, etc.)** — KRW 4,000,000–10,000,000 (~HK$23,000–58,000) for humanities/social sciences; engineering and natural sciences higher.
- **Private universities (Yonsei, Korea, SKKU, Hanyang)** — KRW 8,000,000–16,000,000 (~HK$46,000–93,000) for most programmes; medical and dental significantly higher and largely closed to foreigners.
- **English-taught flagship programmes** — typically priced at the higher end of the private university range.

For comparison, a self-financed local degree in Hong Kong runs roughly HK$120,000–180,000 a year — Korea is meaningfully cheaper even at private universities.

### Living costs in Seoul

Realistic monthly budget for a student living in Seoul:

- Accommodation: KRW 400,000–700,000 (~HK$2,300–4,100) for a goshiwon or shared one-room; on-campus dorm is cheaper at around KRW 200,000–400,000
- Food: KRW 300,000–450,000 (~HK$1,750–2,600)
- Transport: KRW 60,000 (~HK$350) for a transit pass
- National Health Insurance: KRW 40,000–55,000 (mandatory after six months)
- Phone, internet, laundry, social: KRW 200,000–300,000

Total roughly KRW 1,000,000–1,500,000 per month (~HK$5,800–8,700). Outside Seoul (Busan, Daejeon, Daegu), shave 20–30% off.

Annual all-in cost for a self-funded student at a private Seoul university: roughly HK$200,000–270,000 a year. With GKS or a major university scholarship, this can drop to nearly zero.

## D-2 student visa

Once you have an admission letter you apply for the **D-2 student visa** at the Consulate General of the Republic of Korea in Hong Kong.

**Requirements:**
- Standard Admission Letter from the Korean university
- Proof of financial capacity — at least KRW 20,000,000 (~HK$120,000) in your or sponsor's bank account, demonstrated through a recent bank certificate (some embassies want the funds parked for several months)
- Passport with at least six months remaining
- Visa application form, photo
- Application fee — around USD 80 (single entry) or USD 120 (multiple entry)
- Sometimes: chest X-ray (TB screening), depending on the year and programme

**Processing time:** typically 2–4 weeks. Apply at least six weeks before your intended departure; longer if you also need to finalise dormitory paperwork.

**Work rights on D-2:** after six months of legal stay, you can apply for permission to work part-time — up to 20 hours per week during semester, unlimited during vacations. You'll need a TOPIK level requirement (Level 3 for most jobs, Level 4 for some) and approval from your university's international office.

## Living and studying in Korea: what DSE students should know

**Dorm vs off-campus:** Dorms are cheaper but have curfews and roommate placements. Off-campus *one-room* (원룸) studios near campus run KRW 500,000–800,000 with a key money deposit (보증금) of KRW 5–10 million — the deposit is returned at the end. Many students use *goshiwon* or *goshitel* for the first semester until they understand the rental market.

**Weather and clothing:** Seoul winters are seriously cold (regularly -10°C to -15°C with windchill). Hong Kong students consistently underbudget for winter clothing — plan HK$3,000–5,000 for a proper coat, boots, and layers in your first year.

**Healthcare:** National Health Insurance enrolment is mandatory after six months of stay. Coverage is good and premiums are reasonable. Carry your Alien Registration Card (ARC) for clinic visits.

**Banking:** Open a Korean bank account in your first month — most universities will help with this through partnered banks (KB, Woori, Shinhan). You'll need it for stipend, part-time wages, and rent.

**Korean society:** Hierarchical workplace and university norms (선배/후배 senior-junior dynamics) take time. Drinking culture is real but easier to navigate than reputation suggests — you can decline politely without social cost in most international-friendly settings.

## After graduation: the D-10 and beyond

Korea has been actively expanding post-study work options:

**D-10 job-seeking visa** — up to two years to find graduate-level employment. Eligibility hinges on graduating from an accredited Korean university with a points-based threshold (academic record, language ability, age, university ranking).

**E-7 specialised employment visa** — for graduates who land a professional job. Increasingly common for foreign graduates in IT, biotech, design, and language-related fields.

**F-2-7 long-term residence (points-based)** — accumulate enough points (income, Korean ability, education) over your D-10/E-7 period and you can transition to a renewable long-term residence permit, then eventually to F-5 permanent residence.

For Hong Kong students who learn Korean well, the post-graduation labour market in Seoul has steadily opened — Naver, Kakao, Coupang, the major chaebol's international divisions, and the growing English-content industries all hire foreign graduates.

## How does Korea compare to other overseas pathways?

- **Versus Japan** — Korea is more language-front-loaded (TOPIK is harder than EJU's Japanese paper for cold starters in some respects, though both are demanding). Korean private university tuition is lower than top Japanese privates. Job-seeking visa is more generous in Korea (2 years vs Japan's 1 year typical).
- **Versus Taiwan** — Taiwan is cheaper still (NTU tuition is HK$15,000–25,000/year), but Korea's brand recognition for global employment is stronger.
- **Versus Singapore / UK / US** — Korea is dramatically cheaper than all three. Brand prestige from SKY universities is regionally strong but doesn't yet match Oxbridge or Ivy globally.
- **Versus staying in HK** — for self-financed JUPAS students paying HK$120,000+ at lower-banded local programmes, a top Korean private with a partial scholarship can be cheaper *and* offer a different career trajectory.

## A 24-month plan if you start in S5

**S5 first semester (Sep–Dec):** Decide track (Korean-medium vs ETP). If Korean-medium, start lessons immediately — Sejong Institute, Korean Cultural Centre Hong Kong, or online (Talk To Me In Korean, How To Study Korean). Target TOPIK I Level 2 by year end.

**S5 second semester (Jan–Jun):** Push toward TOPIK II Level 3 prep. Sit IELTS by spring if going ETP. Shortlist 5–8 universities, read each foreign student admission guide carefully.

**Summer between S5 and S6:** Korean language summer programme in Seoul if budget allows (Yonsei KLI, Sogang KLEC, Korea University KLC). Hugely effective for jumping a TOPIK level. Visit campuses while there.

**S6 first semester (Sep–Dec):** Sit TOPIK in October or November to lock in a usable score. Draft personal statements. Approach teachers for recommendation letters early — don't wait until December. Submit GKS application in September if pursuing it.

**S6 second semester (Jan–Apr):** Submit Spring-after-DSE university applications (mostly fall-intake deadlines fall here). Sit DSE in March–April. Confirm offers as they arrive.

**Post-DSE (May–Aug):** Final document submission, official DSE result statements, accept offer, apply for D-2 visa, arrange dormitory or housing, fly out for August orientation.

## Final thoughts

Korea sits in a sweet spot for DSE students who want a strong overseas degree without the UK or US price tag, who are open to learning Korean (or are happy with one of the well-developed English-taught programmes), and who see East Asian career markets as viable. The infrastructure for international students is mature, the scholarships are real, and the post-graduation visa lets you actually use your degree on the ground. The hardest part is the language commitment for Korean-medium programmes — start early, take it seriously, and the rest of the pathway is more straightforward than it looks from Hong Kong.
