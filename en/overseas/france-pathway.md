# France Pathway for DSE Students: Études en France, English-Taught Programmes, Tuition and VLS-TS Visa Guide 2026

France has historically been an under-utilised destination for Hong Kong DSE students compared to the UK or Australia, but the pathway has matured significantly in recent years. Public universities still charge a fraction of what UK or US institutions charge, the number of English-taught Bachelor's and Master's programmes has grown substantially, and a 12-month post-study job-seeker permit makes France realistic as a launch pad for a European career — not just a degree destination.

This guide walks through the application architecture (Campus France, Études en France, and Parcoursup), tuition realities at public universities versus grandes écoles, the VLS-TS student visa now processed through TLS Connect for Hong Kong applicants, and what the post-study RÉCE permit (formerly APS) means in practice for DSE students who want to stay and work after graduation.

## Why France for DSE Students

Three structural reasons make France worth a serious look:

1. **Cost relative to research strength.** Even after the 2026 differentiated tuition reform, public university fees for non-EU undergraduates sit at roughly €2,895 per year — a fraction of UK or Irish fees, and inside the same range as some mainland Chinese university tuition. Grandes écoles like Sciences Po, HEC and École Polytechnique cost more, but national universities like Paris-Saclay, Sorbonne Université and Université Paris Cité rank among the world's top 100 in many disciplines while charging public-system fees.

2. **English-taught programme depth at Master's level.** Most Bachelor's programmes are still taught in French, which is the binding constraint for DSE leavers. But there are now over 1,500 English-taught programmes overall, and a critical mass of English-medium Bachelor's options — including Sciences Po's multilingual undergraduate college, BBA programmes at HEC, ESSEC and ESCP, and English-taught science Bachelor's at Paris-Saclay and Université Paris Cité.

3. **Post-study work rights.** Non-EU graduates with a Master's degree (or grandes écoles diploma) qualify for the *Recherche d'emploi / Création d'entreprise* (RÉCE) permit — 12 months of legal stay to find a job or launch a business, with a path onto the Talent Passport once a qualifying employment contract is in hand.

The trade-off is real: France is a French-speaking working environment. Even if you study in English, your day-to-day life, internships and post-study job hunt are dramatically easier with at least B1 French. Plan to learn the language seriously, not as an afterthought.

## DSE Recognition and Academic Requirements

Hong Kong DSE results are recognised across the French higher education system, but recognition does not equal automatic admission. Each institution sets its own minimum scores, and the level of selectivity varies enormously between a public university and a grande école.

A workable benchmark for competitive English-taught Bachelor's programmes:

- **Sciences Po Undergraduate College** — typically expects DSE candidates to present 5 strong subjects with 5\*\* / 5\* in Core English plus several elective 5s. The admissions process is holistic and includes an essay, video interview and academic transcript — not a hard cutoff.
- **Public university Bachelor's (Licence)** — most accept DSE 4-4-3-3-3 (Chinese, English, Mathematics, Liberal Studies/CSD, one elective) as meeting the *baccalauréat* equivalence threshold, but competitive disciplines such as medicine, law and engineering at Paris-Saclay or Sorbonne can require materially higher profiles.
- **Grandes écoles BBA programmes** (HEC, ESSEC, ESCP, EM Lyon, EDHEC) — typically require strong English (IELTS 6.5+ or TOEFL 90+), competitive DSE profile (level 5 in English plus several level 5 electives), plus interview and admission test (SAT, GMAT Focus or in-house exam depending on school).

If your DSE result is weaker, the *Année préparatoire* or foundation-year pathway exists at several public universities and at French language schools, but these almost always require French at B2 by entry — they are not English-medium fallback options.

## Application Architecture: Campus France, EEF and Parcoursup

This is where most DSE applicants get confused. France has three overlapping platforms, and Hong Kong students do not use all of them.

### Études en France (EEF) — your main pathway

Hong Kong is one of the ~70 countries covered by the **Études en France** (EEF) procedure, run by Campus France Hong Kong. If you hold an HKID or Macau ID and are applying to a public university, a school of art or architecture, or most engineering schools, **EEF is mandatory** — you cannot apply directly to the institution.

The EEF cycle for September 2026 entry:

- **1 October 2025**: EEF platform opens for applications
- **15 December 2025**: deadline to submit
- **End of January – early March 2026**: Campus France Hong Kong academic interview
- **30 April 2026**: institutions must give a final decision
- **From May 2026**: visa application phase begins

The EEF application fee is **HKD 1,800**, paid through Alliance Française de Hong Kong. It is non-refundable, even if no admission offer is received.

The Campus France academic interview is a defining feature of the French process: a 20–30 minute conversation (in English or French depending on programme) about your academic project, why France, why this institution, and how you intend to fund yourself. It does not determine admission directly — universities make that call — but it produces an evaluation that institutions weigh, and it gates your visa later.

### Parcoursup — usually not for you

**Parcoursup** is the national platform for first-year higher education in France, used primarily by French *lycéens*. International students from EEF countries — including Hong Kong DSE candidates applying to most public universities — go through EEF, not Parcoursup.

Where Parcoursup matters for DSE students: certain *prépa* (classes préparatoires aux grandes écoles), some BTS programmes, and a small number of first-year programmes at specific institutions. Even in those cases, you typically still need a Campus France pre-consular procedure. Treat Parcoursup as an information catalogue (it lists ~25,000 programmes) rather than your primary application channel.

### Direct application — for grandes écoles and private schools

Sciences Po, HEC, ESSEC, ESCP, INSEAD (Master's only) and most private business schools run their own admission processes. You apply directly on the school's portal, sit their tests and interviews, and receive an offer — *then* you enter EEF afterwards as a "dossier blanc" (already-admitted candidate) to get the Campus France certificate needed for your visa.

For grandes écoles, the timeline runs earlier: Sciences Po's main international admission deadline for September 2026 entry was already in late February 2026. If you are reading this for a 2027 entry, plan around an October–February application window for selective schools.

## English-Taught Programme Landscape

For DSE students entering directly after Form 6 without strong French, the English-taught Bachelor's universe is the realistic option. The main clusters:

- **Sciences Po Undergraduate College** — three-year Bachelor's across seven regional campuses, with English-language tracks at Le Havre (Asia–Pacific focus), Reims (North America/Europe–Africa) and Menton (Mediterranean and Middle East). Year three is spent abroad. This is one of the strongest fits for DSE humanities/social-science profiles.
- **American University of Paris** — fully English-language, US-style liberal-arts curriculum in central Paris.
- **Public university English-taught Bachelor's** — Paris-Saclay's Bachelor in Mathematics and Computer Science, Université Paris Cité's Bachelor in Life Sciences, Sorbonne's Bachelor in Mathematics or Physics, and several at Université Côte d'Azur (data science, biology, economics).
- **Grandes écoles BBA / engineering Bachelor's** — IÉSEG, NEOMA, Skema, EDHEC, EMLV (business); Polytechnique's Bachelor of Science in mathematics/physics/economics; ECE, EFREI and EPITA (engineering, partly English-taught).

English requirements are typically IELTS 6.5 (Academic) or TOEFL iBT 90+. Sciences Po, HEC and elite schools push that to IELTS 7.0+. DSE Core English at level 5 or above is generally accepted in lieu of IELTS by many but not all institutions — always confirm in writing before relying on it.

## Tuition: Public Universities vs Grandes Écoles

The 2026/27 cycle marks an important shift. From September 2026, public universities in France are directed to charge non-EU/EEA students differentiated fees:

- **Bachelor's (Licence)**: ~€2,895 per year
- **Master's**: ~€3,941 per year
- **Doctorate**: ~€380 per year (largely unchanged)

These are still substantially below most UK, US, Australian or Singaporean fees. And there is an important exception: **several major universities — notably Université Paris-Saclay and Sorbonne Université — partially exempt non-EU students**, bringing their fees back down to the same level as French students (around €170 Bachelor's / €243 Master's). Université Paris Cité, Université Lyon 1 and others run similar exemption schemes. This is not a scholarship you apply for — it is automatic for admitted non-EU students, and it materially changes the cost calculus.

Grandes écoles and private schools are a different planet:

- **Sciences Po**: €0 to €14,900 per year for Bachelor's, €0 to €20,640 for Master's, on a sliding scale based on parental household income. Hong Kong families generally fall into the upper tiers.
- **HEC, ESSEC, ESCP BBA**: roughly €15,000–€18,000 per year.
- **HEC, ESSEC, ESCP MIM (Master in Management)**: €40,000–€55,000 across the programme.
- **Polytechnique Bachelor of Science**: €14,800 per year.
- **Engineering grandes écoles** (Centrale, Mines, Ponts): typically €5,000–€15,000 per year for non-EU students.

Living costs sit at roughly **€10,000–€14,000 per year outside Paris** and **€14,000–€18,000 per year in Paris**. Visa rules require you to demonstrate at least €615 per month (€7,380 per year) in available funds — that is the floor, not a realistic budget.

## VLS-TS Student Visa via TLS Connect

Once you have an admission letter and have passed the Campus France final clearance, you apply for a long-stay student visa — the **VLS-TS étudiant** (Visa de Long Séjour valant Titre de Séjour). This visa serves as both your entry visa and your residence permit for your first year, removing the need for a separate prefecture appointment immediately on arrival.

Since **27 October 2025**, the Consulate General of France in Hong Kong and Macau has used **TLS Connect** for visa appointments, replacing the previous direct consular system. The flow:

1. Receive your admission and Campus France clearance.
2. Create a France-Visas account online and complete the visa form.
3. Book an appointment via TLS Connect for biometric submission.
4. Submit documents in person: passport (6+ months validity), admission letter, Campus France certificate, financial proof (€615/month minimum), accommodation evidence, civil status documents, photos and visa fee (around €99).
5. Visa decision typically issued within 2–4 weeks.

Within **3 months of arrival in France**, you must validate your VLS-TS online via the OFII (now ANEF) portal and pay the residency tax (around €60). This step is non-negotiable — failing to validate makes you irregular even with a valid visa sticker.

Begin the visa process at least three months before your programme start date; for September 2026 entry, target a June application.

## Working During Studies

Non-EU students on a VLS-TS may work up to **964 hours per year** (roughly 60% of full-time, or ~20 hours per week during term plus full-time during holidays). The minimum wage (SMIC) sits at around €11.88 per hour gross, so a student working close to the cap can offset around €8,000–€9,000 of annual costs.

Internships are common and often a course requirement, especially in business and engineering schools. Paid internships above two months must pay at least 15% of the social security ceiling per hour (around €4.35/hour in 2026 — this is *gratification*, not a salary). Internship hours generally do not count against the 964-hour cap, but you must have a *convention de stage* (tripartite internship agreement).

## Post-Study: From RÉCE to Talent Passport

This is where France compares well against Germany and the Netherlands but lags the UK Graduate Route in absolute duration. The pathway:

### Step 1 — RÉCE (formerly APS): 12 months job-seeker / business creator permit

The *Autorisation Provisoire de Séjour* was renamed **Recherche d'Emploi ou Création d'Entreprise (RÉCE)** in 2024. It grants 12 months of legal stay to find a job matching your qualifications or to launch a business. Key conditions:

- You must hold a Master's degree (Bac+5), a *licence professionnelle*, a DUT/BUT followed by 12+ months of salaried work during studies, or a *titre d'ingénieur* / grandes écoles degree.
- You must apply at the prefecture before your student permit expires, or at a French consulate within 4 years of graduating if you have already left France.
- The permit is **valid for 12 months and not renewable**.

During the RÉCE period, you may work up to 964 hours (the same student cap) until you sign a qualifying employment contract.

### Step 2 — Talent Passport

If you sign an employment contract with a gross annual salary above **€36,509.20** (this threshold is updated periodically), you can switch to the multi-year *Passeport Talent* — typically four years, renewable, with family rights and a clear path toward a 10-year resident card.

Below that salary threshold, you can still get a standard *salarié* permit, but it is shorter and tied to the specific employer.

### Bachelor's-only graduates

DSE students who complete *only* a Bachelor's at a public university and do not continue to a Master's are not eligible for RÉCE. The most common strategies in this case:

1. **Continue to Master's in France** — the most common path, since Master's tuition is also low at public universities.
2. **Continue to Master's elsewhere in Europe** — France's Bachelor's is widely accepted across the EU and Switzerland.
3. **Return to Hong Kong / mainland with European credentials** — French Bachelor's degrees are well-recognised in MNCs operating in Greater China, particularly in luxury, hospitality, finance and engineering.

## Realistic Timeline for September 2026 Entry

Working backwards from September 2026:

- **March – August 2025**: shortlist programmes; begin French study (target B1 by April 2026 even for English-taught programmes).
- **September 2025**: prepare personal statement, IELTS/TOEFL test, transcripts.
- **1 October 2025**: EEF opens; create account, fill profile, pay HKD 1,800 fee.
- **15 December 2025**: EEF submission deadline.
- **Late January – February 2026**: Campus France academic interview.
- **March – April 2026**: institution decisions; for grandes écoles, parallel application processes likely already concluded.
- **April – May 2026**: confirm offer, pay deposits, secure accommodation (CROUS, private student housing or homestay).
- **May – June 2026**: book TLS Connect appointment, submit VLS-TS visa application.
- **August 2026**: visa issued; book travel.
- **September 2026**: arrive, register, validate VLS-TS within 3 months.

## Who France Suits — and Who It Doesn't

**Strong fit:**

- DSE students aiming for social sciences, humanities, design, fashion, hospitality, mathematics, theoretical physics or fundamental engineering, where French universities are world-class and tuition is low.
- Students willing to invest seriously in French (target B2 within two years) and prepared for a French-language working life after graduation.
- Cost-conscious families who want a research-active European destination but cannot stretch to UK or US private fees.

**Weak fit:**

- Students seeking the shortest possible path to a high-paying tech job — the UK Graduate Route, Ireland's Stamp 1G or German Blue Card pathways are typically faster and friendlier for English-monolingual graduates.
- Students unwilling to learn French. You can survive a Master's in English in Paris, but your career options will be heavily constrained.
- Students whose DSE is borderline and who want a non-selective entry — most public universities are not "soft" on minimum requirements at the dossier-review stage, and EEF rejections after the academic interview do happen.

France rewards students who plan early, take French seriously, and target programmes with genuine fit rather than chasing prestige. Done right, it offers a degree at a fraction of UK or US cost, plus a 12-month runway to convert that degree into a European career — a combination few other destinations match in 2026.
