# Japan University Pathway for DSE Students: EJU, English-Taught Programmes, MEXT Scholarship and Visa Guide 2026

Japan has quietly become one of the most attractive overseas destinations for Hong Kong DSE graduates. National university tuition is roughly HK$30,000 a year — cheaper than local self-financed degrees in Hong Kong. English-taught programmes at Waseda, Keio, Sophia, and Tokyo have grown rapidly. Post-graduation work visas are generous, and the salary outlook in Tokyo for bilingual graduates is competitive with Singapore. Yet the application path is unfamiliar to most DSE students — there are two parallel admission systems (Japanese-medium via EJU, English-medium via direct application), and the documentation sits somewhere between the UCAS-style system and the US essay-driven model.

This guide breaks down everything a DSE student needs: the two application tracks, the EJU exam, top English-taught programmes, MEXT and JASSO scholarships, costs, visa procedure, and what happens after graduation.

## The Two Admission Tracks

Japanese universities admit international undergraduates through two largely separate channels. Choose one before you build your timeline — they have different exams, different deadlines, and different language requirements.

**Track 1: Japanese-medium degree (via EJU).** This is the traditional path. You sit the Examination for Japanese University Admission for International Students (EJU), and most universities then call you for a written test, interview, or both. Instruction is in Japanese, so you typically need JLPT N2 or above by enrolment. Cheapest option (especially at national universities), broadest programme choice, and strongest integration into Japanese society — but the language barrier is real.

**Track 2: English-medium degree (direct application).** A growing list of programmes — most famously Waseda's School of International Liberal Studies (SILS), Keio's PEARL and GIGA, Sophia's FLA, Tokyo's PEAK, Kyoto's iUP, and Osaka's Human Sciences — teach the entire degree in English. You apply directly with TOEFL/IELTS, SAT, transcripts, essays, and reference letters, much like a US application. No Japanese language requirement at entry (though courses are often available alongside the degree). More expensive, but accessible to DSE students with no Japanese background.

Most DSE students should target Track 2 unless they have already reached JLPT N3 by Form 6, in which case Track 1 with another year of language school becomes realistic.

## Track 1: The EJU Exam Explained

The EJU is run by the Japan Student Services Organization (JASSO) twice a year — June and November — at exam centres including Hong Kong. The 2026 second session application window is 6–30 July, with the test itself in November. The test is structured around four subjects:

- **Japanese as a Foreign Language** (compulsory for all Japanese-medium applicants; tests reading, listening, listening-reading, and writing)
- **Science** (Physics, Chemistry, Biology — choose two; required for science/engineering programmes)
- **Japan and the World** (a social-studies paper covering Japanese geography, history, politics, economics, and contemporary issues; required for arts/humanities/social-science programmes)
- **Mathematics** (Course 1 for arts/humanities, Course 2 for science/engineering)

Subjects other than Japanese can be sat in either Japanese or English, which sounds helpful but is misleading: nearly every selective Japanese-medium programme requires you to sit them in Japanese, because the receiving department needs evidence you can study in Japanese. Revised syllabuses for Science, Japan and the World, and Mathematics apply from the 2026 first session, so use up-to-date past papers from the JASSO website rather than older Chinese-language prep books.

After the EJU, top universities (Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, Hitotsubashi, Tsukuba) hold a second-stage internal exam and interview between January and March. This is where competition really happens — EJU scores get you into the pool, but the secondary screening decides the outcome.

A practical timeline for a DSE student going Track 1 is two years: complete DSE in May, enrol in a Japanese language school (語学学校) in Tokyo or Osaka in October, sit JLPT N3 in December and N2 the following July, sit EJU in June and November of that gap year, then apply to universities for an April or September entry the year after.

## Track 2: English-Taught Undergraduate Programmes

The English-medium track has expanded enormously over the past decade. Below are the programmes most relevant for DSE students.

**Waseda University — School of International Liberal Studies (SILS).** Waseda's flagship English-taught programme, with around 600 international students. Liberal-arts curriculum, strong in international relations, business, and cultural studies. Application requires SAT or equivalent, TOEFL 95+ or IELTS 7.0+, two recommendation letters, and an essay. Annual tuition around ¥1.6m (~HK$83,000). Two intakes per year (April and September).

**Keio University — PEARL (Economics) and GIGA (Policy Management / Environment).** Keio is Japan's most prestigious private university for business and economics. PEARL is the English-taught Economics degree at Mita campus. GIGA programmes sit at the Shonan Fujisawa campus and lean toward technology, policy, and design. Tuition ¥1.5–1.7m. SAT or A-Levels accepted; TOEFL 90+ or IELTS 7.0+.

**Sophia University — Faculty of Liberal Arts (FLA).** Catholic university in central Tokyo. FLA is one of Japan's oldest English-taught programmes, with majors in international business, social studies, and comparative culture. Smaller and more pastoral than Waseda. Tuition ~¥1.4m.

**University of Tokyo — PEAK (Programs in English at Komaba).** Two tracks: International Programme on Japan in East Asia, and International Programme on Environmental Sciences. Highly selective — comparable to HKU Medicine in admit rate. SAT 1450+, TOEFL 100+, two strong references, research-flavoured personal statement. As a national university, tuition is only ¥535,800 (~HK$28,000) per year. September intake.

**Kyoto University — iUP (International Undergraduate Program).** Six-month preparatory term followed by a regular Japanese-medium degree, with strong scholarship support (full tuition waiver plus monthly stipend for ~10 students per cycle). Suits academically strong DSE students willing to learn Japanese intensively in their first year. National university tuition.

**Other strong options.** Osaka University Human Sciences (English degree), Akita International University (full-English liberal arts in regional Japan, generous scholarships), Ritsumeikan APU in Beppu (truly bilingual campus, ~50% international students), Tohoku University FGL (science and engineering in English), Nagoya University G30 programmes (engineering, science, humanities).

Application materials for Track 2 typically include: high school transcript (DSE predicted grades for early rounds, actual results after July), DSE certificate or HKEAA result slip, TOEFL/IELTS score, SAT or ACT for top universities, two reference letters, a personal statement of 800–1,500 words, and sometimes a recorded video interview. Deadlines cluster between October and February for the September intake.

## DSE Grade Conversion and Subject Requirements

Most Japanese universities accept the HKDSE as a recognised secondary qualification without needing additional foundation years. There is no fixed conversion, but a working benchmark from past admits:

- **Top tier (Tokyo PEAK, Kyoto iUP, Waseda SILS competitive majors):** 5*5* in core plus 5* in two electives, equivalent to Best Five 32–35.
- **Selective private universities (Keio PEARL, Sophia FLA, Waseda SILS standard):** Best Five 25–30, with strong English (Level 5 or above) and consistent humanities/economics electives.
- **Regional national universities and APU-style programmes:** Best Five 20–24 with English Level 4 and a clear motivation letter.

Mathematics M1/M2 is a strong signal for economics, engineering, and PEAK applications. For SILS and FLA, depth in English Language and a humanities elective (History, Geography, Economics, Liberal Studies/Citizenship) matters more than the science subjects.

## MEXT and JASSO Scholarships

Japan funds international students more generously than most assume. The two main schemes for DSE graduates are:

**MEXT Scholarship (Embassy Recommendation).** Run by Japan's Ministry of Education through the Consulate-General of Japan in Hong Kong, administered in partnership with the Hong Kong Working Family and Student Financial Assistance Agency (WFSFAA). Covers full tuition, return airfare, and a monthly stipend of around ¥117,000 for undergraduates. Eligibility: HKID and HKSAR passport holder, born on or after 2 April 2001, completion of Form 6, with strong academic results. Application is once a year (typically May–June for the following April entry), followed by a written test and interview. Highly competitive — usually fewer than ten Hong Kong undergraduates selected per year — but worth attempting.

**JASSO Honors Scholarship.** ¥48,000 per month for international students already enrolled in Japan, awarded after the first year based on academic performance. Most international students at major universities receive this for at least part of their degree.

**University-specific scholarships.** Waseda, Keio, Sophia, APU, Akita International, and most national universities run additional tuition-waiver and stipend programmes. APU in particular awards 30%, 50%, 65%, 80%, or 100% tuition waivers automatically based on application strength — no separate application required.

A student who lands MEXT effectively pays nothing for the degree. A student who lands a 50% APU waiver plus JASSO Honors brings the total cost close to studying at a self-financed Hong Kong programme.

## Costs and Cost-of-Living

Realistic cost ranges for 2026 entry, based on JASSO and consulate guidance:

**National universities (Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, Tohoku, Nagoya, etc.).** Tuition ¥535,800/year (~HK$28,000). Add admission fee ¥282,000 in the first year. Total tuition over four years: ~HK$155,000.

**Private universities (Waseda, Keio, Sophia, Rikkyo, ICU).** Tuition ¥1.4–1.7m/year (~HK$73,000–88,000). Engineering and medicine higher.

**Living costs.** Tokyo: ¥120,000–180,000/month all-in for student dorm, food, transport, and National Health Insurance. Kyoto, Osaka, Nagoya: ¥90,000–130,000/month. Regional cities (Sendai, Fukuoka, Beppu): ¥70,000–100,000/month. National Health Insurance for students is heavily subsidised at ¥1,500–2,000 per month.

**Total four-year budget.** A DSE student at a national university with a dorm room in a regional city: HK$500,000–650,000 all-in. At a Tokyo private university with no scholarship: HK$1.0–1.3m. Considerably cheaper than the UK (£140,000+) or US (US$280,000+) for a comparable academic profile.

## Student Visa and Certificate of Eligibility

Once you have an unconditional offer, the university applies on your behalf for a Certificate of Eligibility (CoE) from the Japanese immigration authority. Processing takes one to three months — apply early. Once the CoE arrives, you submit it with your passport, application form, photo, and offer letter to the Consulate-General of Japan in Hong Kong (visa fee ¥3,000–6,000) for the actual student visa, which normally takes 5–7 working days.

For 2026, financial proof has been tightened: applicants are expected to show a consistent banking history (typically three to six months of statements) demonstrating funds of at least ¥2,000,000 (~HK$104,000), or equivalent sponsor letters and fixed deposit certificates. Spotty bank statements with last-minute large deposits are flagged.

The student visa permits up to 28 hours per week of part-time work once you obtain a "permission to engage in activity other than that permitted under the status of residence previously granted" stamp at immigration on arrival. Hourly wages of ¥1,100–1,300 are typical in Tokyo, enough to offset food and transport but not tuition.

## After Graduation: Job-Hunting and Visa Conversion

Japan's "shukatsu" (job-hunting) cycle starts in March of your third year and typically ends with informal offers in spring of your fourth year. Major employers — banks, trading houses, consulting firms, tech — recruit international students aggressively, especially those with bilingual ability. Starting salaries for new graduates are ¥250,000–350,000/month (~HK$13,000–18,000), lower than Hong Kong nominally but with strong career progression and lower cost of living.

After graduation, you can convert your student visa to a "Designated Activities" visa for up to one year of continued job-hunting if needed, then to an "Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services" working visa once you have an offer. After ten years of residence (with five on a working visa), permanent residence becomes available; the highly skilled professional track can shorten this to as little as one year.

For a DSE student weighing pathways, Japan offers something distinctive: a top-30 global academic system, low tuition by developed-country standards, a clear post-graduation work pipeline, and a culture that — while different from Hong Kong — is geographically close (four hours by flight) and increasingly accessible in English. The two-track system rewards early planning. Decide which track fits, build the right test portfolio (EJU vs SAT/TOEFL), and begin researching specific programmes in Form 5 rather than after results day.
