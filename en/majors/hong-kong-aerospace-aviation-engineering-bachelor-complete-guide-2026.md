# Hong Kong Aerospace & Aviation Engineering Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Hong Kong is one of the world's busiest aviation hubs. Hong Kong International Airport handles more international cargo than any other airport on the planet, Cathay Pacific operates a major long-haul fleet, and the Three-Runway System has expanded capacity well into the next decade. Behind all of that sits a steady demand for engineers who can design, certify, maintain, and manage aircraft and aerospace systems. For DSE students who love aircraft, propulsion, and flight, two distinct degree pathways exist locally: **HKUST's Aerospace Engineering (JS5270)** and **PolyU's Aviation/Aeronautical Engineering (JS3140)**. They sound similar but train very different engineers. This guide breaks down both so you can choose with your eyes open.

## The Two Pathways at a Glance

| | HKUST JS5270 | PolyU JS3140 |
|---|---|---|
| Programme | Aerospace Engineering (under Dept. of Mechanical & Aerospace Engineering) | BEng (Hons) in Aeronautical Engineering (retitled from "Aviation Engineering" from 2026/27) |
| Streams | Single aerospace stream | Aeronautical Engineering / Air Transport Engineering |
| Duration | 4 years | 4 years |
| Funding | UGC-funded | UGC-funded |
| Orientation | Research- and design-driven, broad aerospace science | Industry- and operations-driven, aviation-focused |
| Best fit for | Aircraft/spacecraft design, R&D, postgraduate study | Airline engineering, MRO, aviation operations, licensing path |

Both are government-funded (UGC) four-year bachelor's degrees with the standard local tuition of around **HK$42,100 per year** for the 2025/26 intake (confirm the exact 2026/27 figure on each university's site). Both are competitive but not at the extreme cut-off of medicine or law.

## HKUST Aerospace Engineering (JS5270)

HKUST runs its aerospace track through the **Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering**. The programme is deliberately broad and science-heavy. Core topics include:

- **Aerodynamics and fluid mechanics** — how air flows over wings and bodies
- **Aircraft structures and materials** — composites, fatigue, and lightweight design
- **Propulsion** — jet engines, turbines, and emerging electric/hybrid propulsion
- **Flight dynamics and control** — stability, avionics, and guidance
- **Sustainable aviation** — emissions reduction, alternative fuels, and next-generation concepts

The teaching style leans toward fundamentals plus hands-on laboratory and project work, with a strong bridge into research. HKUST's engineering school consistently ranks among the world's top tier, and the department feeds students into MPhil/PhD programmes and into aerospace R&D roles, drones/UAV companies, and the broader mechanical engineering job market. Because the degree shares a backbone with mechanical engineering, graduates are not locked into aviation — they can pivot into automotive, energy, robotics, or general manufacturing.

**Who should pick HKUST:** students who enjoy maths and physics, want design and research depth, and may consider postgraduate study or work outside Hong Kong. If you want to *design* aircraft and systems rather than maintain a fleet, this is the stronger fit.

## PolyU Aeronautical Engineering (JS3140)

PolyU's scheme is the more vocational, aviation-industry-aligned route, and it is being **retitled to BEng (Hons) in Aeronautical Engineering effective 2026/27**. It admits under a broad scheme with two directions:

- **Aeronautical Engineering** — aircraft systems, maintenance engineering, airworthiness, structures, and avionics
- **Air Transport Engineering** — the operational and management side of running an airline/airport: logistics, safety, reliability, and air transport systems

A defining feature is industry integration: some subjects are **co-taught by PolyU academics and working aviation professionals**, and the programme builds in **summer internships, technical visits, and career talks**. PolyU has long-standing links with Cathay Pacific, HAECO (Hong Kong Aircraft Engineering Company), the Civil Aviation Department, and the Airport Authority. For students who want to walk into an airline or MRO (maintenance, repair, and overhaul) engineering role, this network matters.

PolyU's aviation programmes are also positioned around professional recognition and the licensing pathway — graduates aiming to become Licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineers (LAME) or to work toward chartered engineer status find the curriculum mapped closely to those goals. Always confirm the latest accreditation and licensing exemptions directly with PolyU, as these are periodically updated.

**Who should pick PolyU:** students who want to work *in the aviation industry* in Hong Kong — airlines, airport, MRO, regulators — and who value internships and a clear professional licensing route over research depth.

## DSE Entrance Requirements

Both programmes use JUPAS and require the standard general university entrance baseline plus a science/maths emphasis:

- **Core subjects:** Chinese Language and English Language at Level 3, Mathematics (Compulsory Part) at Level 2 (Level 3+ is realistically expected for a competitive application), and Citizenship and Social Development at "Attained."
- **Elective subjects:** Strong applicants typically present **Physics** plus a second science or Mathematics Extended Module (M1/M2). M2 is highly valued for an engineering degree because of the calculus content.
- **Competitive profile:** Both programmes generally admit students in roughly the **middle of the engineering band** — below electrical/computer engineering at the same university in some years, but admission scores fluctuate annually. Check the published median and lower-quartile scores on the JUPAS site and on aggregators before finalising your band order.

A practical subject combination for either pathway: **Physics + M2 + one of Chemistry / ICT / Economics**. Physics is effectively non-negotiable; without it your application is weak regardless of overall score.

For the latest year-by-year admission statistics, see the official JUPAS programme pages for [HKUST JS5270](https://www.jupas.edu.hk/en/programme/hkust/JS5270/) and [PolyU JS3140](https://www.jupas.edu.hk/en/programme/polyu/JS3140/).

## Curriculum Comparison

**Years 1–2** are foundational at both schools: engineering mathematics, mechanics, thermodynamics, materials, programming, and electrical basics. HKUST front-loads more theoretical science; PolyU introduces aviation-specific context earlier.

**Years 3–4** is where they diverge sharply:

- **HKUST** deepens aerodynamics, propulsion, structural analysis, control systems, and a capstone design project — often involving UAVs, wind-tunnel work, or computational simulation. Electives reach into mechanical engineering breadth.
- **PolyU** focuses on aircraft systems, maintenance and reliability engineering, airworthiness and regulation, avionics, and (in the Air Transport stream) air transport operations and management, anchored by an industry-linked final-year project and a substantial internship block.

Rule of thumb: HKUST teaches you to **engineer the aircraft**; PolyU teaches you to **engineer and operate the fleet**.

## Careers and Salary Outlook

Hong Kong's aviation ecosystem is one of the largest employers of these graduates:

- **Airlines** — Cathay Pacific, HK Express, Greater Bay Airlines (engineering, fleet, reliability roles)
- **MRO** — HAECO and related maintenance firms
- **Airport & regulators** — Airport Authority Hong Kong, Civil Aviation Department
- **Manufacturing & R&D** — drone/UAV companies, aerospace suppliers, and (for HKUST graduates especially) general mechanical and engineering firms
- **Postgraduate study** — MPhil/PhD, or overseas master's in aerospace, common from HKUST

Graduate starting salaries for engineering degree holders in Hong Kong typically fall in the **HK$18,000–HK$25,000 per month** range, with aviation engineering positions and the licensed-engineer track offering strong long-term progression as licences and chartered status are accumulated. The Three-Runway System and continued cargo dominance mean local demand is structurally healthy, though the industry is cyclical and sensitive to global travel shocks — something the 2020–2022 period demonstrated vividly.

## Which Should You Choose?

Ask yourself three questions:

1. **Design or operations?** If you want to design and research aircraft and aerospace systems — and possibly leave aviation for broader engineering or academia — choose **HKUST JS5270**. If you want to work hands-on in Hong Kong's aviation industry on real fleets and operations, choose **PolyU JS3140**.
2. **How much do internships and industry links matter to you?** PolyU's structured internships, co-taught industry subjects, and licensing alignment are a genuine differentiator for employability in airline/MRO roles.
3. **How strong is your maths?** HKUST's theory load rewards students who are comfortable with calculus and physics (M2 strongly recommended). PolyU is more applied but still maths-intensive.

There is no wrong answer — both lead into aviation careers. The difference is the *kind* of engineer you become. Many students hedge by listing both across their JUPAS bands; if you do, place the one that genuinely matches your goals higher rather than ordering purely by prestige.

## Application Checklist

- Take **Physics** as a DSE elective; add **M1 or M2** (M2 preferred) if possible.
- Target Level 3+ in both languages and Mathematics Compulsory Part.
- Read the current-year admission score statistics on the official JUPAS pages before setting band order.
- Prepare for a possible **interview** — both programmes may interview shortlisted candidates; be ready to discuss why aerospace/aviation, a relevant project, and basic technical reasoning.
- Build evidence of genuine interest: drone projects, model aircraft, science fairs, aviation-related volunteering or visits all strengthen an application and give you something concrete to talk about.

Aerospace and aviation engineering is demanding, but for the right student it is one of the most rewarding technical paths Hong Kong offers — and you can train for it without leaving the city.
