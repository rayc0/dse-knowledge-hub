# Hong Kong Biomedical Engineering Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Biomedical Engineering (BME) sits at the intersection of medicine, biology and engineering — designing the imaging scanners, surgical robots, prosthetics, wearable sensors and tissue-engineered implants that modern healthcare runs on. For Hong Kong DSE students who love mathematics and physics but want their work to have a direct human impact, BME is one of the most compelling degree choices available in 2026. This guide walks through every UGC-funded undergraduate BME programme in Hong Kong, how admission works, what you will actually study, and where graduates end up.

## Why Biomedical Engineering Now

Hong Kong has made biotechnology and medical innovation a strategic priority. The InnoHK research clusters, the Hong Kong Science Park's Health Technology platform, and the Greater Bay Area's fast-growing medical-device manufacturing base have created sustained demand for engineers who understand both clinical needs and technical implementation. Globally, the medical-device market continues to grow faster than GDP, and an ageing population across East Asia guarantees long-term demand for diagnostic, rehabilitation and assistive technologies.

BME is also a hedge. The curriculum is engineering-heavy enough that graduates can move into general electronics, software, data science or quantitative roles, while the life-science grounding opens doors into hospitals, pharmaceutical and biotech firms, and graduate medical research. Few undergraduate degrees keep this many doors open at once.

## The Three Main UGC-Funded Programmes

Three of Hong Kong's eight UGC-funded universities offer a dedicated BME bachelor's degree through JUPAS: the University of Hong Kong (HKU), the Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK), and the Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU). City University and HKUST deliver biomedical content largely through electrical/mechanical engineering streams or postgraduate routes rather than a standalone JUPAS-coded BME degree, so the head-to-head comparison below focuses on the three direct programmes.

### HKU — Bachelor of Engineering in Biomedical Engineering (JS6925)

HKU's BEng(BME) is run by a dedicated School of Biomedical Engineering under the Li Ka Shing Faculty of Medicine, delivered in partnership with the Faculties of Engineering, Dentistry and Science. It is the largest of the three programmes, with a first-year intake of around **75 students**.

The defining feature is **five specialisation tracks** that let students customise their path from the second year onward:

- Advanced Biomedical Signals and Systems
- Biomaterials and Tissue Engineering
- Biomechanics and Biotransport
- Biomedical Imaging Technologies and Applications
- Omics Technologies

The curriculum is built around hands-on training — lab shadowing, specialised workshops, summer internships with industry partners, and a capstone design project. The programme holds full accreditation from the Hong Kong Institution of Engineers (HKIE), which matters for professional engineer registration later. HKU's medical school co-location means students get unusually close exposure to clinical environments and to the Omics/biomedical-imaging research that HKU is internationally known for.

### CUHK — Biomedical Engineering (JS4460)

CUHK's BME programme (programme code BMEG, JUPAS **JS4460**) is offered by the Faculty of Engineering with deep collaboration from the Faculty of Medicine. It is the most selective by virtue of being the smallest, with a first-year intake of just **42 students** — a tight cohort that typically translates into close faculty contact and strong lab access.

CUHK has historic strength in surgical robotics and medical imaging; the university's robotics and AI research feeds directly into BME electives and final-year projects. The department explicitly maps graduate destinations across research and development, manufacturing, quality assurance, consulting, clinical engineering, regulatory affairs and technology entrepreneurship — a useful signal that the curriculum is designed with employability in mind. Admission may include a selective interview.

### PolyU — BSc (Hons) in Biomedical Engineering (JS3150)

PolyU's BME degree (JUPAS **JS3150**) is the most application- and industry-oriented of the three, consistent with PolyU's broader engineering identity. Students enter through a broad engineering first year and then specialise, with roughly **22 places** allocated to BME out of a large shared first-year engineering intake.

PolyU's curriculum emphasises the devices used by health professionals in diagnosis, therapy and rehabilitation, and offers the option of a **secondary major in Artificial Intelligence and Data Analytics** or in **Innovation and Entrepreneurship** — attractive for students who want to combine BME with the AI skills that increasingly dominate medical-device development. PolyU's strong rehabilitation-sciences and optometry faculties give the programme a distinctive applied-clinical flavour.

## Entrance Requirements at a Glance (2026 Entry)

All three programmes use the standard HKDSE "3322" core baseline and require strong science/maths electives. The table below summarises the minimum bars — note that *meeting the minimum is not the same as being competitive*; actual admission scores run well above these floors.

| | HKU JS6925 | CUHK JS4460 | PolyU JS3150 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chinese Language | Level 3 | Level 3 | Level 3 |
| English Language | Level 3 | Level 3 | Level 3 |
| Mathematics (Compulsory) | Level 3 | Level 3 | Level 2 |
| Citizenship & Social Dev. | Attained | Attained | Attained |
| Electives | Strong science/maths electives | 1 of Physics/Chemistry/Biology/M1/M2 at Level 3 | 2 electives at Level 3 (or 1 + M1/M2 at Level 3) |
| First-year intake | ~75 | ~42 | ~22 (within shared engineering cohort) |
| First-year tuition (UGC) | HK$47,000 | HK$47,000 | HK$47,000 |
| HKIE accreditation | Yes | Yes | Yes |

A few practical notes:

- **Mathematics Extended Module (M1/M2)** is highly recommended at all three. BME is mathematically demanding — signal processing, biomechanics and modelling all draw on calculus and linear algebra well beyond the compulsory part. Many competitive applicants take M2.
- **Physics** is the single most useful elective for BME because so much of the field (imaging, instrumentation, biomechanics) is applied physics. Chemistry and Biology help for the biomaterials and physiology side.
- CUHK weights core subjects such as English, Mathematics and a science elective more heavily in its best-five calculation; check the university's published admission-grades documents each cycle for the latest median profile, as scores shift year to year.

## What You Will Actually Study

Across all three universities the first one to two years cover engineering fundamentals: calculus and linear algebra, physics, programming (usually Python/C++), circuits and electronics, mechanics, and an introduction to human anatomy and physiology written for engineers. The middle years move into BME-specific cores:

- **Biomedical instrumentation and sensors** — ECG/EEG acquisition, biosensors, signal conditioning
- **Biomedical signal and image processing** — filtering, MRI/CT/ultrasound reconstruction basics
- **Biomechanics** — modelling bones, joints, soft tissue and fluid flow
- **Biomaterials and tissue engineering** — implant materials, biocompatibility, scaffolds
- **Medical device design and regulatory affairs** — design controls, ISO 13485, the path from prototype to approved product

The final year is dominated by a **capstone design project**, often done with a hospital, a medical-device company or a research lab, plus a cluster of specialisation electives (imaging, neural engineering, rehabilitation robotics, computational biology, etc.). Internships are strongly encouraged everywhere and are effectively expected by employers.

## Career Prospects and Salaries

BME graduates in Hong Kong typically move into one of several lanes:

1. **Medical-device industry** — R&D engineer, product/quality engineer, regulatory affairs, clinical/field application engineer at multinationals and Greater Bay Area manufacturers.
2. **Hospitals and clinical engineering** — managing and validating medical equipment within the Hospital Authority and private hospitals; some pathways lead toward medical physicist roles.
3. **Biotech and health-tech start-ups** — Hong Kong Science Park and the InnoHK clusters actively recruit BME talent, and entrepreneurship support is built into the HKU and PolyU programmes.
4. **Software, data and quantitative roles** — the strong maths/programming core means many graduates pivot into health-data science, AI/ML, or general tech and finance roles.
5. **Postgraduate study** — a large minority continue to MPhil/PhD research or to medical-physics and graduate engineering programmes locally or overseas.

Starting salaries for engineering graduates in Hong Kong generally sit in the HK$18,000–HK$25,000 per month range, with medical-device R&D and well-positioned tech roles toward and above the upper end. The field rewards specialisation: graduates who add AI/data skills or pursue HKIE professional registration tend to see the steepest mid-career progression.

## How to Choose Between HKU, CUHK and PolyU

- **Choose HKU** if you want the broadest specialisation menu (five tracks), the strongest medical-school integration, and the largest cohort with extensive research exposure — especially in imaging and omics.
- **Choose CUHK** if you want a small, selective cohort with close faculty contact and want to tap CUHK's surgical-robotics and medical-imaging research strength.
- **Choose PolyU** if you want the most applied, industry-facing training and the option to formally combine BME with an AI & Data Analytics or Entrepreneurship secondary major.

For JUPAS banding, a sensible strategy for a strong science candidate is to place the preferred programme in Band A, a second BME programme in Band A or early Band B, and a related broad-engineering programme (electronic, mechanical or general engineering) as an insurance choice — BME admission scores are competitive and a related engineering offer keeps a transfer or postgraduate route open.

## Final Word

Biomedical Engineering is not the easy option — it demands comfort with heavy mathematics, physics and programming on top of biology — but it is one of the few Hong Kong degrees that simultaneously serves the healthcare mission, the city's biotech ambitions, and the practical reality that engineering plus data skills travel well across industries. For DSE 2026 candidates with strong maths and physics who want their engineering to matter to patients, all three programmes are excellent; the right pick comes down to cohort size, research strengths and how much you value built-in AI/entrepreneurship options.

Verify every figure on the official JUPAS programme pages and each university's admissions site before finalising your choices, as intakes, requirements and weightings are updated annually.
