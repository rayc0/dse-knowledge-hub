Hong Kong is one of the very few cities in the world that integrates traditional Chinese Medicine (CM) into both formal university education and the public healthcare system. Following the enactment of the Chinese Medicine Ordinance in 1999 and the establishment of the Chinese Medicine Council of Hong Kong, the city has built a rigorous undergraduate, master's and doctoral pipeline for CM, anchored by three publicly funded universities - Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU), The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK) and The University of Hong Kong (HKU) - each offering a six-year, full-time Bachelor of Chinese Medicine double-degree programme. For DSE students, mainland Gaokao candidates and international applicants who want a profession that fuses classical CM theory with modern biomedical science, Hong Kong's CM bachelor's pathway is genuinely distinctive: bilingual, internationally portable, and tied to a regulated, government-recognised practice in a global city. This guide breaks down programme structures, entrance requirements, tuition, internship arrangements and post-graduation licensing for the 2026 intake.

## 1. Why Hong Kong's "Chinese Medicine + Biomedical Science" Double Degree Is Different

Unlike Mainland China's traditional five-year Bachelor of Chinese Medicine, all three Hong Kong programmes are **six-year double-degree** courses. Graduates leave with two parchments: a Bachelor of Chinese Medicine (BCM) plus a closely linked biomedical or pharmacy bachelor's degree. The architecture exists for a reason. Since 2014 the Hospital Authority has run an "Integrated Chinese-Western Medicine" pilot in public hospitals, requiring CM practitioners to read Western-style medical records, interpret laboratory results, and communicate with Western-trained doctors. As a result, roughly 30-40% of credits across the three programmes go to anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, pharmacology, pathology and clinical-medicine fundamentals, while 60-70% covers CM theory, materia medica (中药学), formulae (方剂学), acupuncture and moxibustion, plus internal, external, gynaecology, paediatrics and orthopaedics-traumatology specialties.

This dual training matters at graduation. Holders are not only eligible to sit the **Hong Kong Chinese Medicine Practitioners Licensing Examination** to become Registered CM Practitioners, but they also possess the biomedical literacy required for cross-border practice, AHPRA registration in Australia, NCCAOM examinations in the United States, or further postgraduate research in clinical and translational CM.

## 2. Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU) - Bachelor of Chinese Medicine and Bachelor of Science (Hons) in Biomedical Science

HKBU was Hong Kong's first university to offer a UGC-funded CM bachelor's programme, launching in 1998. Today its School of Chinese Medicine is the largest CM academic establishment in the city, running everything from associate-degree top-up routes to research PhDs and continuing-education tracks for working practitioners.

**Programme code and structure.** The JUPAS code is **JS2410**, formally titled *Bachelor of Chinese Medicine and Bachelor of Science (Hons) in Biomedical Science*. The six-year, full-time curriculum builds in **no fewer than 15 months of clinical internship** (around 30 weeks of structured rotation plus three months of specialised attachment). Internship sites span HKBU's three University Chinese Medicine clinics, the Hospital Authority's 18 Chinese Medicine Centres for Training and Research, and partner teaching hospitals in Mainland China including Guangzhou University of Chinese Medicine's First Affiliated Hospital, Beijing University of Chinese Medicine's Dongzhimen Hospital, Nanjing UCM's Affiliated Hospital, Chengdu UCM's Affiliated Hospital and Heilongjiang UCM's First Affiliated Hospital.

**Entrance requirements.**
- **JUPAS (DSE) route.** The standard 4C+2X profile applies: Chinese and English at Level 4, Mathematics and Citizenship and Social Development at Level 3, plus two electives. Two science electives (Biology, Chemistry, Physics) are strongly preferred. Recent median Best-5 admission scores have ranged from 30 to 32 points.
- **Non-JUPAS (mainland Gaokao) route.** Applicants must score above their province's "Special Type Admission Control Line" (the de facto Tier-1 line), with successful candidates typically 80-120 marks above that threshold; an English subject score of 125/150 or higher is recommended. Applicants without Gaokao results may apply with IB (38+ recommended), A-Level (AAA) or SAT (1450+).
- **Interview.** All shortlisted candidates attend a bilingual Chinese-English interview that probes classical Chinese medical-text reading ability, comprehension of foundational CM concepts and patient-communication skills.

**Tuition.** Local UGC-subsidised tuition is approximately **HK$44,500 per year**; non-local tuition (including Mainland students) is approximately **HK$171,000 per year** for the 2025/26 cohort, totalling around HK$1.03 million across six years. A range of entrance scholarships covers up to a full first-year waiver, with continuation scholarships subject to GPA performance.

## 3. The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK) - Bachelor of Chinese Medicine

CUHK's School of Chinese Medicine was founded in 1998 and admitted its first undergraduate cohort in 2002. It is structured as a school under the Faculty of Medicine, making CUHK the only Hong Kong CM programme run within a comprehensive medical school - which means CM students share basic-science modules with MBChB students and gain integrated exposure at Prince of Wales Hospital, Pok Oi Hospital and the Nethersole Chinese Medicine Specialist Clinic.

**Programme code and structure.** The JUPAS code is **JS4542**, simply named *Chinese Medicine*. The six-year curriculum splits into a 4.5-year pre-clinical and clinical phase plus an 18-month full-time clinical clerkship. Graduates receive both the Bachelor of Chinese Medicine and a Bachelor of Science (Honours) in Biomedical Science.

**Entrance requirements.**
- **JUPAS.** Core requirements mirror HKBU's, but median Best-5 scores typically fall between **32 and 34 points** in recent intakes, with many admittees carrying additional weighting from M1 or M2 mathematics modules.
- **Non-JUPAS.** Mainland applicants apply through CUHK's Mainland Undergraduate Admission Scheme; competitive Gaokao totals usually exceed the provincial Special Type Admission Control Line by 100-150 marks, with English at 120/150 minimum. CUHK's overall Mainland intake is around 300, of which the CM programme typically admits 2-3 students per cohort.
- **Interview.** Predominantly conducted in English, with a Chinese segment, and frequently includes simulated patient-communication scenarios.

**Tuition.** Local UGC tuition is approximately **HK$44,500 per year**; non-local tuition for 2025/26 is approximately **HK$171,000 per year** with announced step-ups for 2026/27 and 2027/28 in line with UGC policy. CUHK's "Vice-Chancellor's Scholarships" cover full tuition plus a residence allowance for the highest-scoring Mainland and international entrants.

## 4. The University of Hong Kong (HKU) - Bachelor of Chinese Medicine

HKU was the latest of the three to enter the field, with its School of Chinese Medicine established under the Li Ka Shing Faculty of Medicine. The programme leverages HKU's MBBS infrastructure - including Queen Mary Hospital, the HKU-Shenzhen Hospital and a network of Mainland teaching hospitals - to deliver an integrative six-year course.

**Programme code and structure.** The JUPAS code is **JS6482**, titled *Bachelor of Chinese Medicine*. Years 1-3 emphasise basic biomedical sciences alongside CM foundations, Year 4 features junior clerkship at HKU-Shenzhen Hospital, and Year 6 culminates in a clinical clerkship at top Mainland CM teaching hospitals. The degree is conferred jointly with a Bachelor of Science in Biomedical Science.

**Entrance requirements.**
- **JUPAS.** Programme score is calculated on Category A subjects with M1/M2 or a Category C subject counted whichever is higher. Median Best-5 admissions have historically tracked CUHK's, in the **31-33 point** band, but rise sharply for applicants competing for HKU Foundation Entrance Scholarships.
- **Non-JUPAS.** Mainland applicants apply via HKU's National Admissions Scheme. English (TOEFL 80+ or IELTS 6.0+) and a strong Gaokao English score are mandatory; HKU explicitly requires "good command of written and spoken Chinese (Cantonese and Putonghua)" - the only programme of the three to flag Cantonese fluency upfront.
- **Interview.** Bilingual, with an emphasis on integrative case reasoning that bridges biomedical and CM frameworks.

**Tuition.** Local UGC tuition is approximately **HK$44,500 per year**; non-local tuition for 2025/26 is approximately **HK$171,000 per year**. HKU operates the city's most generous full-cost scholarship pool for top Mainland entrants, including the HKU Foundation Entrance Scholarship covering tuition, residence and living expenses.

## 6. The Bachelor of Pharmacy (Hons) in Chinese Medicine - HKBU's Alternative Track (JS2420)

For students drawn to herbal pharmacology, quality control and pharmaceutical R&D rather than clinical practice, HKBU also runs a separate **JS2420 Bachelor of Pharmacy (Hons) in Chinese Medicine** - a four-year programme focused on Chinese materia medica processing, GMP-compliant manufacturing, herbal-product analytical chemistry, and proprietary CM regulation. Graduates do not become Registered CM Practitioners; instead they enter the herbal-medicine industry, regulatory bodies (the Department of Health's Chinese Medicines Division), or proceed to MSc/PhD research. Tuition mirrors JS2410.

## 7. The Registered Chinese Medicine Practitioner Licensing Examination

A Hong Kong CM bachelor's degree alone does not authorise a graduate to practise. The **Chinese Medicine Practitioners Licensing Examination**, conducted by the Chinese Medicine Council of Hong Kong, is the gateway to the Registered CM Practitioner roster. Eligibility requires the satisfactory completion of an approved undergraduate CM degree of no less than five years' duration; all three Hong Kong programmes appear in Table 1 of the Council's annual Candidates' Handbook, so graduates qualify automatically.

The examination has two parts. The **written examination** is held in late May or June and covers CM fundamentals, classical theory, materia medica, formulae and acupuncture, and integrative biomedical knowledge. The **clinical examination** runs from late July to August and assesses live diagnostic and therapeutic competency through OSCE-style stations with standardised patients. Recent first-attempt pass rates have hovered around 55-65% for fresh Hong Kong graduates, considerably higher than the 30-40% pass rate for overseas-trained applicants. Successful candidates apply to the Chinese Medicine Council for registration as a Registered Chinese Medicine Practitioner under the Chinese Medicine Ordinance (Cap. 549).

## 8. Career Outcomes and Greater Bay Area Mobility

Hong Kong has roughly 7,500 Registered CM Practitioners serving a population of 7.5 million - a ratio that has been steadily expanding as the government rolls out a permanent integrated Chinese-Western medicine programme in public hospitals and operates the long-awaited **Chinese Medicine Hospital of Hong Kong** in Tseung Kwan O, the city's first dedicated CM teaching hospital, which opened phased clinical services from late 2025. This new infrastructure adds hundreds of full-time CM clinical posts, residency-equivalent training places, and academic positions, reshaping career trajectories for fresh graduates.

Typical first-job pathways:
- **Hospital Authority Chinese Medicine Centres for Training and Research.** Salaried clinical posts at HK$45,000-60,000 per month, with structured rotation across internal medicine, acupuncture and orthopaedics specialties.
- **Chinese Medicine Hospital of Hong Kong.** New residency-style positions launched 2025/26, with research and teaching pathways for those continuing to MSc/PhD.
- **Private practice and group clinics.** Independent practice typically begins after two to three years of supervised experience; income varies widely, with established practitioners earning HK$80,000+ per month.
- **Greater Bay Area cross-border practice.** Under the GBA Medical Talents Exchange and the streamlined cross-border CM practitioner recognition scheme, Hong Kong-registered CM practitioners can apply for short-term clinical practice rights in Guangdong Province, particularly in Shenzhen, Zhuhai and the Hengqin Cooperation Zone.
- **International CM practice.** Australian AHPRA registration is the most common overseas route; United States NCCAOM, Singapore TCMPB and Canadian provincial regulators also recognise Hong Kong qualifications subject to additional examinations.
- **Research and academia.** All three universities run MPhil and PhD programmes with Hong Kong PhD Fellowship Scheme funding for outstanding candidates.

## 9. Choosing Between HKBU, CUHK and HKU

Because the licensing exam treats all three programmes as equivalent, the choice should turn on personal fit rather than prestige.

- **HKBU** suits applicants who want the deepest CM-focused environment, the broadest Mainland teaching-hospital network and the longest clinical exposure. It is the most "CM-centric" of the three, and its alumni dominate the Hospital Authority's CM clinic workforce.
- **CUHK** rewards students who value tight integration with a comprehensive Western-medicine school, want to share modules with MBChB students, and aspire to translational or biomedical research alongside clinical practice.
- **HKU** is the strongest pick for students prioritising Greater Bay Area integration via HKU-Shenzhen Hospital, who are bilingual in Cantonese and Putonghua, and who want access to HKU's broader scholarship pool and global research footprint.

Whichever you choose, the six-year journey is demanding: classical Chinese-language study of the *Huangdi Neijing*, *Shanghan Lun* and *Jinkui Yaolue*, anatomy lab, Western-medicine clerkship, herbal-formulae composition exams, and overseas internship rotations all sit alongside a normal undergraduate workload. Strong written Chinese, biological-science aptitude, and the temperament to commit to a regulated clinical profession matter more than raw test scores. For students who fit, however, a Hong Kong CM bachelor's degree opens a genuinely globalised, dual-system clinical career - rare anywhere else in the world.
