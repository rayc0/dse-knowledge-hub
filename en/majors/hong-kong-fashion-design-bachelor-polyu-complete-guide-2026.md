# Hong Kong Fashion Design Bachelor Complete Guide 2026: PolyU JS3050 — JUPAS Scores, Five Specialisms, Portfolio, Careers

Hong Kong sits at the centre of global fashion supply chains — from the luxury retail corridor of Tsim Sha Tsui and Central, to the sourcing offices of Li & Fung, to the manufacturing networks stretching across the Greater Bay Area. For DSE students who want to turn creative instinct into a professional career in fashion, **The Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU)** offers the only full Bachelor of Arts (Honours) degree dedicated to fashion in the city.

This guide breaks down everything you need to know about the **BA (Hons) Scheme in Fashion** (JUPAS code **JS3050**) at PolyU's **School of Fashion and Textiles (SFT)** — formerly the Institute of Textiles and Clothing (ITC) — for the 2026 JUPAS cycle: scoring, five specialisms, interview and portfolio expectations, curriculum, and what graduates actually do after they leave.

## Why study Fashion in Hong Kong?

PolyU's **School of Fashion and Textiles** is the most established fashion school in Asia and one of the few institutions in the world that combines design, business, and textile technology under a single roof. Its alumni network includes founders, creative directors, and senior buyers across LVMH, Kering, H&M, Lane Crawford, Joyce, Lululemon, Anta, Bosideng, and Shein — and the school maintains active research partnerships with leading textile mills, sportswear brands, and digital fashion platforms.

PolyU's design disciplines have consistently been ranked among the strongest in Asia. The wider Faculty (which includes the School of Design and the School of Fashion and Textiles) has been recognised on global "best design schools" lists for more than a decade, and PolyU is currently ranked **54th in the QS World University Rankings 2026**, with Art and Design ranked top in Hong Kong.

For DSE students, the practical advantages are real: graduates can interview directly with brand offices and manufacturers in Hong Kong, Shenzhen, Dongguan, and Guangzhou — a sourcing ecosystem no other fashion school in the world sits inside.

JS3050 is a **scheme-based programme**: all entrants share a common Year 1, then choose one of five specialisms at the end of that year (subject to performance and quota).

## The five specialisms

This is the structural feature that makes JS3050 distinctive — most overseas fashion programmes force you to pick a specialism on day one. At PolyU you experience the full breadth before committing.

### 1. Fashion Design

The classic creative track. Develops drawing, draping, pattern-making, garment construction, CAD (Clo3D, Browzwear), trend research, and collection-building. Students build a portfolio across womenswear, menswear, or unisex collections, culminating in a Year 4 graduate show that is open to industry recruiters and the press.

### 2. Knitwear Design

A globally rare specialism. PolyU has dedicated **Stoll and Shima Seiki industrial knitting machines** on campus — the same equipment used by luxury knitwear houses. Students learn programming for whole-garment knitting (no sewing required), structure design, and yarn engineering. Graduates have an unusually open recruiting funnel into knitwear-heavy brands like Pringle, John Smedley, and the technical-knit divisions of sportswear majors.

### 3. Contour Fashion and Activewear

Lingerie, intimate apparel, swimwear, performance sportswear. This specialism leans heavily into anthropometrics, fit science, smart textiles, and biomechanics. Hong Kong is the global hub for intimate apparel manufacturing (Top Form, Regina Miracle, Clover Group all have R&D offices here), and graduates routinely move into product development roles at Victoria's Secret, Wacoal, Lululemon, Nike, and Anta.

### 4. Fashion Business

For students whose strength is commercial rather than purely creative. Covers buying and merchandising, retail operations, digital marketing, brand management, supply chain, and fashion analytics. Graduates target buying offices, e-commerce platforms (Tmall, Shein, Farfetch), and brand offices in roles like assistant buyer, merchandiser, or brand executive.

### 5. Fashion Innovation and Technology

The newest specialism, reflecting where the industry is moving: 3D garment simulation, digital fashion (NFTs, virtual try-on, gaming skins), smart wearables, AI-driven design, and sustainability technology. Strong link to PolyU's research labs in functional textiles and to industry partners like CLO Virtual Fashion and Browzwear.

If you do not secure a specialism (capacity-controlled), you graduate with a general **BA (Hons) in Fashion** — still a recognised degree, but the specialism placement is what most students aim for.

## Subject requirements and JUPAS scoring

Unlike Engineering or Business at PolyU, JS3050 has **no compulsory elective subjects**. The "4C" core (English, Chinese, Mathematics Compulsory Part, Citizenship and Social Development) is required at the standard university minimum — typically English 3, Chinese 3, Maths 2, CSD Attained.

### Preferred subjects (higher weighting in 2026)

PolyU publishes a subject weighting document each year. For JS3050, the following carry **enhanced weighting** (multiplier > 1.0):

- **Visual Arts** — strongest signal of creative intent
- **Technology and Living (Fashion, Clothing and Textiles)** — direct subject match
- **Design and Applied Technology** — useful for the Innovation & Technology track
- **Business, Accounting and Financial Studies (BAFS)** — relevant for Fashion Business
- **Economics**, **History**, **Chinese History**, **Geography**
- **Biology**, **Chemistry** — relevant for textile science / sustainable materials
- **Applied Learning** subjects in Fashion Image Design, Retail Management, etc. — counted with weighting

### Score formula

PolyU uses **"Any Best 5 + bonus for 6th subject at Level 3 or above"** with subject weighting applied. As a rough calibration:

- **2025/26 average score for JS3050: 170.4** (weighted, on PolyU's internal scale)
- Roughly equivalent to a raw DSE profile of approximately **5*, 5, 5, 4, 4, 4** in well-weighted subjects

This puts JS3050 in the **mid-band of PolyU programmes** — competitive but not at the level of Quantitative Finance, Physiotherapy, or Optometry. The decisive factor for borderline candidates is the **interview and portfolio**.

## Interview and portfolio: the real gatekeeper

This is where JS3050 differs sharply from most other JUPAS programmes. Academic merit gets you the interview, but the **interview is heavily weighted in final selection** — and for creative specialisms, applicants are strongly encouraged to bring a portfolio.

### What to expect

- **Format**: 15–30 minutes, group or individual, conducted in **English**
- **Invitation**: All Band A applicants are interviewed; Band B/C selectively
- **Bonus**: Strong interview performance gives **bonus scores** that can lift borderline academic candidates over the cut
- **Portfolio**: Not formally required, but **highly recommended** for Fashion Design, Knitwear, Contour & Activewear, and Fashion Innovation & Technology applicants

### What the portfolio should show

You do not need a polished, agency-style portfolio. What admissions tutors want to see is **evidence that you observe, think, and make**. A typical successful portfolio for a DSE applicant contains:

1. **Sketchbook pages** — research, mood boards, trend observation, fabric swatches
2. **Drawing studies** — figure sketches, garment details, technical flats
3. **One or two finished or partially finished garments** (photographed on a stand or model)
4. **Cross-disciplinary work** — textile experiments, 3D prints, illustration, graphic work, photography
5. **A short written reflection** on what you were exploring and what you learned

Visual Arts SBA work, ApL Fashion Image Design coursework, and personal projects (zines, upcycling, tailoring) all count. **Quality over quantity** — eight strong pieces beat thirty mediocre ones.

### Interview questions you should prepare for

- "Walk me through one project in your portfolio."
- "Which designer's recent collection caught your attention, and why?"
- "What is the difference between fashion and clothing?"
- "Why this specialism, not the others?"
- "Where do you see the fashion industry in five years?"

Knowledge of current designers (Jonathan Anderson at Dior, Demna at Balenciaga, Pieter Mulier at Alaïa, Sandy Liang, Robyn Lynch, Susan Fang) and Hong Kong / Greater China brands (JNBY, Bosideng, Comme Moi, Pronounce) is expected for design specialisms.

## Curriculum structure: what you actually study

### Year 1 (common)

Foundational design thinking, fashion history, basic pattern-making and construction, digital design tools, fashion business fundamentals, and one elective from each specialism cluster. End of Year 1: specialism selection.

### Years 2–3

Deep specialism content. Design students do studio-heavy semesters with frequent crits; Business students take buying, merchandising, retail analytics, and consumer behaviour; Innovation & Technology students take 3D simulation, smart textiles, and sustainability.

A typical week for a Design student in Year 2 involves 12–18 hours of studio plus lectures — significantly more contact time than most other PolyU programmes.

### Industrial placement (Year 3 summer)

Most JS3050 students complete a **work-integrated education** placement of 3–6 months. Common placements include:

- **Local**: Lane Crawford buying office, Joyce, G2000, Lululemon Hong Kong, Initial, Anteprima
- **Mainland**: Bosideng, Anta, Li-Ning, Shein, Urban Revivo
- **Overseas**: Selected placements with London, Milan, and Paris partners through the school's exchange network

### Year 4

Capstone year. Design specialism students build a **graduation collection** (typically 5–8 looks) shown at the **PolyU Fashion Show** — one of the school's flagship industry events. Business specialism students complete a final-year project with an industry partner. Innovation & Technology students often produce digital collections or smart-garment prototypes.

## Exchange and overseas opportunities

PolyU SFT runs one of the most active fashion-school exchange networks in Asia. Recurring partners include:

- **London College of Fashion / Central Saint Martins (UCL)** — UK
- **Parsons School of Design / FIT** — New York
- **Politecnico di Milano** — Italy
- **Bunka Fashion College** — Tokyo
- **Donghua University** — Shanghai

Roughly one in three Design students completes a one-semester exchange in Year 3, and the school subsidises portfolios of selected students to compete in international competitions (Hyères, ITS, YKK).

## Tuition and funding

For local Hong Kong students, JS3050 is a **government-funded UGC programme**:

- **Local tuition**: HK$44,500 per year (2026/27 standard)
- **Non-local tuition**: HK$171,000 per year
- **Additional studio fees**: ~HK$3,000–5,000 per year for materials, fabric, prints, and graduation collection costs (budget more in final year)

Available scholarships include the PolyU Entrance Scholarships (automatic for DSE 5** profiles), HKSAR Government Scholarship Fund, and a small number of industry-sponsored scholarships from Lane Crawford and other partners.

## Career outcomes: what graduates actually do

PolyU SFT publishes high employment rates — well above 95% within six months of graduation, including those continuing to postgraduate study.

### Typical first jobs by specialism

- **Fashion Design**: Assistant Designer, Technical Designer, Design Coordinator (sportswear, fast fashion, luxury subsidiaries). Starting salary HK$18,000–22,000/month.
- **Knitwear Design**: Knitwear Designer, Product Developer. Higher starting salaries due to scarcity — HK$20,000–25,000/month.
- **Contour Fashion and Activewear**: Product Developer, Technical Designer in lingerie/activewear. HK$19,000–24,000/month.
- **Fashion Business**: Assistant Buyer, Merchandiser, E-commerce Operator. HK$18,000–22,000/month.
- **Fashion Innovation and Technology**: 3D Designer, Digital Product Developer, Sustainability Analyst. HK$20,000–25,000/month.

### Mid-career trajectories

Graduates with five to seven years' experience typically move into Senior Designer, Buyer, Brand Manager, or Product Development Manager roles, often with international postings. A meaningful minority — especially in knitwear and contour — move into founder roles, launching their own labels with manufacturing relationships built during placement.

### Postgraduate pathways

A small but growing group of graduates continue to Master's programmes at Central Saint Martins, Parsons, Royal College of Art, or PolyU's own MA in Fashion and Textile Design. Postgraduate study tends to follow rather than precede the first job in this industry.

## Alternatives if you don't get JS3050

PolyU is the only Hong Kong university offering a full Bachelor's degree in Fashion. Realistic alternatives:

- **HKDI (Hong Kong Design Institute)** — VTC's design arm; offers Higher Diploma programmes in Fashion Design, Fashion Branding & Buying, and a top-up Bachelor's via partnership with Birmingham City University, University of South Wales, and others. Strong portfolio-building track for DSE students whose academic scores fall short of JS3050.
- **Caritas Institute of Higher Education** — small Higher Diploma in Fashion Design with progression options.
- **HKMU (Hong Kong Metropolitan University)** — BA in Creative Arts with fashion-adjacent options.
- **Overseas**: London College of Fashion, Central Saint Martins, Parsons, Bunka, Donghua — DSE results are widely accepted; portfolio carries the same weight.

For many DSE students, the strongest pathway is **HKDI Higher Diploma → top-up degree → industry**. Two years at HKDI sharpens the portfolio enough that some applicants subsequently transfer into PolyU's senior-year intake.

## A realistic prep plan for DSE students aiming at JS3050

If you are in **Form 5 or Form 6** and JS3050 is your goal:

1. **Take Visual Arts** as an elective if you can — it carries weight and produces portfolio material.
2. **Build a sketchbook**. Cheap A4 sketchbooks, daily observation, fabric scraps, ideas. This is more valuable than buying expensive software.
3. **Learn one digital tool well** — Procreate or Adobe Illustrator for flats. Clo3D if you have access. Demonstrating any 3D skill is a significant differentiator.
4. **Make one finished garment** before your interview, even a simple one. Photograph it well.
5. **Read widely**. Follow Business of Fashion, Vogue Business, WWD. Know what is happening in the industry.
6. **Visit shows**. PolyU's annual graduate show is open to the public — go in your Form 6 year. The HKTDC Fashion Week and the school's open days both run admissions briefings.
7. **Practise interviewing in English**. The interview is in English, and articulating creative ideas verbally is a learned skill.

## The honest assessment

JS3050 is the right programme for two kinds of DSE student: the **creative student** who has been making and observing for years and wants the most concentrated fashion training in Asia, and the **commercially minded student** who sees fashion as a global industry and wants to enter buying, merchandising, or product roles with a top-tier degree on the CV.

It is not the right programme for someone who likes shopping and watching Netflix runway documentaries — the workload is studio-heavy, the crits are public and direct, and the industry is famously demanding on hours and pay in the early years.

If you have read this far and you are still excited, that is a good sign. Start the sketchbook. The portfolio takes longer to build than the academic results — begin early.
