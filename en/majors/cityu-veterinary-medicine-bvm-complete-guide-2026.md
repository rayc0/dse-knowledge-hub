# CityU Bachelor of Veterinary Medicine (BVM) Complete Guide 2026

City University of Hong Kong's Jockey Club College of Veterinary Medicine and Life Sciences (JCC) runs the **only undergraduate veterinary programme in Hong Kong**, and the **only vet programme in Asia** to hold dual accreditation from both the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS) of the United Kingdom and the Australasian Veterinary Boards Council (AVBC). It is also a flagship programme co-developed with Cornell University's College of Veterinary Medicine, currently ranked 3rd in the world for veterinary science.

If you are a DSE candidate dreaming of becoming a veterinarian — whether your interest sits with companion animals, livestock, marine mammals, wildlife conservation or zoonotic disease research — this is the most direct, locally-based pathway. This guide consolidates the 2026 entry information for both JUPAS and Non-JUPAS applicants and explains what life inside the six-year BVM programme actually looks like.

## Why CityU BVM Matters

Before 2017, anyone in Hong Kong who wanted to qualify as a registered veterinary surgeon had to study overseas — typically a five- or six-year BVSc/BVM in the UK, Australia, the US or mainland China. The cost regularly exceeded HK$2 million in tuition alone, and graduates faced visa, conversion and relocation hurdles to come home.

The launch of the BVM at CityU in 2017 changed that calculus. The first cohort graduated in 2023 and successfully registered with the Veterinary Surgeons Board of Hong Kong, validating the entire programme design end-to-end. From admission to clinic, you can now train domestically for a profession that — until very recently — required years abroad.

Three features make CityU BVM unusually strong for an undergraduate veterinary programme:

1. **Dual professional accreditation**: graduates can register and practise in Hong Kong, the United Kingdom, Australia and New Zealand without sitting additional licensing exams.
2. **Cornell-co-developed curriculum**: the programme imports the structure, problem-based learning approach and clinical philosophy of Cornell's DVM — one of the most respected vet schools globally.
3. **One Health framing**: the curriculum integrates animal health, human public health and environmental health from day one, opening doors well beyond clinical practice.

The 30-student annual intake is deliberately small — clinical training requires intensive small-group rotations, hands-on lab work and one-on-one mentorship at the teaching hospital. This is also why competition is intense: only roughly 30 seats per year for Hong Kong's only domestic vet programme.

## Admissions Requirements (2026 Entry)

### JUPAS Applicants

CityU calculates the academic score for BVM using **Best 5** subjects, which **must include** four core/elective subjects:

- English Language
- Mathematics (Compulsory Part)
- Biology
- Chemistry

The fifth subject is your highest remaining grade. Chinese Language and Citizenship & Social Development must still meet minimum attainment levels (typically 3322), but they are not loaded into the academic score formula.

**Reference scores from the most recent admission cycle:**

| Metric | Score |
| --- | --- |
| Best 5 admission score (recent) | ~33.5 |
| Minimum entrance requirement | ~32 |
| Median admitted student | 30.5 and above |

For context, a Best 5 of 33 typically means five subjects averaging Level 6/6/7/7/7 — territory shared with HKU/CUHK MBBS and Pharmacy. CityU does, however, operate a **Flexible Admissions Arrangement** for JUPAS, meaning applicants who fall slightly below the indicative score but show outstanding non-academic strengths (animal-care experience, research, leadership) may still be considered.

Students admitted with the ten highest scores each year — across both JUPAS and direct entry — receive the **Flagship Scholarship**, which is one of CityU's most valuable undergraduate awards.

### Non-JUPAS / Direct Applicants

Non-JUPAS applicants include:

- International qualifications (GCE A-Levels, IB Diploma, SAT/AP, Australian ATAR, Canadian high school)
- Mainland Gaokao students applying via CityU's mainland admissions track
- Associate Degree / Higher Diploma graduates seeking advanced standing (rare for BVM)
- Mature students with relevant experience

Indicative thresholds for international applicants:

| Qualification | Indicative threshold |
| --- | --- |
| GCE A-Levels | A*A*A in three A2 subjects (Chemistry + Biology required) |
| IB Diploma | 40+/45 with HL Chemistry and HL Biology at 6/7 |
| SAT + AP | SAT 1450+ with multiple AP 5s (incl. Chem and Bio) |
| Gaokao | Top 1–2% of the province; subject scores in Chemistry/Biology are weighted |

English-language proficiency: IELTS 6.5 (no band below 6.0) or TOEFL iBT 79+. Direct/Non-JUPAS applicants must also submit:

- Personal statement
- Academic transcript
- Reference letters (typically two)
- Evidence of veterinary or animal-related experience (strongly preferred)
- Interview (shortlisted candidates only)

The **interview** is a meaningful part of selection. Expect questions on animal-welfare ethics, why veterinary medicine over human medicine, your experience handling animals, your reaction to scenarios involving euthanasia, and your understanding of the One Health concept.

## The Six-Year Curriculum

### Years 1–2: Pre-Clinical

The first two years are biomedical foundations: anatomy, physiology, cell biology, biochemistry, genetics and an introduction to species comparison. Students learn to handle a wide range of animals — small companion animals, equines, ruminants, exotics and aquatic species — for routine clinical examinations.

Core themes are introduced from day one:

- Animal health and welfare
- Aquatic animal health
- Emerging infectious diseases
- Food safety and public health

### Years 3–4: Para-Clinical and Clinical Foundations

Students move into pathology, microbiology, parasitology, pharmacology, veterinary public health and professional studies. Teaching is heavily case-based and uses **problem-based learning (PBL)** in small groups — a Cornell trademark. You will start interacting with patients in supervised settings at CityU's teaching practice.

### Years 5–6: Clinical Rotations and Research

The final two years are dominated by clinical rotations across seven core areas, with placements at CityU's facilities, partner hospitals and external clinics in Hong Kong, mainland China, Australia and the UK. Students also complete a **6-credit Research Project**, often co-supervised with Cornell academics or other international partners.

By graduation, students have built a Day-One Competency portfolio aligned to RCVS and AVBC standards — the same bar applied to graduates of UK and Australian vet schools.

### Teaching Facilities

CityU has invested heavily in:

- A purpose-built **Veterinary Medical Centre** providing referral-level small-animal care
- Anatomy and pathology teaching laboratories
- Diagnostic imaging suites (CT, MRI, ultrasound)
- Aquatic animal facilities for fish and marine species
- Microbiology, virology and One Health research labs

The College also collaborates with the Agriculture, Fisheries and Conservation Department (AFCD), Ocean Park, the SPCA and several private clinical groups for placements.

Over six years, local students pay roughly **HK$267,000** in tuition in total — by far the cheapest route to a fully accredited veterinary qualification anywhere in Asia, and a fraction of the cost of studying abroad.

### Living Costs

Estimated additional cost of living in Hong Kong:

- On-campus residence: HK$15,000–25,000 per year (highly competitive)
- Off-campus shared flat near Kowloon Tong: HK$5,000–8,000 per month
- Food, transport, books: HK$4,000–6,000 per month

Total annual cost-of-living range: **HK$60,000–120,000**.

### Scholarships

- **Flagship Scholarship**: top 10 admittees each year — among the most valuable undergraduate awards at CityU
- **CityU Entrance Scholarships**: tiered awards for high JUPAS scorers and outstanding direct applicants
- **HKSAR Government Scholarships**: includes the Hong Kong Scholarship for Excellence Scheme, Reaching Out Award and Self-financing Post-secondary Education Fund schemes
- **Targeted scholarships**: for non-local students, mainland students and indigenous Hong Kong students from rural backgrounds

Need-based assistance is available through the Working Family and Student Financial Assistance Agency (WFSFAA) Tertiary Student Finance Scheme.

## Accreditation: Why RCVS and AVBC Matter

### Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS)

The RCVS is the statutory regulator for vets in the United Kingdom. Holding RCVS accreditation means:

- Graduates can register with the RCVS and practise anywhere in the UK
- Graduates can practise in the European Economic Area through mutual recognition where applicable
- The programme has been benchmarked against UK Day-One Competency standards

### Australasian Veterinary Boards Council (AVBC)

The AVBC accredits veterinary programmes for Australia and New Zealand. AVBC accreditation means:

- Graduates can register and practise in Australia and New Zealand
- The programme has met AVBC's outcome-based competency standards
- CityU students can also seek accreditation transfer pathways into the United States and Canada (subject to additional steps)

### Hong Kong Registration

Local registration is via the **Veterinary Surgeons Board of Hong Kong (VSB)** under Cap. 529 Veterinary Surgeons Registration Ordinance. CityU BVM is a recognised qualification, and graduates register directly without sitting a separate licensing exam.

## Career Outcomes

### Clinical Practice

The most common path: **small-animal clinical practice** in Hong Kong. There are over 200 registered private veterinary clinics across Hong Kong and Kowloon, plus referral hospitals offering specialty surgery, internal medicine, dermatology and oncology.

Salary expectations:

- **New graduates**: HK$420,000–500,000 per annum (~HK$35,000–42,000 per month)
- **3–5 years experience**: HK$600,000–800,000 per annum
- **Senior / specialist**: HK$900,000–1,500,000+ per annum
- **Practice owners**: variable; established practices generate substantial profit

### Government and Public Sector

The **Agriculture, Fisheries and Conservation Department (AFCD)** is one of the largest employers, hiring veterinarians for animal welfare regulation, quarantine, wildlife management, food safety and zoonotic disease surveillance. Government vet salaries follow the Master Pay Scale and offer strong long-term security.

### Public Health and One Health

The One Health framing of the BVM positions graduates for careers spanning:

- Centre for Health Protection (zoonotic disease surveillance)
- Centre for Food Safety (foodborne pathogen monitoring)
- Hospital Authority and Department of Health research programmes
- WHO-affiliated regional initiatives

### Wildlife, Conservation and Marine

Ocean Park, WWF Hong Kong, the Kadoorie Farm and Botanic Garden, and several conservation NGOs employ vets for wildlife rehabilitation, marine mammal care and biodiversity research.

### Industry and Research

Pharmaceutical companies (animal health divisions of MSD, Zoetis, Boehringer Ingelheim), pet food companies, and academic research positions at CityU itself, HKU LKS Faculty of Medicine and other Asia-Pacific universities.

### Overseas Practice

Because of dual RCVS/AVBC accreditation, graduates can move to the UK, Australia or New Zealand without further exams. This makes the BVM unusually portable for an Asian veterinary degree — a significant advantage if you envisage a global career or intend to specialise overseas before returning.

## Application Strategy

### For JUPAS Candidates

1. **Place JS1801 in Band A1 or A2**. The programme is small and competitive — a Band B placement effectively excludes you.
2. **Lock in Biology and Chemistry as electives**. Both are mandatory for the academic score calculation.
3. **Aim for Best 5 of 33–34**. Targeting Level 5* in Eng/Maths/Bio/Chem and Level 5/5\* in your fifth subject puts you in the realistic admission band.
4. **Build genuine animal-related experience**. Volunteering at SPCA, working with a private clinic, helping at farms or wildlife rescues. This matters for the OEA section and Flexible Admissions consideration.
5. **Prepare for interview if shortlisted**. Practise scenario-based questions on welfare ethics, clinical decision-making and the One Health framework.

### For Non-JUPAS Candidates

1. **Apply early**. Direct applications open in September and rolling consideration starts immediately.
2. **Showcase animal experience**. Internships at clinics, shadowing vets, lab research with animal models — quantify hours and outcomes.
3. **Polish your personal statement**. Articulate why veterinary medicine, why CityU specifically, and how you understand One Health.
4. **Secure strong reference letters**. At least one from a science teacher who can speak to your lab work, ideally one from a vet or animal-care professional.

## Common Misconceptions

**"Veterinary medicine is easier to get into than human medicine."**
At CityU's intake size (30 vs HKU/CUHK MBBS at ~250+ each), entry standards are equally competitive and in some years marginally higher per seat.

**"Vets only work with cats and dogs."**
The BVM trains generalist vets ready for companion animals, livestock, exotics, aquatic species, wildlife and lab animals. Career flexibility is one of the programme's defining strengths.

**"I don't need to study Biology AND Chemistry."**
Both are mandatory electives in the academic score formula. Without both, you cannot meet the entry requirements.

**"There are no jobs for vets in Hong Kong."**
Demand exceeds supply. Hong Kong's pet population has grown sharply over the past decade, and government and public-health roles are also expanding under the One Health agenda.

## Final Word

CityU's Bachelor of Veterinary Medicine is a rare opportunity: Hong Kong's only undergraduate vet programme, dual-accredited internationally, jointly designed with Cornell, taught entirely in English, and priced at a tiny fraction of overseas alternatives. For DSE students who are serious about a career caring for animals — or about the broader One Health agenda linking animal, human and environmental wellbeing — JS1801 deserves a spot at the very top of your JUPAS list.

The competition is real, the programme is demanding, and the six years are long. But for the right student, this is the most efficient and impactful way to become a registered veterinarian based out of Asia — with the freedom to practise in Hong Kong, the UK, Australia or New Zealand once you graduate.
