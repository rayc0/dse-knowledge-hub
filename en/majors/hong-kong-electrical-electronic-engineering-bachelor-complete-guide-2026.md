# Hong Kong Electrical & Electronic Engineering Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Electrical and electronic engineering (EEE/EE) is the discipline behind almost every powered object you touch — the grid that lights the city, the chips inside your phone, the radios in a base station, the inverters in an electric vehicle, and the control loops that keep an MTR train on schedule. It is one of the broadest and most resilient engineering degrees a DSE graduate can take: the fundamentals transfer cleanly between power, semiconductors, telecommunications, robotics, and increasingly AI hardware.

Hong Kong has a specific reason to keep producing EE graduates. The city is pushing electrification of transport, smart-grid upgrades by the two power companies, a microelectronics centre in the Northern Metropolis, and a chip-design ecosystem feeding the Greater Bay Area. This guide walks through every JUPAS-accessible EE programme in 2026, what the curriculum contains, how admission works, and what the career — including the professional engineer pathway — looks like after graduation.

## The programmes and JUPAS codes at a glance

Five of the eight UGC-funded universities offer electrical/electronic engineering as a JUPAS-accessible route. Note that most are entered through a **broad first-year engineering gateway** rather than a department-specific code — you choose the EE major at the end of Year 1.

| University | JUPAS code | How you enter | Typical 2025 best-5 score (reference) |
|---|---|---|---|
| HKU | JS6963 (BEng Electrical Engineering / Electronic) | Some direct; also via JS6010 Engineering | ~24–27 |
| CUHK | JS4263 (BEng Electronic Engineering) | Direct programme entry | ~25–28 |
| HKUST | JS5200/JS5100 Engineering gateway | Broad-based; declare EE/ELEC in Year 1 | ~26–30 (gateway) |
| PolyU | JS3868 (BEng Electrical Engineering) / Electronic & Information Engineering | Scheme-based, then major | ~22–26 |
| CityU | JS1204 (Electronic & Information Engineering) / Engineering JS1201 | Gateway then stream | ~21–25 |

The scores above are *indicative* best-five DSE figures drawn from past-year reference data. JUPAS published score weightings and the score-conversion table change every cycle, and universities explicitly warn that prior figures should not be used to predict a future year's cut-off. Always verify the current formula on each university's admissions portal and the official JUPAS site before finalising your band order.

A practical note on codes: HKUST and CityU run almost everything through a wide engineering gateway, so the "EE" decision is deferred. HKU and PolyU offer a mix of broad and named entry. CUHK's Electronic Engineering is one of the more direct routes. If you are certain you want EE, a named code reduces the risk of being squeezed out of the major after a competitive first year.

## Entry requirements

All five programmes share the standard UGC minimum: DSE level 3 in English and Chinese, level 2 in Citizenship and Social Development, and a strong showing in mathematics. For engineering specifically:

- **Compulsory Mathematics** is essential — aim for level 5 or above. EE is the most maths-heavy of the mainstream engineering disciplines (signals, fields, control theory).
- **Mathematics Extended Module M1 or M2** is highly recommended and is given bonus weighting by HKU, HKUST and CUHK engineering admissions. M2 (calculus and algebra) maps most directly onto EE coursework.
- **Physics** is the single most useful elective; some programmes treat it as a de facto expectation even when not strictly required.
- A second science (Chemistry or ICT) or a strong M1/M2 result strengthens a competitive application.

International and non-JUPAS applicants (IB, A-Level, AP, overseas) apply through each university's non-JUPAS channel with comparable maths/physics expectations — typically IB 32–38 or A-Level grades around ABB–A*AA depending on the institution and year.

## What the curriculum actually contains

The first year of any EE degree is foundational and largely shared with other engineering majors: calculus and linear algebra, mechanics and electromagnetism, programming (usually C/C++ and Python), and an introductory circuits course. This common base is why gateway admission works — you can still pivot to mechanical, computer, or biomedical engineering before declaring.

From Year 2, the EE-specific core converges across all five universities:

- **Circuit analysis and electronics** — analog and digital circuit design, operational amplifiers, diodes and transistors.
- **Signals and systems** — Fourier and Laplace transforms, sampling, filtering; the mathematical spine of the degree.
- **Electromagnetics and fields** — Maxwell's equations applied to transmission lines, antennas and waveguides.
- **Digital systems and microprocessors** — logic design, HDL (Verilog/VHDL), embedded systems.
- **Control systems** — feedback, stability, and the theory behind robotics and process automation.
- **Power and energy** — machines, power electronics, and increasingly renewable integration and smart grids.

Years 3 and 4 are where the universities differentiate through specialisation tracks and a substantial final-year project (FYP) or capstone:

- **HKU** offers distinct Electrical Engineering and Electronic Engineering streams, with strength in power systems, energy and biomedical electronics.
- **CUHK** Electronic Engineering is known for IC design, photonics and biomedical signal processing.
- **HKUST** is research-intensive with deep benches in microelectronics, IoT, communications and AI chip design; its Academy of Interdisciplinary Studies also enables individualised tech tracks.
- **PolyU** leans applied — electrical services engineering, building electrical systems and information engineering with clear industry alignment.
- **CityU** combines electronic and information engineering with strong telecommunications and nanotechnology offerings.

## Accreditation and the professional engineer pathway

This is the section every prospective EE student should read carefully, because it determines the value of the degree in the job market.

All five programmes are accredited by the **Hong Kong Institution of Engineers (HKIE)**, and most also carry recognition from the UK's **Institution of Engineering and Technology (IET)** under the Sydney/Washington Accord framework, which makes the qualification portable internationally.

The professional pathway runs as follows:

1. **Graduate** with the accredited BEng — you become a Graduate Member of HKIE.
2. **Scheme "A" Training** — a structured two-year (approximately) graduate training programme under an employer registered with HKIE. This is the most direct route and is offered by major employers such as the power companies, the MTR Corporation, and large engineering consultancies.
3. **Professional Assessment** — submit experience reports and pass a professional interview/assessment.
4. **Corporate Member (MHKIE)** and, with continued experience, **Registered Professional Engineer (RPE)** status.

Becoming an RPE materially raises earning power and is effectively mandatory for engineers who certify electrical works, sign off on building services, or take supervisory roles in regulated sectors. When comparing programmes, confirm the *current* accreditation status on the HKIE register rather than relying on older brochures.

## Internships, exchange and industry exposure

EE programmes in Hong Kong place heavy weight on practical exposure. Expect:

- **Mandatory or strongly encouraged internships** — typically 8–12 weeks over a summer, often arranged through the department. Common hosts include CLP Power, HK Electric, the MTR Corporation, ASM, ASMPT, Hong Kong Applied Science and Technology Research Institute (ASTRI), and telecom operators.
- **Final-year projects** that are frequently industry-sponsored or research-linked, sometimes leading directly to a job offer.
- **Exchange programmes** — all five universities run semester-long exchanges with partner institutions in Europe, North America, the Mainland and Asia. EE travels well academically, so exchange is rarely a curriculum obstacle.
- **Greater Bay Area exposure** — growing internship and project links with Shenzhen's hardware and chip-design ecosystem, an increasingly common selling point for HKUST and CUHK.

## Career prospects and salaries

EE remains one of the more reliably employable Hong Kong degrees. Graduate employment surveys from the universities consistently show near-full employment for those seeking work, with a meaningful minority (often around one in six) proceeding to postgraduate study — a Master's or PhD in engineering, or an MBA later in their careers.

Typical employment destinations:

- **Power and utilities** — CLP Power, HK Electric: stable careers, strong Scheme "A" training, clear RPE progression.
- **Transport and infrastructure** — MTR Corporation, Airport Authority, engineering consultancies (Arup, AECOM, Atkins).
- **Semiconductors and electronics** — ASM/ASMPT, ASTRI, plus Greater Bay Area chip and hardware firms.
- **Telecommunications** — HKT, China Mobile HK, equipment vendors.
- **Finance and technology** — a notable share of EE graduates move into quantitative, technology or data roles at banks and tech firms, where the quantitative training transfers well.

On compensation: fresh-graduate monthly salaries for engineering roles in Hong Kong commonly start in the range of roughly HK$18,000–28,000, with the power companies and large consultancies typically at the upper end and structured progression once Scheme "A" and RPE milestones are reached. Mid-career engineers and project managers earn substantially more, often HK$40,000+ monthly, scaling with professional registration and responsibility. Graduates who pivot into technology or finance roles can see faster early salary growth but trade away the regulated career structure. Figures vary year to year — treat them as orientation, not a quote.

## How to choose between the five

A pragmatic decision framework:

- **You want the strongest research and chip/AI-hardware trajectory** → HKUST, then CUHK. Both feed naturally into postgraduate study and the Greater Bay Area semiconductor pipeline.
- **You want flexibility to switch engineering majors after Year 1** → favour the gateway codes at HKUST or CityU.
- **You want a power/energy and classic professional-engineer career** → HKU's electrical stream and PolyU's electrical/building services route are the most direct to Scheme "A" with the utilities.
- **You want applied, industry-aligned training with clear job mapping** → PolyU.
- **Your DSE score is mid-band but you are committed to EE** → CityU and PolyU offer realistic entry with strong accreditation; do not over-stack only the highest-cut-off codes in Band A.

A final strategic point on JUPAS banding: because most EE entry is via broad engineering gateways, your Year-1 performance often decides whether you actually get the EE major. If EE is your firm goal, prioritise a named EE code where one exists, and treat the gateway codes as flexible second choices rather than guaranteed routes.

## Key takeaways

- Five universities — HKU, CUHK, HKUST, PolyU, CityU — offer JUPAS-accessible EE, several through broad engineering gateways.
- Compulsory Maths plus M1/M2 and Physics is the strongest DSE preparation; M2 maps best onto the curriculum.
- All programmes are HKIE-accredited; the Scheme "A" → MHKIE → RPE pathway is the route to full professional and earning status.
- Internships with the power companies, MTR and the semiconductor sector are central to employability.
- EE remains broadly employable across utilities, infrastructure, semiconductors, telecoms and technology/finance.

Always confirm the current JUPAS score formula, programme codes and HKIE accreditation status on the official university and JUPAS websites before submitting your application — these change every admissions cycle, and the figures in this guide are reference orientation only.
