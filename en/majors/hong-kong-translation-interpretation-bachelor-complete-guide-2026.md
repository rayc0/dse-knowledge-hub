# Hong Kong Translation & Interpretation Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Translation is one of Hong Kong's oldest and most distinctive academic disciplines. Sitting at the intersection of Chinese, English, law, business, media and — increasingly — artificial intelligence, a Translation degree from a Hong Kong university opens doors that pure language degrees do not. Graduates work as in-house translators for global law firms and investment banks, conference interpreters at the Legislative Council, subtitlers for Netflix Asia, localisation managers at Tencent and Apple, civil-service Administrative Officers, and PhD researchers in computational linguistics.

This guide compares the five UGC-funded undergraduate routes available through JUPAS in 2026: CUHK's BA in Translation (JS4123), HKBU's flagship Arts scheme that includes a Translation stream (JS2020), Lingnan University's BA (Hons) in Translation (JS7204), PolyU's BA (Hons) in Linguistics and Translation (JS3818) and CityU's combined Department of Linguistics and Translation pathway (JS1109 / JS1101). It also covers what makes each programme unique, realistic DSE score bands, the impact of AI on the field, and where graduates actually end up.

## Why Study Translation in Hong Kong?

Hong Kong remains the only city in the Greater China region where Chinese (Cantonese and Putonghua) and English share co-official status, where bilingual legislation is constitutionally required, and where over 90% of listed companies file in both languages. The result is a permanent, structural demand for high-end translators and interpreters that purely-Anglophone or purely-Sinophone markets cannot replicate.

CUHK's Department of Translation is the oldest in Greater China (founded 1972); HKBU and PolyU are members of CIUTI, the global association of elite translation schools that includes the universities of Geneva and Vienna. Together, the five Hong Kong programmes graduate around 250–300 specialists annually — a tight enough pipeline that the market continues to absorb them despite headlines about machine translation.

A second reason is the **bilingual law and government ecosystem**. The Department of Justice, the Judiciary, the Legislative Council Secretariat, and major chambers (Des Voeux, Temple) hire dozens of Hong Kong–trained translators and court interpreters every year. These remain civil-service-graded careers with starting salaries comparable to junior solicitors.

## CUHK BA in Translation (JS4123) — The Gold Standard

The Chinese University of Hong Kong's Department of Translation is, by a wide margin, the most selective and most internationally recognised translation undergraduate programme in Hong Kong. It graduates roughly 30 students a year, almost all of whom find work in translation-related fields, government, or law within six months — or proceed to graduate study at CUHK, the Monterey Institute, ISIT-Paris, or the University of Geneva.

The four-year curriculum is structured around four pillars:

1. **Translation studies and theory** — including the canonical works of Eugene Nida, Lawrence Venuti, and Hong Kong's own scholars (Lai Tim-Cheong, Eva Hung).
2. **Specialised translation** — literary translation, legal translation, business and financial translation, journalistic translation, and subtitling.
3. **Interpreting** — consecutive interpreting from Year 2, with simultaneous interpreting introduced in Year 3 in the department's purpose-built SI labs.
4. **Computer-aided translation (CAT) and AI workflows** — Trados Studio, memoQ, post-editing of machine translation output, and prompt engineering for large-language-model translation.

CUHK uniquely offers a **double-major option with Linguistics, English, or Chinese Language and Literature**, and a popular minor in Comparative Literature. Most students do at least one semester of exchange at Durham, UCL, Edinburgh, or one of CUHK's partners in Taiwan and Japan.

**Typical DSE profile**: 4-4-4-4 in core, plus 5*-5 in two electives (Chinese Literature, History, or Literature in English commonly). Chinese Language at Level 5 is effectively required because half the translation workload is into Chinese.

## HKBU BA (Hons) Arts Scheme (JS2020) — Translation Stream

Hong Kong Baptist University's College of Arts offers a single broad scheme — JS2020 — under which students declare a major from among five options at the end of Year 1: Chinese Language and Literature, Creative and Professional Writing, English Language and Literature, Humanities, or **Translation**.

This design has two big advantages for DSE students who are not yet 100% committed to Translation. First, it lowers the stakes of the choice — a student admitted on a 4-4-5-4 + 5/5 profile can sample comparative literature, linguistics and translation theory in Year 1 before deciding. Second, it makes credit-bearing minors trivial: every Translation major also tends to pick up a second-stream minor in either English Language and Literature or Chinese Language and Literature.

The Translation stream itself emphasises **bilingual communication for the workplace** — corporate translation, legal translation, audiovisual translation and subtitling, and a strong sequence in business interpreting. HKBU is also one of the few Hong Kong departments with a dedicated **Christian and religious-text translation** elective, reflecting its denominational heritage.

Graduates typically enter media (RTHK, TVB, Now TV), publishing, NGO communications and the civil service.

## Lingnan University BA (Hons) in Translation (JS7204) — Concentrations

Lingnan's Translation department restructured its undergraduate programme in 2024 around two concentrations: **Cross-Cultural Communication** and **Digital Corporate Communication**. Students declare a concentration at the end of Year 2.

The Cross-Cultural Communication concentration is the more traditional path — literary translation, history of translation, interpretation, and cultural studies. The Digital Corporate Communication concentration is newer and reflects the reality that most translation jobs today involve project management, CAT-tool fluency, transcreation for advertising, and post-editing of MT output. Seven new courses launched in 2024 cover AI in translation, project management, and corporate communication workflows.

Lingnan's small size — around 50 Translation majors per cohort — is its biggest selling point. Many courses are run as 10-15 student seminars, and the department offers strong support for postgraduate progression to UK or European MA programmes.

**Typical DSE profile**: 4-4-4-4 in core, plus 4-5 in any two electives. Lingnan accepts a wider mix of elective subjects than CUHK and is more flexible on M1/M2 substitution.

## PolyU BA (Hons) in Linguistics and Translation (JS3818)

PolyU's Department of Chinese and Bilingual Studies (CBS) is unusual in pairing linguistics rigour with translation practice. The BA in Linguistics and Translation (JS3818) treats the two disciplines as inseparable: students take core courses in phonetics, syntax, sociolinguistics, discourse analysis, **and** in business translation, legal translation, simultaneous interpreting, and Chinese-English contrastive grammar.

This pairing is particularly attractive to students considering postgraduate study in **computational linguistics, NLP, or speech technology** — PolyU has historically strong links to corporate research labs at Huawei, Tencent, and Alibaba's DAMO Academy.

PolyU also runs a broader scheme — **JS3241 (Translation, Language Sciences and Analytics)** — under which students can apply with a less specific commitment and choose between BA Linguistics and Translation, BSc Language Science and Technology (JS3243), or BSc Speech-Language Pathology (a separately competitive code). Students who later decide they prefer the technology side gain a more flexible exit.

PolyU's compulsory **Work-Integrated Education (WIE)** requirement places every Linguistics and Translation student in a 200-hour internship — typically with the Department of Justice, a court, a publishing house, or a corporate communications team — before graduation.

## CityU Department of Linguistics and Translation (JS1109 / JS1101)

CityU does not offer a standalone "BA in Translation" code. Instead, the Department of Linguistics and Translation administers two adjacent JUPAS codes:

- **JS1109 (BA Linguistics and Language Applications)** — features include general linguistics, computational linguistics, corpus linguistics, experimental linguistics, and language technology applications. Translation is available as a substantial elective stream.
- **JS1101 (BA English)** — streams in English and Professional Communication or Language and Literature, with Translation electives accessible across the department.

CityU's distinctive offering is its **Joint Bachelor's Degree Programmes with National Taiwan University and Columbia University**: students spend two years at CityU and two years at the partner institution and receive both degrees on graduation. Starting in 2026/27, JUPAS applicants will be able to apply directly for Year-1 entry to the CityU–Columbia joint programme — a route worth flagging for the strongest candidates.

## Specialisations Inside the Field

Whichever programme you enter, you will eventually specialise. The main paths are:

**Legal Translation** — by far the most lucrative pre-postgraduate exit. The Department of Justice runs an in-house Legal Translation Officer (LTO) civil-service grade with a starting MPS pay point comparable to that of an Assistant Solicitor. CUHK, HKBU and CityU all teach dedicated legal translation modules.

**Conference Interpretation** — simultaneous interpreting from a booth. This is a postgraduate specialisation (CUHK MA Interpreting, PolyU MA Translating and Interpreting) but you must start consecutive interpreting at undergraduate level to be competitive for admission. CUHK and PolyU have the best SI lab facilities.

**Audiovisual Translation and Subtitling** — Netflix Asia, Now TV, Disney+ and the streaming platforms have created hundreds of new freelance roles in Hong Kong since 2022. HKBU and Lingnan have the strongest curricular coverage.

**Localisation and Transcreation** — game localisation (Tencent, NetEase, miHoYo), software localisation (Apple, Microsoft, Google) and marketing transcreation. Increasingly an AI-augmented workflow that values post-editing skill more than from-scratch translation.

**Court Interpretation** — Cantonese-English in the Magistrates' Courts, with a separate examination administered by the Judiciary. A reliable second career for many translation graduates.

## AI, Machine Translation and the Future of the Field

The question every prospective Translation student asks in 2026 is reasonable: with GPT-class models, will there still be a profession in four years' time?

The honest answer from Hong Kong's departments is "yes, but transformed." All five programmes have rewritten their curricula since 2023 to centre **post-editing of machine translation (PEMT) and AI-assisted workflows** rather than from-scratch human translation for routine content. The premium has moved up the value chain: legal, literary, diplomatic, and high-stakes commercial translation still command human rates, while bulk content has migrated to MT + human QA.

This means the skill set graduates need looks different: prompt engineering for LLM translation, terminology and TM management in CAT environments, quality estimation, and — crucially — **subject-matter expertise**. A translator with strong knowledge of finance, IP law, or medicine is far harder to displace than one with general bilingual ability.

Several departments now require or strongly recommend a minor in a substantive content domain (law, economics, computer science, biology) precisely for this reason.

## Postgraduate Progression

Around 35-45% of Hong Kong Translation graduates proceed directly to postgraduate study — significantly higher than the average Arts graduate. Common destinations:

- **CUHK MA in Translation** and **MA in Computer-Aided Translation** (one-year taught, very strong)
- **PolyU MA in Translating and Interpreting** (CIUTI-accredited; conference interpreting track)
- **HKBU MA in Translation and Bilingual Communication**
- **University of Leeds, University of Bath, UCL MSc in Specialised Translation** (UK)
- **University of Geneva FTI**, **ISIT Paris**, **Monterey Institute of International Studies** (US)

For interpreting specifically, postgraduate study is essentially mandatory — no one is hired as a conference interpreter directly out of an undergraduate degree.

## Realistic Career Outcomes

Six months after graduation, a typical CUHK or PolyU Translation cohort distributes roughly as:

- 20-25% in postgraduate study (Hong Kong or overseas)
- 20-25% in government or court translation/interpretation positions
- 25-30% in corporate roles — banks, law firms, consultancies, in-house translation departments
- 15-20% in media, publishing, and creative industries
- 10-15% in education and academia
- A growing minority (5-10%) in tech localisation and AI/NLP-adjacent roles

Starting salaries range from HKD 23,000-30,000 per month for entry-level corporate translation, HKD 30,000+ for Department of Justice LTOs, and competitive packages for tech localisation that can reach HKD 35,000-40,000 at FAANG-tier companies.

## Choosing Between Programmes

A simplified decision framework:

- **Want the most rigorous, internationally recognised programme and have the DSE score?** → CUHK JS4123.
- **Want academic flexibility and time to decide between five Arts majors?** → HKBU JS2020.
- **Prefer small seminars and a balance of traditional and digital tracks?** → Lingnan JS7204.
- **Interested in the linguistics/NLP/technology side as well as translation?** → PolyU JS3818 or the broader JS3241 scheme.
- **Want maximum international mobility, including a joint degree with NTU or Columbia?** → CityU JS1109 (Linguistics and Language Applications, with translation electives).

A final note: Translation programmes in Hong Kong place much heavier emphasis on Chinese Language proficiency than English-stream Arts programmes. A DSE Chinese Language score of Level 5 or above is effectively a baseline expectation at CUHK and PolyU and a strong asset at HKBU and Lingnan. If your strength is overwhelmingly in English with weaker Chinese, you may want to compare these programmes against [Hong Kong Linguistics](/en/majors/hong-kong-linguistics-bachelor-complete-guide-2026) or English-stream Arts programmes before applying.

## Conclusion

Translation in Hong Kong is not a shrinking discipline — it is a rapidly restructuring one. The five UGC-funded undergraduate programmes have all updated their curricula to reflect the realities of AI, post-editing, and a bifurcating market in which routine work is automated and specialised work commands a premium. Students who combine strong bilingual fluency with a substantive content domain — law, finance, technology, or media — continue to graduate into a market that values them. For DSE candidates with native or near-native Cantonese and strong English (or vice versa), this remains one of the most distinctively "Hong Kong" degrees on offer, and one of the strongest gateways into the city's bilingual professional ecosystem.
