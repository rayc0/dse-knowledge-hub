# Hong Kong Chemical Engineering Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Chemical engineering is the discipline that turns laboratory chemistry into industrial reality. It is the engineering of *processes* — taking a reaction that works in a beaker and scaling it into a plant that runs safely, economically and continuously at the scale of thousands of tonnes a year. Refineries, pharmaceutical manufacturing, semiconductor photoresist and gas supply, batteries and electrolytes, polymers, foods, water treatment, hydrogen and carbon capture all sit on chemical-engineering foundations. The discipline's "process-systems thinking" — mass and energy balances, reaction kinetics, separation, control and economics — is precisely what the Greater Bay Area's new-energy, advanced-materials, biopharmaceutical and environmental industries are short of. This guide covers everything a DSE student needs to know about studying chemical engineering in Hong Kong in 2026.

## The Hong Kong landscape: a two-route map

Unlike mechanical or civil engineering, chemical engineering is **not** offered as a standalone BEng at all eight UGC universities. Hong Kong realistically gives DSE students two distinct routes:

1. **The engineering route — HKUST JS5220 (Department of Chemical and Biological Engineering).** This is the only true accredited *Chemical Engineering* BEng programme in Hong Kong, and the flagship for anyone targeting the process industries or a Chartered Chemical Engineer career.
2. **The applied-science route — PolyU JS3011 (BSc (Hons) Scheme in Biotechnology and Chemical Technology), via the BSc (Hons) in Chemical Technology.** This is a science degree with a strong applied/industrial-chemistry flavour rather than a process-engineering degree — closer to "industrial chemistry" than to "chemical engineering" in the classical sense.

HKU and CUHK do not run dedicated undergraduate chemical engineering; their relevant offerings are chemistry, materials, or energy-and-environment programmes. CityU's closest undergraduate options are BSc Chemistry (including forensic and cosmetic streams) rather than a chemical-engineering BEng. So for the vast majority of DSE students serious about chemical engineering, the decision comes down to **HKUST JS5220 vs PolyU JS3011** — and they are genuinely different degrees, not just two versions of the same thing.

## HKUST JS5220 — Chemical and Biological Engineering

HKUST admits chemical engineering students through **department-based admission**. Applicants who already know they want this discipline enter the Department of Chemical and Biological Engineering directly through JS5220, rather than through the broad School of Engineering (JS5200) gateway. Inside the department, students can ultimately graduate with a BEng in **Chemical Engineering**, **Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering**, or **Chemical Engineering with an extended major in, for example, Artificial Intelligence**.

**Admission requirements.** HKUST's general JUPAS requirement is the familiar "**332A33**" pattern — Level 3 in English and Chinese, 3 in Mathematics, Attained in Citizenship & Social Development, and Level 3 in two electives. For JS5220 the department gives a clear weighting preference to **science subjects / ICT and Mathematics Extended (M1/M2)**. In practice, competitive applicants present Chemistry plus Physics or Biology, ideally with M1 or M2.

**Admission scores.** Under HKUST's 2026 simulated scoring formula, JS5220 showed a **median around 47** and a **lower quartile around 44**, with the highest attainable score near 89. Translated into DSE reality, the department typically takes students in roughly the **5×4 to 5×5 / 5\* band across best-five-or-six subjects** — strong, but generally a notch below HKUST's most over-subscribed programmes such as data science or quantitative finance. This makes JS5220 one of the more attainable HKUST engineering entries for a well-prepared science student.

**Curriculum.** Year 1 is a common engineering foundation (mathematics, physics, chemistry, computing and an introduction to engineering). From Year 2 the core chemical-engineering sequence builds: material and energy balances, fluid mechanics, heat and mass transfer, thermodynamics, chemical reaction engineering, separation processes, process control, and a capstone **plant/process design** project. The "and Biological" half of the department's name is real — students take significant bioprocess, biomolecular and biotechnology content, which aligns the degree well with Hong Kong's and the GBA's biopharmaceutical push.

**Accreditation.** HKUST's chemical engineering BEng is accredited by **The Hong Kong Institution of Engineers (HKIE)**. Because HKIE is a signatory to the **Washington Accord**, an HKIE-accredited degree is recognised for engineering practice across the UK, US, Australia, Canada, Singapore and other signatory jurisdictions — the foundation for chartered status worldwide.

## PolyU JS3011 — Biotechnology and Chemical Technology Scheme

PolyU's JS3011 is a **broad-admission scheme**, not a single programme. Students are admitted into the scheme first and select a major — **BSc (Hons) in Chemical Technology** or **BSc (Hons) in Applied Biology with Biotechnology** — during Semester 2 of Year One, based on preference, GPA and an interview.

**Admission requirements.** JS3011 has **no compulsory subject requirement**, which makes it unusually accessible — a student with strong DSE results but only one science elective can still be competitive. The first-year intake of about 152 is shared across the related science schemes (JS3008, JS3011, JS3030, JS3255), with roughly **55 places** flowing into JS3011 itself.

**What it actually is.** Chemical Technology at PolyU is an **applied-chemistry / industrial-chemistry science degree** — instrumental analysis, materials chemistry, polymer and surface science, environmental and analytical chemistry, with industry-facing project and placement components. It is *not* a process-engineering degree: you will not do the full unit-operations / plant-design / process-control core that defines a classical chemical-engineering programme, and the award is a **BSc, not a BEng**. For laboratory, QA/QC, analytical, materials and product-development careers this is an excellent fit; for plant process engineering and chartered chemical-engineer status, JS5220 is the stronger base.

## HKUST JS5220 vs PolyU JS3011 at a glance

| | **HKUST JS5220** | **PolyU JS3011 (Chemical Technology)** |
|---|---|---|
| Award | BEng (Chemical Eng / Chem & Biomolecular Eng) | BSc (Hons) Chemical Technology |
| Nature | Process engineering | Applied / industrial chemistry |
| Admission | Department-based, direct | Broad scheme, major chosen Year 1 |
| Subject preference | Science/ICT + M1/M2 weighted | No compulsory subject |
| Competitiveness | Mid-upper HKUST engineering band | Moderate, accessible |
| HKIE accreditation | Yes (Washington Accord) | Not an engineering accreditation |
| Best for | Process/plant, energy, chartered engineer | Lab, QA/QC, materials, product R&D |

A useful rule of thumb: if your goal contains the words *plant, process, refinery, reactor, scale-up, or Chartered Engineer*, aim for JS5220. If it contains *laboratory, analysis, materials, formulation, or product chemistry*, JS3011 is a credible and less competitive path.

## Careers and salaries

Chemical and process engineering graduates in Hong Kong and the GBA have a wider exit range than the small local "heavy chemical" footprint suggests. Typical destinations include:

- **Energy and gas** — Towngas, CLP, power and LNG operators (process and plant roles).
- **Semiconductor and electronics materials** — photoresists, electronic gases, CMP slurries, increasingly important across the GBA fab supply chain.
- **Biopharmaceutical manufacturing** — bioprocess, fill-finish and QA roles, a fast-growing GBA segment.
- **Environmental and water** — wastewater, desalination, emissions control, carbon capture.
- **New energy** — battery materials and electrolytes, hydrogen, fuel cells.
- **Consumer products, food, cosmetics, polymers** — formulation, scale-up and quality.
- **Finance and consulting** — the quantitative process-economics training travels well into operations consulting and commodities.

Fresh-graduate engineering salaries in Hong Kong for 2026 generally sit in the **HK$18,000–25,000/month** range, with process/chemical engineers in energy and semiconductor firms often at the upper end of that band and faster progression for those who pursue chartered status. Mainland GBA new-energy and biopharma employers frequently match or exceed Hong Kong starting packages for strong bilingual graduates.

## The professional engineer pathway

For the engineering route, the qualification ladder is:

1. **HKIE-accredited BEng** (HKUST JS5220) → graduate membership of HKIE.
2. **Scheme "A" training** — a structured 2–4 year graduate training agreement with an HKIE-approved employer.
3. **Professional Assessment** — the HKIE Professional Interview/Assessment.
4. **Corporate Member (MHKIE) → Registered Professional Engineer (RPE)** in the relevant discipline.

Because HKIE is a Washington Accord signatory, the same BEng also supports **Chartered Engineer (CEng)** via IChemE/IMechE in the UK and equivalent recognition in Australia and Singapore — important if your career may cross borders. The PolyU BSc route does not lead to RPE/CEng directly; graduates wanting chartered engineering status typically convert via a relevant master's degree.

## How to choose, and what to do in DSE

**Subject planning.** For JS5220, take **Chemistry plus Physics (or Biology)** and, if you can sustain it, **M1 or M2** — the explicit M1/M2 weighting can be the difference at the margin. For JS3011, Chemistry or Biology helps but is not mandatory; focus on a strong overall best-five profile.

**Decision checklist:**

- Do I want to engineer *processes and plants*, or do *applied chemistry*? → JS5220 vs JS3011.
- Do I need a Washington-Accord BEng for an international engineering career? → JS5220.
- Is my DSE profile strong in science + M1/M2? → JS5220 is realistic; if science is thinner, JS3011 is the safer route into the field.
- Am I drawn to biotech/biopharma? → Both work; JS5220's biomolecular track keeps the engineering door open, JS3011's biotechnology major is more lab-oriented.

**Band A strategy.** A common, sensible JUPAS pattern is JS5220 as a high Band A choice with JS3011 (or a broad PolyU/CityU science scheme) as a lower-band safety that still keeps you inside the chemical-sciences field.

## Bottom line

Hong Kong's undergraduate chemical-engineering market is narrow but clear. **HKUST JS5220** is the one true accredited Chemical Engineering BEng — the right target if you want process engineering, the chartered-engineer pathway, and maximum international portability, and it is among the more attainable HKUST engineering programmes for a solid science student. **PolyU JS3011's Chemical Technology** is a strong, more accessible applied-science alternative that opens laboratory, analytical, materials and product-R&D careers, but it is a BSc, not an engineering degree. Decide which of those two futures you want *before* you submit your JUPAS list — it changes which programme should sit at the top of your Band A.
