# Hong Kong Robotics Engineering Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Robotics has moved from a research curiosity into Hong Kong's industrial spine. Cleanroom assembly at ASMPT, autonomous baggage handling at Hong Kong International Airport, semiconductor wafer-handling robots at SMIC's Tseung Kwan O facility, surgical robotics trials at Queen Mary Hospital and DJI's drone fleets all draw on the same pool of robotics-trained engineers. For DSE candidates entering university in September 2026, four universities now offer dedicated robotics undergraduate pathways, and the Hong Kong Microelectronics R&D Institute, the CUHK-Shenzhen Robotics Institute (CKSRI) and InnoHK platforms are funding the local supply chain. This guide unpacks the JUPAS programmes, the four-year curriculum, the competition and internship routes, graduate salaries, professional registration and master's articulation in one place.

## The Hong Kong robotics talent landscape in 2026

Hong Kong's robotics scene is unusually concentrated. Five anchors define the ecosystem: HKUST's Cheng Kar-Shun Robotics Institute (CKSRI), the CUHK Multiscale Medical Robotics Center (MRC) at the InnoHK cluster, PolyU's Research Institute for Advanced Manufacturing (RIAM), the Hong Kong Centre for Logistics Robotics (HKCLR) on the HKSTP campus, and DJI's Greater Bay Area robotics talent pipeline operating out of Shenzhen with strong cross-border recruitment from Hong Kong universities.

The job ladder splits into three streams. Hardware roboticists go into manufacturing automation (ASMPT, FANUC, ABB, Yaskawa), aerospace and aviation maintenance (HAECO, Cathay Pacific Engineering), and industrial logistics (MTR Corporation, Hong Kong International Airport). Software roboticists drift into autonomous systems, computer vision and SLAM at DJI, Pony.ai (Hong Kong office), SenseTime and the InnoHK platforms. The medical robotics niche — surgical robots, rehabilitation exoskeletons, capsule endoscopy — is dominated by CUHK MRC spin-offs and Queen Mary–HKU joint trials.

## The four JUPAS robotics pathways

### PolyU JS3741 — BEng (Hons) Scheme in Mechanical Engineering (Intelligent Robotics Engineering / Mechanical Engineering)

PolyU is the only Hong Kong university with "Robotics Engineering" in the degree title. JS3741 is a Year-1-broad-entry scheme: students enrol into the common Mechanical Engineering Scheme, then declare either the Intelligent Robotics Engineering degree or the Mechanical Engineering degree at the end of Year 1 without further competitive assessment. The scheme intake is approximately 51 places out of a 351-strong common engineering Year-1 pool.

The Intelligent Robotics Engineering specialism is taught jointly by the Department of Mechanical Engineering and the Department of Industrial and Systems Engineering, with strong contributions from PolyU's RIAM. Compulsory courses include Robotics Fundamentals, Mechatronic Systems Design, Industrial Automation, Machine Vision, Embedded Systems and a capstone Robot Engineering Design Project. PolyU does not publish a specific median DSE score for JS3741, but for the 2025 cycle there were 334 Band A applications producing 27 offers, with 16 of those offers going to Band A applicants — a tight conversion ratio consistent with PolyU's middle-band engineering schemes.

### CUHK JS4408 — Mechanical and Automation Engineering (MAEG)

CUHK's MAEG programme is the most automation-and-robotics-flavoured general mechanical degree in the JUPAS catalogue. The curriculum is anchored on two pillars: the physical interaction of engineering systems with the world (mechanics, thermodynamics, materials) and the self-operability of those systems (control, sensing, robotics). MAEG students take MAEG2050 "Robot Development in Practice: From Design to Prototyping" in Year 2 — a project-based course in which teams design and prototype a working robot — and can stream into a Robotics and AI focus through Year-3 electives such as MAEG3070 Robotics: Modelling and Control and MAEG4070 Advanced Robotics.

CUHK MAEG students get unusually direct exposure to research robotics through the CUHK Department of Mechanical and Automation Engineering's robotics labs and through the Multiscale Medical Robotics Center at InnoHK. The MAEG programme is accredited by the Hong Kong Institution of Engineers (HKIE) under Scheme A in the Mechanical discipline. Cantonese-medium graduates targeting research often follow MAEG with an MPhil at the same department before applying to ETH Zürich, CMU Robotics Institute or Imperial College for PhD study.

### CityU JS1207 — BEng (Hons) Mechanical Engineering

CityU's Mechanical Engineering programme under the Department of Mechanical Engineering offers a Smart Manufacturing and Robotics stream. The compulsory core covers mechanics, thermodynamics, materials and manufacturing, and the robotics electives include Industrial Robotics, Robot Vision, Autonomous Systems and a Smart Manufacturing capstone. CityU's strength lies in its tight integration with the Hong Kong Centre for Cerebro-cardiovascular Health Engineering (COCHE) and the Hong Kong Centre for Logistics Robotics (HKCLR), both InnoHK centres co-led by CityU faculty. Year-3 students are eligible to apply for paid research assistantships at these centres, which often convert into MPhil offers.

### HKUST — Robotics Minor and BEng with robotics specialisation

HKUST does not yet operate a JUPAS-coded "Robotics Engineering" degree. Instead it offers a Robotics Minor open to all School of Engineering and School of Science undergraduates, drawing on courses from Electronic and Computer Engineering (ELEC4220 Mobile Robots), Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering (MECH4810 Robot Manipulators), and Computer Science and Engineering (COMP4901L Robotic Perception and Learning). HKUST students gain robotics depth primarily through the Cheng Kar-Shun Robotics Institute, the HKUST Robotics Team (founded 2004) and the legendary Team ENTERPRIZE RoboMaster squad — which won the RoboMaster International Championship in 2017, 2018, 2019, 2023 and 2024.

HKUST's BEng in Electronic Engineering, Computer Engineering and Mechanical Engineering all funnel into robotics research. JS5200 (School of Engineering broad-based admission) is the standard JUPAS entry, requiring DSE Best 5 in the high 30s for competitive admission. Students declare their major and pick up the Robotics Minor at the end of Year 1.

## A typical four-year robotics undergraduate curriculum

Year 1 is broad foundation: Engineering Mathematics I and II (linear algebra, multivariable calculus, ordinary differential equations), Physics for Engineers, Introductory Programming in Python and C++, Engineering Graphics and CAD, and an introduction-to-engineering profession seminar. PolyU and HKUST run Year 1 as a common engineering year; CUHK and CityU let students declare a stream at the end of Year 1.

Year 2 introduces the robotics core. Compulsory subjects across the four universities converge on the same five blocks: classical mechanics and dynamics, electrical circuits and electronics, embedded systems (typically taught on STM32 or Arduino plus a Raspberry Pi or NVIDIA Jetson), feedback control (PID, state-space) and a hands-on robot build course such as PolyU's ME2026 Mechatronic Design Project or CUHK's MAEG2050.

Year 3 layers the advanced robotics curriculum: robot kinematics and dynamics (Denavit-Hartenberg parameters, Jacobians, inverse kinematics), motion planning (RRT*, A*, MPC), machine vision and SLAM (OpenCV, ORB-SLAM, RTAB-Map), machine learning for robotics (reinforcement learning, imitation learning), and an industrial-robotics applications course taught with KUKA, ABB and UR5/UR10 arms. Most programmes mandate a summer internship between Year 3 and Year 4 of at least eight weeks.

Year 4 is the capstone year. Students take a two-semester Robot Engineering Final Year Project (FYP) — typically a working robot platform delivered to a real customer (a manufacturer, a hospital, an InnoHK centre or an HKSTP startup) — plus electives in surgical robotics, soft robotics, swarm robotics or autonomous driving. PolyU and CUHK both require the FYP to be presented at a public exhibition; CUHK FYPs in MAEG routinely feed into the IROS and ICRA student competitions.

## The competition route: RoboMaster, ABU Robocon and beyond

Hong Kong's robotics culture is competition-driven. The two flagship contests are RoboMaster (run by DJI in Shenzhen) and ABU Robocon (the Asia-Pacific intercollegiate contest). HKUST Team ENTERPRIZE has won the RoboMaster international title in 2017, 2018, 2019, 2023 and 2024 — a record unmatched by any other university in the world. Membership of ENTERPRIZE is a near-guaranteed pipeline into DJI's graduate engineering programme.

ABU Robocon 2025 was held in Ulaanbaatar, Mongolia in August 2025 with a "Robot Basketball" theme. HKU won the local Hong Kong contest and represented the city; Team Hong Kong, China finished as 1st Runner-up at the international finals. Hong Kong will host the ABU Robocon 2026 international event — the second time the city has hosted since 2012 — and HKSTP and RTHK will co-organise. For DSE entrants, joining the university Robocon or RoboMaster team in Year 1 is the single highest-leverage move you can make in this major: the teams take freshers, the work is project-driven, and recruiters from DJI, ASMPT and the InnoHK centres recruit directly out of the team rosters.

Other relevant contests include the MATE Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV) Competition, the Asian Intelligent Robot Contest, the IEEE Region 10 Robotics Competition and the DJI RoboMaster Youth Challenge (a feeder track now linked to secondary-school Applied Learning STEM programmes).

## Internships, graduate starting salaries and the DJI talent pipeline

The high-leverage robotics internships in Hong Kong cluster around five employers. ASMPT runs a structured 12-week summer internship out of its Tai Po facility, focused on cleanroom assembly automation and machine-vision inspection. FANUC's Hong Kong office offers PLC and industrial-robot integration internships. ABB Hong Kong recruits Year-3 interns into its Robotics & Discrete Automation business. The MTR Corporation hires Year-3 engineering interns into its Stations Automation team for predictive-maintenance and autonomous-cleaning-robot projects. AAHK (Airport Authority Hong Kong) recruits robotics interns into the SkyDeck baggage automation and autonomous-vehicle apron pilots.

DJI runs a dedicated Greater Bay Area campus-recruitment channel that targets HKUST, CUHK, PolyU and CityU robotics-track students. Year-3 interns at DJI typically rotate through gimbal control, flight-control firmware, computer vision or SDK engineering. Conversion rates from internship to full-time graduate offer are above 50 percent for HKUST RoboMaster team members.

Graduate starting salaries vary widely by employer and stream. Industry data for 2025 places entry-level robotics engineers (1–3 years) in Hong Kong at an average of around HK$487,000 per year, with new-graduate roles in the HK$19,000–25,000 monthly band. Tier-1 employers pay above this: ASMPT graduate engineers start around HK$28,000–32,000 monthly with a 13th-month and performance bonus; DJI graduate hires (typically based in Shenzhen but recruited from Hong Kong) start at RMB 28,000–35,000 monthly plus equity-style bonus; the InnoHK research centres (COCHE, MRC, HKCLR) pay HK$26,000–30,000 monthly for Research Assistant I positions that double as MPhil pipelines.

## HKIE professional registration and the Mechanical Scheme A route

All four robotics undergraduate routes — PolyU JS3741, CUHK MAEG JS4408, CityU JS1207 and HKUST BEng — are accredited by the Hong Kong Institution of Engineers (HKIE) under Scheme A in the Mechanical discipline. Graduates who follow Scheme A complete a three-year structured Graduate Training (often hosted by Scheme A employers such as MTR, ASMPT, HAECO, AAHK, the EMSD or the Drainage Services Department), then pass the HKIE Professional Assessment to become Members (MHKIE), which carries reciprocal Chartered Engineer status under the Washington Accord. The HKIE Mechanical discipline accreditation is what makes a Hong Kong robotics degree directly portable to Australia (Engineers Australia), the UK (IMechE), Canada and Singapore.

For students aiming at chartered status by age 26, the typical pipeline is: BEng (4 years) → 3 years of Scheme A Graduate Training at an HKIE-recognised employer → Professional Assessment (interview, written reports, technical presentation) → MHKIE registration.

## Postgraduate articulation: ETH Zürich, CMU Robotics Institute, Imperial College and the local MPhil track

Hong Kong robotics undergraduates feed three main postgraduate channels. The local MPhil track — at CUHK MAE, HKUST ECE/MAE, PolyU ME and CityU ME — is the standard route for students who want to stay in Hong Kong and lever into a PhD or InnoHK research role; the MPhil takes two years, is fully funded by a Postgraduate Studentship (around HK$18,925 monthly in 2025/26), and is the entry point to the Hong Kong PhD Fellowship Scheme.

The European master's track is dominated by ETH Zürich's MSc in Robotics, Systems and Control (RSC), TU Delft's MSc in Robotics, EPFL's MSc in Robotics and Imperial College London's MSc in Human and Biological Robotics. ETH RSC accepts five to ten Hong Kong applicants per year, almost entirely from HKUST, CUHK MAEG and HKU MechE; the programme is taught in English, fees are around CHF 1,500 per year, and ETH has a long-standing exchange agreement with HKUST.

The North American track is led by Carnegie Mellon Robotics Institute's MSR (Master of Science in Robotics) and MRSD (Master of Science in Robotic Systems Development) programmes, Stanford's MS in Mechanical Engineering with a robotics focus, MIT's MEng in EECS and Georgia Tech's MS Robotics. CMU RI is the single most prestigious destination, with two-year fully-funded MSR offers going to roughly two to four Hong Kong applicants annually.

## Choosing between the four pathways

If you want "Robotics Engineering" on your degree certificate and you value structured, project-driven hardware training, **PolyU JS3741 Intelligent Robotics Engineering** is the cleanest fit. If you want the deepest research environment with a clear MPhil-to-PhD pipeline and access to the InnoHK Medical Robotics centre, **CUHK MAEG (JS4408)** wins. If you want to combine robotics with strong cross-disciplinary InnoHK exposure (logistics robotics, cardiovascular engineering), **CityU JS1207** offers the most flexible Year-3 electives. If you want elite international competition exposure and the highest probability of a DJI / RoboMaster pipeline, **HKUST** (any engineering BEng + Robotics Minor + ENTERPRIZE membership) remains the strongest brand.

Whatever you choose, the differentiator is not the degree title — it is whether you join a competition team in Year 1, secure a tier-1 internship in summer of Year 3 and finish a meaningful FYP. The robotics industry in Hong Kong and the Greater Bay Area is hiring; the candidates with team experience plus a real-world capstone walk into offers.
