# Hong Kong Journalism, Communication & Media Bachelor Complete Guide 2026

Hong Kong is one of Asia's great media cities — home to international broadcasters, regional bureaus of global news organisations, a dense public-relations and advertising industry, and a fast-growing digital-content economy. For DSE and international students who want to tell stories, shape public opinion, build brands or work at the intersection of media and technology, a journalism or communication degree from a leading Hong Kong university is a strong, career-flexible choice. This guide covers the JUPAS codes, admission requirements, curriculum design, programme differences, portfolio and interview expectations, and the realistic career outcomes you should plan around for the 2026 cycle.

## Why study communication in Hong Kong

Communication is a broad family of disciplines: journalism, public relations, advertising, broadcasting, film and television, digital media, organisational communication and media studies. Hong Kong's appeal is threefold. First, it is a free-flowing information hub with a bilingual (Cantonese, English and increasingly Mandarin) media market that gives students unusual access to both Greater China and international stories. Second, its communication schools are research-intensive and globally ranked — HKBU and CUHK in particular appear consistently among the top communication faculties in Asia. Third, the city's PR, advertising and corporate-communications sector is large relative to its population, so graduates are not limited to newsroom jobs; the majority of communication graduates move into branding, marketing, corporate affairs and content roles.

The field is also being reshaped by artificial intelligence. Automated writing, generative content, audience analytics and verification tools have changed what employers expect from new graduates. Hong Kong's universities have responded by embedding data and AI literacy into the curriculum — most visibly in HKU's new degree, which puts AI in the programme title itself.

## The main JUPAS programmes for 2026

### Hong Kong Baptist University — JS2310 (Bachelor of Communication)

HKBU's School of Communication and Film is the best-known communication school in Hong Kong and one of the oldest in the region. **JS2310 — Bachelor of Communication (Hons)** offers two majors: *Journalism and Digital Media* and *Public Relations and Advertising*. Admission is broad-based: students enter the Bachelor of Communication, then select their major and concentration after a common first year. HKBU also runs separate film, cinema and animation programmes for students interested in moving-image production rather than journalism or strategic communication.

HKBU is the volume leader in communication education and has the strongest alumni network across Hong Kong newsrooms, PR agencies and broadcasters. Its journalism stream is known for hands-on reporting and a working student newsroom; its PR and advertising stream feeds directly into the agency world.

### The Chinese University of Hong Kong — JS4850 (Journalism and Communication)

CUHK's School of Journalism and Communication is the other research powerhouse, with strengths in journalism, advertising, corporate communication, creative media and new-media research. **JS4850 — Journalism and Communication** is a four-year degree with around 42 first-year places and a first-year tuition fee of HK$47,000 for the 2026 cycle. CUHK's programme is academically rigorous and theory-rich while still offering professional concentrations, and it benefits from a strong bilingual environment and a well-resourced media centre.

### The University of Hong Kong — JS6822 (Bachelor of Journalism, Media and Artificial Intelligence)

HKU has restructured its long-running journalism degree into the **Bachelor of Journalism, Media and Artificial Intelligence (JS6822)** — a technology-driven, double-major curriculum that integrates core journalistic training with data, computational methods and AI tools. It is a four-year programme with a small, selective intake (around 19 first-year places), a first-year tuition fee of HK$47,000, and selective interviews. HKU explicitly considers school principals' reports, extra-curricular records and campus-journalism experience, so demonstrated involvement in student media is a meaningful advantage here. (Note: HKU lists this programme under both JS6822 and, in some earlier materials, JS6822-family codes — always confirm the current code on the JUPAS site before finalising your choices.)

### City University of Hong Kong — Department of Media and Communication

CityU's Department of Media and Communication offers degrees in media and communication and in creative media (the School of Creative Media is internationally recognised for digital and interactive media art). CityU's strength is the blend of communication theory with technology, interactive design and the creative industries, making it a good fit for students drawn to digital, UX-adjacent and creative-tech career paths rather than traditional reporting.

Other options include PolyU's communication and design-led media offerings and the self-financing sector (for example, Hang Seng University and Shue Yan University both run well-regarded journalism and communication programmes that can be a realistic route for students with mid-range DSE results).

## Admission requirements

All UGC-funded programmes share the **"3-3-2-2-2-2"** minimum: Level 3 in Chinese Language and English Language, Level 2 in Mathematics (Compulsory Part), "Attained" in Citizenship and Social Development, and Level 2 in two elective subjects. For communication degrees, however, the *minimum* is far below the *competitive* level.

- **English Language is the critical subject.** Communication is a language-intensive discipline, and English-stream concentrations and international newsroom work require strong English. Competitive applicants typically present Level 4–5 in English; some programmes weight English more heavily than other subjects in their score calculation.
- **Chinese Language matters for Cantonese/Chinese-medium journalism streams**, where reporting and writing are done in Chinese. International students applying through non-JUPAS routes should check whether an alternative language qualification is accepted.
- **No specific elective subjects are required.** Communication welcomes students from arts, science and business backgrounds. Subjects such as English Literature, History, Geography, Economics, BAFS, Visual Arts and ICT all support the discipline, but admissions tutors care more about language ability and demonstrated interest than a particular subject mix.
- **Median admission scores** are not published as fixed cut-offs and vary year to year; in recent cycles the great majority of offers at HKBU, CUHK and HKU went to Band A applicants with upper-mid to high "best-five/best-six" scores. Treat published past scores as reference only — band placement, subject results and interview performance all feed the final decision.

International applicants (IB, A-Level, SAT/AP) apply through each university's non-JUPAS international admissions scheme and should target equivalent strong-English profiles; many communication schools also accept transfer and associate-degree articulation entrants.

## Portfolios, interviews and the application edge

Communication is one of the few fields where what you have *done* can matter as much as your exam scores. Programmes that interview — HKU notably, and HKBU and CUHK for borderline or specialised cases — look for:

- **Evidence of media activity:** campus newspaper or magazine work, podcasting, video, photography, social-media content with real reach, debate, public speaking, or a personal blog or portfolio.
- **News awareness:** the ability to discuss current affairs in Hong Kong and internationally with judgement, not just opinion.
- **Communication skills under pressure:** clarity, structure and confidence in spoken English (and Chinese where relevant).
- **A genuine "why":** admissions tutors can quickly tell the difference between an applicant who consumes media and one who only wants a flexible degree.

Build a portfolio early. Even modest, self-initiated work — a school news site, an Instagram explainer series, a short documentary — is more persuasive than claimed interest. For HKU's AI-focused degree, showing any data or technical curiosity (a scraped dataset, a chart-driven story, an experiment with AI tools) signals fit.

## Curriculum: what you actually study

A typical Hong Kong communication degree runs four years and is structured around a common foundation, then specialisation:

- **Year 1 — Foundation:** communication theory, media and society, news writing, visual communication, media law and ethics, plus university-wide language and general-education requirements.
- **Years 2–3 — Major and concentration:** depending on stream, this includes reporting and editing, multimedia and data journalism, broadcast/video production, public relations strategy, advertising and campaign planning, corporate and crisis communication, audience analytics, and media research methods.
- **Years 3–4 — Capstone and internship:** a professional internship (often with a news organisation, agency or corporate communications team), plus a capstone project — a published investigative piece, a full campaign, a documentary, or an applied research thesis.

Production facilities — TV studios, radio and podcast suites, editing labs and media-innovation centres — are a real differentiator between schools, and the strongest programmes integrate AI-assisted reporting, verification, audience-data and content-automation tools directly into coursework rather than treating them as electives.

## Career outcomes

The single most important thing to understand is that a communication degree is *not* a one-way ticket to a newsroom. In practice, graduates spread across:

- **Public relations and corporate communications** — agencies and in-house teams; consistently the largest employer of communication graduates in Hong Kong, with the clearest salary progression.
- **Advertising, marketing and brand/content** — creative agencies, digital marketing, social-media management, content strategy.
- **Journalism and broadcasting** — newspapers, wire services, TV and radio, digital-native outlets; competitive and lower-paid at entry, but a strong launchpad for analytical and writing careers.
- **Digital, social and creative media** — platform content, video production, UX writing, creative-tech roles (especially for CityU creative-media and HKU AI-track graduates).
- **Government, NGO and public-affairs roles** — information officers, policy communication, advocacy.

Starting salaries vary widely by sector: agency PR and journalism entry roles tend to be modest, while corporate communications, in-house digital marketing and tech-company content roles pay more and scale faster. The graduates who do best treat the degree as a platform for a portfolio and a network, not just a credential — internships, freelance bylines and side projects during study are the strongest predictors of a good first job.

## Choosing between the schools

A simple way to decide:

- **HKBU (JS2310)** — choose if you want the deepest professional newsroom and agency network, the broadest alumni base, and a clear journalism-vs-PR/advertising fork.
- **CUHK (JS4850)** — choose if you want the most academically rigorous, research-strong programme with strong bilingual training and a wide range of concentrations.
- **HKU (JS6822)** — choose if you are excited by the media-plus-AI direction, want a small selective cohort, and can show technical or data curiosity.
- **CityU** — choose if your interest leans digital, interactive and creative-tech rather than traditional reporting.
- **Self-financing (HSUHK, Shue Yan)** — realistic, well-regarded options if your DSE results sit below the funded-programme competitive range.

Whichever route you take, communication rewards initiative more than almost any other major. Strong English, demonstrated media work, sharp news judgement and a willingness to learn data and AI tools will matter more over your career than the small differences between admission scores. Confirm all JUPAS codes, places and fees on the official JUPAS and university admissions sites before you lock in your 2026 choices, as programme structures in this field are changing quickly.
