# Hong Kong Nursing Bachelor's Programmes Complete Guide 2026

Nursing is one of the most consistently sought-after JUPAS choices in Hong Kong. The territory faces a chronic shortage of registered nurses — the Hospital Authority, private hospitals and an ageing population create a near-guaranteed pipeline of jobs for qualified graduates. For DSE candidates with mid-to-upper-band scores, a strong interest in healthcare and the patience for five years of clinical training, a Bachelor of Nursing is one of the most secure career pathways available outside of medicine.

This guide compares the four universities offering full nursing bachelor's degrees in Hong Kong — The University of Hong Kong (HKU), The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK), The Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU) and Hong Kong Metropolitan University (HKMU) — and walks through admission requirements, tuition, the Nursing Council Licensing Examination, and what graduate life actually looks like in the Hospital Authority and beyond.

## Why study nursing in Hong Kong?

Three structural facts make nursing an unusually high-leverage degree in Hong Kong.

First, **demand is locked in by demographics**. By the early 2030s, more than one in three Hong Kong residents will be aged 65 or above. Public hospitals, private hospitals, residential care homes, community health centres and specialist clinics are all expanding nursing teams, and the government has explicitly identified nursing as a priority manpower area.

Second, **starting salaries are competitive**. New graduates joining the Hospital Authority as Registered Nurses (RNs) start on the master pay scale at roughly HK$40,000–46,000 per month, with a defined ladder up through Advanced Practice Nurse, Nursing Officer, Ward Manager and beyond. Annual leave, training subsidies and a non-contributory provident fund add meaningful value on top of the cash salary.

Third, **the qualification is portable**. A Hong Kong RN registration is well respected in Singapore, the UK, Australia, Canada and the Middle East. Many graduates choose to spend two or three years in HA, sit a bridging exam such as the NMC OSCE in the UK or the NCLEX-RN in North America, and move overseas with significant clinical experience already on their CV.

## HKU — Bachelor of Nursing (JS6468 / JS6418)

HKU's School of Nursing sits within the Li Ka Shing Faculty of Medicine and is the smallest of the four programmes by faculty size but draws on the resources of the Queen Mary Hospital teaching cluster. The programme is a five-year full-time honours degree comprising 46 courses across foundational sciences (anatomy, physiology, pharmacology), nursing practice, clinical placements and a final-year capstone or research project.

In 2024/25 HKU launched an **Advanced Leadership Track (JS6418)** alongside the standard JS6468. The Leadership Track admits a smaller number of high-achieving students who follow the same clinical curriculum but receive additional management, research-methods and global-health training, with a guaranteed overseas exchange or internship at a partner institution. Both codes share the 210-seat intake.

Calculation of programme score uses Best 5 with no subject weighting, and applicants must take English, Chinese, Maths and Citizenship & Social Development as the four core subjects. Selection interviews are required for shortlisted candidates, and good working knowledge of Cantonese and English is essential because most clinical placements are in Cantonese-speaking wards.

The 2025 admission score for JS6468 sat in the upper-low-30s on Best 5 (out of 35 with 4-core+2-elective), broadly competitive with CUHK Nursing.

## CUHK — Bachelor of Nursing (JS4513)

CUHK's Nethersole School of Nursing is the largest of the three traditional university programmes by graduating cohort and is consistently ranked as one of the top five most popular JUPAS choices among all CUHK programmes. Clinical placements run primarily through the Prince of Wales Hospital, which is the New Territories East Cluster's flagship and one of the largest acute hospitals in Hong Kong.

CUHK uses 4C+2X weighting for its Best 5 calculation in Nursing, giving extra weight to core subjects. Biology is **not** strictly required, but most successful applicants have taken Biology or Chemistry as an elective, since these subjects directly feed into Year 1 Anatomy and Physiology. The programme integrates simulation-lab teaching, problem-based learning and a mandatory community-health placement, and offers electives in geriatric, paediatric, mental-health and critical-care nursing in later years.

CUHK Nursing's 2025 admission score sat in the low-to-mid 30s for the Best 5 4C+2X calculation. Lower-quartile and median figures vary slightly year on year — applicants should consult the CUHK admissions office's published *Useful Information for JUPAS Applicants* booklet for current data.

## PolyU — School of Nursing

PolyU's School of Nursing is the largest by intake — combined enrolment across JS3648 (general) and JS3337 (mental health) exceeds 260 students per year. PolyU has the deepest historical roots in Hong Kong nursing education, having trained nurses since the 1970s through its predecessor Hong Kong Polytechnic, and runs the most extensive clinical-placement network spanning Hospital Authority hospitals across all seven clusters.

### BSc (Hons) in Nursing (JS3648)

The general nursing track requires Level 3 or above in at least two HKDSE Category A elective subjects, but no compulsory science subject. PolyU uses subject weighting in its score calculation: English, Maths and a science elective (Biology, Chemistry, Physics or Combined Science) typically receive higher weights, which means strong performers in sciences are at a real advantage despite the absence of a hard prerequisite.

In 2024/25 the average admission score for JS3648 was approximately 212.5 (weighted, on a scale roughly comparable to Best 5 × 6 with subject multipliers). The programme launched a new **Global Nursing Leader Distinction** in 2025/26 — high-achieving entrants are invited to participate in WHO Collaborating Centre exchanges in Europe, North America or Australia.

### BSc (Hons) in Mental Health Nursing (JS3337)

PolyU is the **only** university in Hong Kong offering an undergraduate degree dedicated to psychiatric nursing. Graduates are eligible to register as Psychiatric RNs and typically work in psychiatric hospitals (Castle Peak, Kwai Chung, Siu Lam), community mental-health teams and psychiatric units of acute hospitals.

The 2024/25 average admission score was approximately 212.5 — the same band as the general nursing programme, though the smaller 70-place intake means competition can fluctuate sharply between cycles. The programme includes substantial training in psychotherapy basics, addictions nursing, forensic mental health and child & adolescent psychiatry, alongside the general nursing core.

## HKMU — Bachelor of Nursing with Honours (SSSDP, JSSU40)

Hong Kong Metropolitan University (formerly OUHK) is a self-financing institution but its nursing programme is **subsidised under the Study Subsidy Scheme for Designated Professions/Sectors (SSSDP)** — meaning students pay only roughly the same tuition as UGC-funded programmes after the government subsidy is applied. The annual tuition after the SSSDP subsidy of around HK$89,620 is approximately HK$55,150.

HKMU's nursing programme is the largest by absolute intake (around 280 places per year combined across General Health Care and Mental Health pathways), and admission scores are typically lower than the three traditional universities, making it the realistic primary target for applicants in the mid-band-B range. The clinical placement network is extensive — HKMU has formal partnerships with both public and private hospital groups.

HKMU also offers a separate **Higher Diploma in Nursing (HDN)** sub-degree route. HDN graduates can qualify as Enrolled Nurses (ENs), and many later articulate into the bachelor's programme through Pathway 2 to upgrade to RN.

## The Nursing Council Licensing Examination

A bachelor's degree alone does not automatically license you to practise. All graduates must pass the **Nursing Council of Hong Kong Licensing Examination** for either Registration (RN) or Enrolment (EN). Although graduates of the four accredited bachelor programmes are typically granted a streamlined route, all candidates must meet the Council's eligibility standards.

### Examination structure

- **Written part** — multiple-choice and short-answer paper covering general nursing knowledge, clinical practice, ethics and legislation
- **Practical part** — OSCE-style clinical skills assessment, conducted only after the written part is passed
- **Fees (2026)** — HK$860 for RN registration, HK$920 for EN enrolment

### Important rules

Candidates have a maximum of **three attempts**. After three failures, re-sitting requires special permission from the Council. Applicants must make their first attempt within two years of receiving Council approval. Applications submitted by end-October each year are assessed for the following year's examination.

For the four accredited bachelor's programmes, the curriculum is designed so that graduates pass on the first attempt at very high rates — typically above 95%.

## Career outcomes and salary

### Hospital Authority entry

The Hospital Authority is by far the largest employer of nurses in Hong Kong, recruiting the bulk of each graduating cohort. The HA runs a **centralised recruitment exercise** every year and graduates from the four bachelor programmes are typically all but guaranteed a place if they pass the licensing exam.

Starting salary for a Registered Nurse on the HA master pay scale is approximately **HK$40,000–46,000 per month**, including allowances. Average total compensation in the first year (with overtime, on-call and shift allowances) commonly reaches HK$55,000+ per month. Public-sector nurses also receive 21–28 days of annual leave, paid sick leave, an Examination Leave allowance for further qualifications, and an attractive non-contributory provident fund after vesting.

### Specialisation and career ladder

After 2–3 years of bedside experience, RNs can apply for specialisation in areas such as:

- **Critical care** — ICU, CCU, A&E
- **Operating theatre / perioperative**
- **Oncology**
- **Paediatric and neonatal**
- **Mental health and community psychiatric**
- **Midwifery** — requires additional 18-month training to qualify as a Registered Midwife
- **Community and public health nursing**

The HA career ladder progresses RN → Advanced Practice Nurse (APN) → Nursing Officer (NO) → Ward Manager (WM) → Department Operations Manager (DOM). A Nursing Officer typically earns HK$65,000–80,000 monthly, and Ward Managers HK$90,000–110,000+.

### Private sector and overseas

Private hospitals (Hong Kong Adventist, Matilda, Gleneagles, Hong Kong Sanatorium, etc.) offer comparable or slightly higher base salaries and more predictable hours. International work is well established — Hong Kong RN registration is highly regarded, and bridging routes into the UK NMC, Australian AHPRA and Canadian/US licensure are well documented.

## Choosing between the four programmes

For top-band applicants (Best 5 ~33+), **HKU** and **CUHK** are the prestige choices and offer the strongest research-track, postgraduate and overseas-mobility links. HKU's new Advanced Leadership Track is worth shortlisting for students considering hospital management or academic careers.

For applicants with strong subject scores and an interest in **specialisation**, **PolyU** has unmatched depth — its mental-health track is unique in Hong Kong, and its general programme has the broadest clinical-placement network across the territory. PolyU is also the strongest choice for students who want to start clinical exposure early, as the curriculum front-loads ward-based learning.

For mid-band-B applicants, **HKMU** is the realistic primary target. The SSSDP subsidy makes tuition essentially equivalent to UGC universities, the clinical training is rigorous and Council-accredited, and graduates are recruited by the Hospital Authority on the same terms as those from HKU/CUHK/PolyU. The qualification at the end is the same RN registration.

## Practical tips for DSE applicants

**Take Biology** — even where it is not strictly required (PolyU JS3648, CUHK JS4513), Biology is the single best preparation for the Year 1 anatomy and physiology load. Chemistry is the second-best choice. Avoid taking neither.

**Demonstrate genuine interest before the interview**. Volunteer at a hospital, elderly home or community-care NGO. Shadow a working nurse if family connections allow. Read widely about Hong Kong's healthcare system, the public-private mix and the role of NGOs. Interviewers ask "why nursing?" and the strongest answers come from real exposure.

**Prepare for shift work**. Five years of clinical placements include night shifts, weekend rotations and physically demanding wards. Realistic self-assessment matters — students who underestimate the physical and emotional demands often do not finish the programme.

**Consider the Higher Diploma route as a fallback**, not as a first choice. HKMU's HDN is a legitimate path to EN registration with later articulation to RN, but it adds 2–3 years compared to the direct bachelor's route. For most candidates capable of nursing, aiming for one of the four bachelor programmes is the better play.

## Conclusion

A nursing bachelor's degree in Hong Kong is one of the most reliable returns on five years of study available in the JUPAS system. Demand is locked in by demography, starting salaries are strong, the qualification travels internationally, and the clinical careers are deep — there are paths into management, research, education, midwifery and advanced practice for those who want them. Whether you target HKU's research-intensive programme, CUHK's broad-spectrum clinical training, PolyU's specialised tracks or HKMU's accessible SSSDP route, the destination is the same — registration as a Registered Nurse with the Nursing Council of Hong Kong, and a career with rare combination of meaning and security.

For applicants weighing nursing against medicine, dentistry or pharmacy, the trade-off is real but worth thinking through carefully — five years versus six, lower DSE entry threshold but a profession whose autonomy, scope of practice and earning ceiling have all been expanding for two decades. For the right student, nursing is not a fallback. It is the answer.
