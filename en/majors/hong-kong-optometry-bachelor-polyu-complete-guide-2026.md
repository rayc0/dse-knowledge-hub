# Hong Kong Optometry Bachelor (PolyU JS3290) Complete Guide 2026

If you want to become a licensed optometrist in Hong Kong, there is exactly one undergraduate route: the **Bachelor of Science (Honours) in Optometry** at the Hong Kong Polytechnic University (PolyU), JUPAS code **JS3290**. No other UGC-funded university in Hong Kong runs an optometry degree, and no overseas optometry qualification is automatically recognised by the Optometrists Board for Part I registration without further assessment. This makes JS3290 the single most strategic — and most competitive — entry point into the profession.

This guide walks DSE candidates and Non-JUPAS applicants through everything that matters in 2026: admission scoring, the five-year curriculum, the Optometrists Board licensing system, clinical training, tuition, graduate salaries and overseas recognition.

## Why Optometry, and Why Only PolyU

In Hong Kong the optometry profession is regulated under the **Supplementary Medical Professions Ordinance (Cap. 359)**. The Optometrists Board maintains four parts on the licence register (Parts I–IV), with **Part I** being the highest tier. Only Part I optometrists are authorised to perform comprehensive eye examinations, use diagnostic pharmaceutical agents, and provide the full scope of primary eye-care services that include detection of ocular disease, binocular vision assessment and contact lens fitting.

The PolyU School of Optometry was established in 1979 and remains the only institution in Hong Kong whose undergraduates are eligible for **direct entry to Part I** of the Optometrists Register on graduation. Holders of overseas optometry degrees — even from accredited US, UK or Australian programmes — must apply individually and may be required to sit licensing examinations or complete supervised practice before being granted Part I status.

The practical consequence: if your goal is to practise as a licensed optometrist in Hong Kong before age 25, JS3290 is by far the fastest route.

## Programme Snapshot 2026

- **JUPAS code:** JS3290
- **Award:** Bachelor of Science (Honours) in Optometry
- **Duration:** 5 years full-time
- **Total credits:** 152 (including 20 clinical/field-experience credits)
- **Intake quota:** 60 places (combined JUPAS + Non-JUPAS + International)
- **Funding:** Government-funded (UGC)
- **2025/26 average HKDSE admission score:** 260.6 (Best 5 + 6th-subject bonus)
- **Entry type:** Direct entry to Year 1 (not a broad-based scheme)
- **Tuition (2026/27):** HK$44,500 per year for local government-funded students (subject to Government adjustment); non-local fees substantially higher
- **Professional outcome:** Eligible for direct registration as a Part I optometrist with the Optometrists Board of Hong Kong

The 60-place quota is one of the smallest among UGC-funded professional programmes in Hong Kong — comparable to CityU's Bachelor of Veterinary Medicine and significantly tighter than nursing or pharmacy. Combined with the fact that direct registration as a Part I optometrist is reserved for PolyU graduates, this scarcity drives admission scores up consistently to the high 250s and low 260s.

## JUPAS Admission Requirements

### Minimum requirements

JS3290 follows PolyU's **General Entrance Requirements**:

- Chinese Language: Level 3
- English Language: Level 3
- Mathematics (Compulsory Part): Level 2
- Citizenship and Social Development: Attained
- Two elective subjects: Level 3 each (M1/M2 counted as elective)

These minimums get you considered. They will not get you offered.

### Score calculation (2026)

PolyU calculates the JS3290 admission score using:

**Any best 5 subjects + bonus score for the 6th subject if at Level 3 or above.**

Conversion: Level 5** = 8.5, 5* = 7, 5 = 5.5, 4 = 4, 3 = 3, 2 = 2, 1 = 1. There are no compulsory subjects, but PolyU applies positive weighting on **preferred subjects**.

### Preferred subjects with positive weighting

- Chinese Language
- English Language
- Mathematics (Compulsory Part)
- Mathematics Extended Module 1 (Calculus and Statistics)
- Mathematics Extended Module 2 (Algebra and Calculus)
- Biology
- Chemistry
- Physics
- Combined Science (any two of Bio/Chem/Phys)

The strongest profiles take **two of Biology / Chemistry / Physics**, plus M1 or M2. Biology + Chemistry is the most common combination because both feed directly into the Year 1–2 visual science modules; Physics is highly useful for the optics components.

### Realistic competitive scores

For DSE 2025 admissions, the Best-5 + 6th-subject median score for offered candidates clustered around **27–30** under the standard 5**=7 conversion (or 260+ under the 8.5-scale). Practically, 5*5*5*55 across English, Chinese, Maths and two science subjects is a comfortable Band A1 profile. 5544 + 5 in Liberal Studies / CS will likely fall short of the cut-off in a typical year.

### Special physical and language requirements

PolyU explicitly requires applicants to "possess the sensory and motor skills necessary to perform an eye examination". This is not a screening filter at the JUPAS stage, but candidates with severe colour vision deficiency, significant tremor or motor impairment should consult the School of Optometry before applying — clinical training requires **fine bimanual coordination** for slit-lamp work, contact-lens fitting and instillation of eye drops.

The programme also expects students to develop **good Cantonese skills before starting clinical placements**, because the majority of clinic patients are Cantonese-speaking Hong Kong residents. International and Mainland students are admitted but should plan early Cantonese acquisition.

## Non-JUPAS and International Applicants

Non-local applicants apply directly to PolyU (not via JUPAS). Required components:

- **Academic credentials:** Gaokao with strong scores in Chinese, English, Maths and at least one science (typically 50+ above first-tier line); IB 36+ with HL sciences; A-Levels AAA including two of Bio/Chem/Phys/Maths; or equivalent
- **English proficiency:** IELTS 6.5 (no band below 6.0) or TOEFL iBT 80
- **Personal Statement:** Maximum 500 words explaining motivation for optometry
- **Reference letter:** Required for international candidates
- **Interview:** 15-minute individual interview conducted in English

The interview probes motivation, basic understanding of the optometric scope of practice, ethical reasoning around patient care, and manual dexterity awareness. Candidates are routinely asked the difference between an optometrist, an ophthalmologist and an optician — a surprising number of applicants conflate the three.

## Five-Year Curriculum: What You Actually Study

The 152-credit programme is divided into a science-foundation phase, a clinical-skills phase and a clinical-immersion phase.

### Year 1 — Foundation

- General University Requirements (English, Chinese, Service Learning, GE clusters)
- Human Anatomy and Physiology
- General and Visual Optics
- Geometric and Physical Optics
- Introduction to Optometry and Visual Science

Year 1 is heavy on optics: students learn how light refracts, how spherical and cylindrical lenses correct ametropia, and how the eye's optical system forms images on the retina. Expect mathematics-intensive problem sets.

### Year 2 — Visual Science

- Ocular Anatomy and Physiology
- Visual Perception and Neuroscience
- Optometric Instrumentation
- General Pathology and Microbiology
- Pharmacology for Optometrists

This is the year where the discipline narrows from "applied physics" to "applied biomedical science". Pharmacology is particularly important: Part I optometrists are authorised to instil diagnostic agents (mydriatics, cycloplegics, topical anaesthetics) and need to understand drug interactions, contraindications and adverse reactions.

### Year 3 — Clinical Techniques

- Refraction and Subjective Examination
- Binocular Vision and Orthoptics
- Contact Lens Theory and Fitting
- Ocular Disease I (anterior segment)
- Paediatric Optometry

Year 3 is where students first work on each other and on volunteer patients in the **Skills Laboratory** under tight supervision. Practical assessments are continuous — every clinical examination skill is signed off individually before students progress.

### Year 4 — Clinical Practice and Specialisation

- Ocular Disease II (posterior segment, glaucoma, retinal disease)
- Low Vision and Visual Rehabilitation
- Geriatric Optometry
- Public Health and Occupational Optometry
- Clinical Practice (real patients, supervised)

Students start their **clinical placements at the PolyU Optometry Clinic** — a fully operational primary-care eye clinic on the PolyU Hung Hom campus that sees thousands of public patients each year. Patient bookings span paediatric vision screening, contact lens fitting, low-vision assessment and ocular disease management.

### Year 5 — Internship and Capstone

- Five semesters of cumulative supervised clinical training (continued from Year 4)
- Honours Project (research thesis or extended clinical case study)
- Advanced Clinical Optometry
- Professional Practice Management
- Elective rotations: Hospital Authority eye clinics, paediatric specialty clinics, low-vision services, sports vision, satellite community clinics

Final-year students take primary responsibility for managing their own patient panels under faculty supervision. This is functionally a residency-light experience and is the reason PolyU graduates are deemed exam-ready for Part I registration on graduation rather than after a separate licensing year.

## The Optometrists Board Part I Registration

PolyU's BSc (Hons) in Optometry is recognised by the Optometrists Board of Hong Kong for **direct entry to Part I of the optometrist register** under the Supplementary Medical Professions Ordinance. Graduates apply via the eForm system (DH0208) administered by the Department of Health.

Required documentation:

- Original PolyU degree certificate and academic transcript
- Hong Kong ID copy
- Two passport-sized photos
- Application fee
- Continuing Professional Development undertaking

Most graduates receive Part I registration within 4–8 weeks of graduation, allowing them to start full clinical practice the same calendar year they finish the degree. There is no separate national licensing examination — the curriculum's clinical assessments serve that role.

## Clinical Training Infrastructure

Year 4–5 clinical training takes place at:

- **PolyU Optometry Clinic** (Hung Hom main campus) — the largest student-run eye clinic in Hong Kong
- **PolyU Satellite Clinics** in Kowloon and the New Territories serving lower-income communities
- **Hospital Authority eye departments** (rotation slots at Queen Elizabeth Hospital, Hong Kong Eye Hospital and others)
- **Paediatric specialty clinics** for myopia control and amblyopia management
- **Low-vision rehabilitation services** in collaboration with the Hong Kong Society for the Blind

The clinic operates on a real patient-fee model. Students are supervised by registered Part I optometrists and faculty ophthalmologists.

## Tuition, Scholarships and Cost of Study

- **Local UGC-funded tuition:** HK$44,500 per year (2026/27, subject to Government revision)
- **Non-local tuition:** HK$145,000–171,000 per year depending on cohort
- **Five-year total local cost of study:** approximately HK$222,500 in tuition + accommodation and living
- **Scholarships:** PolyU Entrance Scholarships (up to full tuition for top JUPAS scorers), Hong Kong Government's Student Finance Office TSFS/NLSFT loans, HKSAR Government Scholarship Fund, and a small number of dedicated Optometry alumni scholarships

Compared with overseas optometry — a four-year US OD typically costs US$120,000–200,000 in tuition alone — PolyU's local-fee track is a remarkable value proposition.

## Graduate Outcomes and Salaries

The PolyU School of Optometry consistently reports near-100% employment within three months of graduation. Typical first-job destinations:

- **Private optometry chains:** OPTICAL 88, Puyi Optical, Hong Kong Optical, Vision Express
- **Independent practices:** boutique clinics in Central, Causeway Bay, Sha Tin, Tseung Kwan O
- **Hospital Authority clinical optometrist posts:** salary point 16+ on the HA pay scale
- **In-house optometry roles** at international brands and contact lens manufacturers
- **Department of Health** (school health programme, public eye-care services)

Starting salaries for fresh graduates in 2025 ranged from **HK$25,000 to HK$35,000 per month** in private practice, with senior optometrists in established practices earning HK$50,000–80,000+ after 5–7 years. Hospital Authority optometrists start around point 16 of the HA Master Pay Scale (~HK$36,000 monthly) with structured progression.

A small but growing cohort pursue **postgraduate clinical specialisation**: PolyU offers a Doctor of Optometry (OD) and a residency programme in paediatric, primary care, low vision and ocular disease specialties. Some graduates also pursue a Master of Public Health, an MPhil/PhD in Visual Science, or an MBA for practice management.

## Overseas Recognition

PolyU's BSc (Hons) in Optometry is recognised for licensing — usually with additional examinations or supervised practice — in:

- **Australia** (via Optometry Council of Australia and New Zealand assessment, then OBA registration)
- **United Kingdom** (via the General Optical Council Overseas Registration assessment route)
- **Mainland China** (recognised at provincial registration level; specific bridge programmes in Guangdong)
- **Singapore** (through Allied Health Professions Council case-by-case assessment)

Direct US licensure (state-by-state) generally requires conversion to a Doctor of Optometry, but PolyU graduates routinely gain advanced standing in OD bridge programmes at US schools such as SUNY College of Optometry and the New England College of Optometry.

## Application Strategy and Timeline

For DSE candidates aiming at JS3290 in 2026:

- **Subject selection (Form 4):** lock in two of Biology, Chemistry, Physics, plus M1 or M2 if your school offers it
- **Year 5–6 sprint:** prioritise English, Chinese and the two preferred sciences for Level 5 or above; aim for at least 5*5*5*55 (29 in best-5 + bonus)
- **JUPAS Band A:** if your goal is optometry, JS3290 must sit in Band A (preferably A1). The score cutoff means it does not realistically appear in Band B offers
- **Backup choices:** Pharmacy at CUHK or HKU, Nursing at PolyU/HKU/CUHK, Biomedical Sciences at HKUST, Chinese Medicine at CUHK/HKBU/PolyU
- **Non-JUPAS deadline:** typically late November of the preceding year; secure references and IELTS/TOEFL scores by October

For interview preparation, study the **scope of practice** of a Hong Kong Part I optometrist, read the Optometrists Board Code of Practice, and be ready to articulate why optometry — not medicine, not nursing — fits your career goals.

## Common Misconceptions

**Optometrist vs. ophthalmologist.** An ophthalmologist is a registered medical doctor who has completed medicine, internship and ophthalmology specialty training (MBBS + 6+ years), and can perform eye surgery. An optometrist is a non-medical primary eye-care professional who diagnoses refractive errors and ocular disease and prescribes optical and pharmaceutical treatment, but does not perform surgery.

**Optometrist vs. optician.** Opticians (Parts III/IV) dispense glasses against a prescription written by a Part I optometrist or ophthalmologist; they do not perform full eye examinations.

**"I can study optometry overseas and come back to practise."** Not without the Optometrists Board's case-by-case assessment, possibly an examination, and possibly supervised practice. Students underestimate this barrier every year.

## Bottom Line

PolyU JS3290 is one of the most strategically valuable degrees in Hong Kong: a 60-place, government-funded, profession-licensing programme with near-universal employment and a clear path to a HK$25,000–80,000+ monthly career inside five years of starting Form 6. The trade-off is admission competitiveness — high-260s admission scores in 2025 — and an explicit physical-skills requirement. If your DSE profile is heading toward 5*5*5*55 in English, Chinese, Maths and two of Biology / Chemistry / Physics, and you are comfortable with bimanual clinical work in Cantonese, JS3290 is one of the highest-yield Band A choices on the JUPAS list.
