## 2. Paper 1: Reading Comprehension

Paper 1 tests candidates' ability to read, understand, and respond to a range of English texts including factual articles, opinion pieces, literary extracts, and informational materials.

### 2.1 Part A: Short Texts

Part A typically features **3–5 short texts** (advertisements, notices, charts, emails, short articles) with multiple-choice or short-answer questions.

**Strategy for Part A:**
- Read questions **before** the text — this lets you scan for specific information.
- For notices and advertisements, pay close attention to **conditions**, **exceptions**, and **small print**.
- MC answers for short texts often require you to identify the **main purpose** or **intended audience** — look for clues in the title, format, and register.
- Don't over-think: answers for short text questions are usually directly stated, not implied.

### 2.2 Part B: Extended Reading Texts

Part B includes **two longer passages** (typically 600–900 words each) covering different text types: journalism, literary prose, science communication, or argumentative essays.

#### Question Type Breakdown

| Question Type | Marks | Key Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Vocabulary in context | 2–4 | Use surrounding sentences to deduce meaning; don't rely solely on memorised definitions |
| Factual retrieval | 4–8 | Underline specific details as you read; answer must match text exactly |
| Inference & implication | 6–10 | Look for contrast words (however, yet, despite); answer what the writer *suggests*, not just states |
| Language & style analysis | 6–10 | Name the device → explain its effect → link to the writer's purpose |
| Comparison of two texts | 8–12 | Parallel structure: address both texts for each point; never describe them separately |
| Evaluation / personal response | 6–8 | State your view clearly; support with specific textual evidence; avoid vague generalisations |

#### Reading Strategy: The 4-Step Approach

1. **Preview** (2 min): Read the title, subheadings, and first/last paragraph to get the gist.
2. **Skim-read questions** (3 min): Identify what each question targets (fact, inference, language, etc.).
3. **Active reading** (15–20 min): Read the full text, annotating: circle key claims, underline supporting evidence, mark tone shifts.
4. **Answer strategically** (remaining time): For inference and language questions, always quote briefly from the text before interpreting.

#### Language & Style Analysis Framework

When asked to analyse a literary or rhetorical device:

```
"The writer uses [device] in the phrase '[quote]'.
This creates an impression of [effect/image].
This is effective because [link to writer's purpose/theme]."
```

**High-frequency devices to know:**
- **Metaphor / simile**: creates vivid imagery; compresses complex ideas
- **Rhetorical question**: engages readers; implies the answer is obvious
- **Repetition**: emphasises a point; creates rhythm or urgency
- **Contrast / juxtaposition**: highlights differences; creates irony or tension
- **Listing / tricolon**: builds momentum; gives impression of comprehensiveness
- **Irony / sarcasm**: creates distance from the subject; may critique without direct accusation

**Time allocation (Paper 1, 1 hr 30 min):**
- Part A (short texts): 20–25 minutes
- Part B Text 1: 30–35 minutes
- Part B Text 2: 30–35 minutes
- Review: 5 minutes

## 3. Paper 2: Writing

Paper 2 tests candidates' ability to write for different purposes, audiences, and genres. It has two parts.

### 3.1 Part A: Short Writing Tasks

Part A typically provides **prompts requiring 100–200 word responses** in a specific format: emails, notices, social media posts, short reports, or speech extracts.

**Part A High-Score Strategy:**
1. **Format first**: Use the correct layout (greeting/sign-off for emails, subject line, etc.). Format marks are easy wins.
2. **Tone matching**: Identify the relationship between writer and reader — formal letter to a company vs informal message to a friend.
3. **Task completion**: Tick off every bullet point or requirement in the prompt. Missing even one will cost marks.
4. **Conciseness**: With 100–200 words, every sentence must serve a purpose. Cut filler phrases.

### 3.2 Part B: Extended Writing

Part B requires one longer piece of writing (approximately **400–500 words**) from a choice of tasks. Common genres include:

| Genre | Common Task Types | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Argumentative / Discursive Essay | Discuss the pros and cons; argue for/against a proposition | Clear thesis, balanced evidence, logical connectors |
| Narrative / Personal Recount | Write about an experience; continue a story | Vivid description, character development, narrative arc |
| Descriptive Writing | Describe a place, person, or scene | Sensory detail, figurative language, atmosphere |
| Formal Report / Proposal | Recommend a solution; evaluate options | Headings, formal register, evidence-based recommendations |
| Letter | Write a letter of complaint, persuasion, or advice | Appropriate register, clear purpose, structured paragraphs |
| Speech / Talk | Write a talk for a specific audience | Direct address, rhetorical devices, engaging opening |
| Review | Review a book, film, place, or event | Summary + personal evaluation, specific examples |
| Feature Article | Magazine or newspaper article on a topic | Engaging headline, informative body, variety of sentence structure |

#### The PACT Framework for Every Writing Task

Before writing, ask yourself:
- **P — Purpose**: What am I trying to achieve? (inform, persuade, entertain, advise)
- **A — Audience**: Who will read this? (age, background, relationship to writer)
- **C — Content**: What 3–4 key points will I include?
- **T — Tone**: Formal / semi-formal / informal? Serious / humorous / empathetic?

#### Argumentative Essay Structure

```
Introduction (50–80 words):
• Hook (rhetorical question, surprising statistic, or anecdote)
• Background context
• Clear thesis statement

Body Paragraph 1 (80–100 words):
• Topic sentence (main argument)
• Explanation / elaboration
• Specific example or evidence
• Link back to thesis

Body Paragraph 2 (80–100 words):
• Topic sentence (second argument or counter-argument)
• Explanation / elaboration
• Specific example or evidence
• Link back to thesis

Body Paragraph 3 / Counter-argument (60–80 words):
• Acknowledge opposing view: "Admittedly, some argue that..."
• Refute with stronger evidence: "However, this overlooks..."

Conclusion (50–70 words):
• Restate thesis (in different words)
• Summarise key arguments
• End with a wider implication or call to action
```

#### Common Weaknesses to Avoid

| Weakness | Fix |
|---|---|
| Vague examples ("Many people think…") | Use specific real-world examples: names, places, statistics |
| Repetitive vocabulary | Use synonyms; vary sentence openers |
| Run-on sentences / comma splices | Use full stops more often; join clauses with appropriate conjunctions |
| Paragraphs that lack a topic sentence | Every paragraph must begin with a clear controlling idea |
| Generic conclusion that just repeats the introduction | Add a forward-looking statement or implication in the conclusion |

**Time allocation (Paper 2, 1 hr 30 min):**
- Part A (short task): 25–30 minutes
- Part B (extended writing): 50–55 minutes (including 10 min planning)
- Review: 5–8 minutes

## 4. Paper 3: Listening & Integrated Skills

Paper 3 is divided into two parts: Listening Comprehension (Part A) and Integrated Skills (Part B).

### 4.1 Part A: Listening Comprehension

Part A plays **3–4 recordings** covering different contexts: announcements, discussions, talks, interviews, telephone conversations, or radio programmes.

#### Before the Recording Plays

- Use the reading time to preview questions carefully.
- Predict what kind of information you need: numbers, names, opinions, reasons, sequences.
- Mark keywords in each question to guide your listening focus.

#### During the Recording

- Write brief notes — do NOT try to write full sentences.
- Use symbols: ↑ = increase, ↓ = decrease, ✓ = agree, ✗ = disagree, → = leads to.
- Pay attention to **signpost language**: "Firstly…", "On the other hand…", "What I mean is…", "In contrast to…"
- Notice **tone shifts**: a speaker moving from description to opinion, or from endorsement to criticism.

#### Question Types and Tactics

| Question Type | What to Listen For | Common Trap |
|---|---|---|
| True / False / Not Given | Exact wording vs paraphrase | "Not Given" ≠ False — if not mentioned, choose NG |
| Multiple Choice | The overall message of a section | Distractors often use words from the recording but with wrong meaning |
| Short Answer / Gap Fill | Specific facts (names, times, figures) | Keep answers concise; use the speaker's exact words if possible |
| Note Completion | Structured information | Match the format — if a noun is needed, don't write a verb phrase |
| Extended Response | Summary of speaker's viewpoint | Must paraphrase, not just quote; show you understood the reasoning |

### 4.2 Part B: Integrated Skills

Part B provides **written source materials** (3–4 texts totalling 800–1,200 words) and requires candidates to produce a written output (e.g., email, memo, proposal, report, presentation notes) that **integrates information from the texts**.

#### Integrated Skills Step-by-Step

**Step 1 — Read the task brief carefully (3 min)**
- Who are you? (role / identity)
- Who is your audience?
- What form must the output take?
- What is the core purpose?

**Step 2 — Map the source materials (10–12 min)**
- Read each source text and label key information: 📌 Fact / 💡 Opinion / ⚠️ Problem / ✅ Solution
- Create a quick "information matrix" — which sources address which aspects of the task

**Step 3 — Plan your output (5 min)**
- Decide which information to include, exclude, and combine
- Plan paragraph order (logical sequence, not the order of sources)

**Step 4 — Write (30–35 min)**
- Maintain the appropriate format and register throughout
- Integrate information from **at least 2–3 different sources** per main point
- Paraphrase actively: do not lift entire sentences
- Use connectors to show relationships: "According to Source A, … This is supported by Source C, which shows…"

**Step 5 — Proofread (5 min)**
- Check subject-verb agreement, article usage (a/an/the), tense consistency
- Ensure format elements are complete (subject line, greeting, signature if applicable)

#### Key Language for Integrated Writing

```
Reporting information:
"According to [source], …"
"[Source] highlights that …"
"As indicated in [source], …"

Comparing / contrasting:
"While Source A suggests…, Source B argues…"
"Both sources agree that…, although they differ on…"

Drawing conclusions:
"This suggests that…"
"It can therefore be concluded that…"
"These findings indicate that…"

Making recommendations:
"Based on the above, it is recommended that…"
"In light of the evidence, [organisation] should consider…"
```

**Time allocation (Paper 3, ~2 hrs):**
- Part A (listening): ~50–60 minutes
- Part B (integrated skills): ~55–65 minutes
- Review: 5 minutes

## 8. Exam-Day Strategies

### Paper 1
- **Read questions first**: For every text, skim the questions before reading — this transforms passive reading into targeted scanning.
- **Use the text for evidence**: Every inference or language question answer should include a brief quote. Unsupported answers rarely achieve full marks.
- **Don't leave blanks**: For MC questions, there is no negative marking — always choose the best available option.

### Paper 2
- **Spend 10 minutes planning**: A clear plan prevents structural collapse mid-essay. List 3 main points before writing a single sentence.
- **First sentence matters**: Examiners read the opening with extra attention — start with a hook, not "In today's society…" (overused, generic).
- **Proofread last 5 minutes**: Check: tense consistency, subject-verb agreement, word choice. Even one paragraph of careful proofreading can lift a mark.

### Paper 3
- **Manage your pace in integrated skills**: If you spend too long reading sources, you won't have enough time to write a complete, coherent output. Aim to finish reading within 15 minutes.
- **Format = free marks**: In integrated writing, correct format (email subject line, greeting, sign-off) is easy to secure — never skip it.
- **Answer the brief, not just the sources**: Some candidates write a detailed summary of the sources but forget to fulfil the actual task. Re-read the task brief halfway through writing.

## Quick Reference: Key Connectors and Transitional Phrases

```
ADDING INFORMATION:
Furthermore, … / Moreover, … / In addition to this, …
Not only … but also …

CONTRASTING:
However, … / Nevertheless, … / On the other hand, …
Despite this, … / Although … / While …

GIVING EXAMPLES:
For instance, … / For example, … / To illustrate this, …
This is evident in … / A case in point is …

SHOWING CAUSE AND EFFECT:
As a result, … / Consequently, … / Therefore, …
This leads to … / This explains why …

CONCLUDING:
In conclusion, … / To sum up, … / Ultimately, …
It is clear that … / All things considered, …

CONCEDING / COUNTER-ARGUING:
Admittedly, … / It is true that …, however, …
While some may argue that …, the evidence suggests …
```

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: How much should I quote from the text in Paper 1?**
A: Keep quotes short (3–8 words is usually enough). The marker wants to see your interpretation, not a copy of the passage. Quote just enough to anchor your point, then explain.

**Q2: Is British or American spelling preferred?**
A: Both are accepted, but you must be **consistent** throughout. Mixing "colour" and "color" in the same paper looks careless.

**Q3: How formal should Paper 2 essays be?**
A: Unless the task specifies informal writing (e.g., a personal blog post or letter to a friend), default to **semi-formal to formal** register. Avoid contractions (I've, can't) in formal essays.

**Q4: Can I use bullet points in the extended writing (Part B)?**
A: Only if the task explicitly requires a list format (e.g., report or proposal). For essays, speeches, and narratives, use full paragraphs.

**Q5: What is the typical word count for Paper 2 Part B?**
A: 400–500 words is the typical target. Significantly under (below 300) suggests underdevelopment; significantly over (above 600) can reflect poor time management or lack of focus.

**Q6: How is Paper 3 Integrated Skills marked — is it like a summary?**
A: It is more than a summary. Markers look for **synthesis** (combining ideas from multiple sources into a coherent argument or plan) rather than just paraphrasing each source in sequence. A good integrated response makes connections between sources.
